Unit 8 · Section 1
Biot-Savart Law
The B-field equivalent of Coulomb's Law — integrating current elements
🧲 The Big Picture

Just as $q \to \vec{E} \to \vec{F}_E$, we have $i \to \vec{B} \to \vec{F}_B$.

The Biot-Savart Law tells you the infinitesimal $d\vec{B}$ contributed by a tiny current element $i\,d\vec{s}$ at a field point P:

$$d\vec{B}_P = \frac{\mu_0 i}{4\pi}\frac{d\vec{s}\times\hat{r}}{r^2}$$

Variables

  • $d\vec{s}$: vector along wire in direction of $i$
  • $\hat{r}$: unit vector pointing from $d\vec{s}$ toward P
  • $r$: distance from $d\vec{s}$ to P
  • $\mu_0 = 4\pi\times10^{-7}$ T·m/A

Direction via RHR

$d\vec{s}\times\hat{r}$ gives the direction of $d\vec{B}$. Use RHR on the cross product: fingers along $d\vec{s}$, curl toward $\hat{r}$, thumb → $d\vec{B}$.

$\vec{B} = \displaystyle\int d\vec{B}$ — superposition holds.

📏 Finite Wire → Infinite Wire

For a straight wire of length $L$, at perpendicular distance $R$ from a point P, integrating Biot-Savart gives:

$$B = \frac{\mu_0 i}{2\pi R}\cdot\frac{L/2}{\sqrt{(L/2)^2 + R^2}} \quad \text{(finite wire, exact)}$$

Derivation trick: substitute $s = R\tan\alpha$, so $ds = R\,d\alpha/\cos^2\alpha$ and $(R^2+s^2)^{3/2} = R^3/\cos^3\alpha$. The integral becomes $\int\cos\alpha\,d\alpha = \sin\alpha$.

Infinite wire limit: $L \to \infty$ (i.e. $L/2 \gg R$)

$$B = \frac{\mu_0 i}{2\pi R}$$

Direction: circles around the wire per RHR. Into the page on the right for $i$ pointing right.

Forces on Current-Carrying Wires
Parallel wires, the Newton's 3rd law pair, and the rail gun
⚡ Force on a Wire in a B-Field

A wire of length $L$ carrying current $i$ in a field $\vec{B}$:

$$\vec{F} = i\vec{L}\times\vec{B}$$

$\vec{L}$ is the vector of length $L$ pointing in the direction of current flow.

🔗 Two Parallel Wires

Wire $a$ creates a B-field at wire $b$. If $d \ll L$ (wires behave as infinite):

$$B_b = \frac{\mu_0 i_a}{2\pi d}$$

This field exerts a force on wire $b$:

$$F_{ab} = i_b L B_b = \frac{\mu_0 i_a i_b L}{2\pi d}$$

Parallel currents → Attract

RHR: wire $a$ creates $\vec{B}$ downward at $b$ (for $i_a$ rightward). $i_b\vec{L}\times\vec{B}$ points toward $a$.

Antiparallel currents → Repel

$\vec{F}_{ba} = -\vec{F}_{ab}$ — Newton's 3rd Law. Magnitude same, direction opposite.

💥 Rail Gun (Real Application)

Two parallel conducting rails carry current $i$. A conducting fuse/projectile bridges them (length $d$). The rail currents create a $\vec{B}$ field pointing downward at the fuse. Then:

$$\vec{F}_B = i\,d\,(\hat{i}\times\vec{B}) \quad\text{— accelerates projectile out}$$

The fuse is Joule-heated until it becomes plasma (high conductivity). $id\vec{B}$ then launches the projectile at extreme velocity. This is a direct use of $\vec{F} = i\vec{L}\times\vec{B}$.

Ampère's Law
The B-field analog of Gauss's Law — exploit symmetry to avoid Biot-Savart
🔄 The Law

For any closed loop $C$:

$$\oint_C \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{s} = \mu_0\, i_\text{enc}$$

$i_\text{enc}$ = net current passing through any surface bounded by $C$. We only evaluate $\vec{B}$ on the loop we chose.

Finding $i_\text{enc}$ — sign convention (RHR)

  • Pick a direction of integration around $C$ (show with arrows)
  • Run your 4 fingers of the right hand along the integration path
  • Your thumb points in the direction of positive current through $C$
  • Currents in the opposite direction count as negative
  • Currents not passing through $C$ contribute zero
🗺️ Strategy for Ampère's Law
  • 1Figure out the direction of $\vec{B}$ from the current configuration using RHR
  • 2Pick a closed loop $C$ that is either parallel to $\vec{B}$ (so $\vec{B}\cdot d\vec{s} = B\,ds$), perpendicular (contributes 0), or where $B=0$
  • 3Calculate $i_\text{enc}$ using RHR to get signs right
  • 4Do the integral $\oint_C$ — usually just $B \times (\text{path length})$
📐 Example: Long Straight Wire

$\vec{B}$ forms closed circles around the wire (RHR). Choose a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$:

$\vec{B} \parallel d\vec{s}$ everywhere on the circle, and $B$ is constant → $\oint \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{s} = B \cdot 2\pi r$

$$B \cdot 2\pi r = \mu_0 i \implies B(r) = \frac{\mu_0 i}{2\pi r}$$

Same result as Biot-Savart for infinite wire — but derived in two lines instead of a full integral.

For two wires both inside loop: $i_\text{enc} = i_1 - i_2$ (if they go in opposite directions).

🔬 Generalized Ampère's Law

When current is distributed (not a thin wire), $i_\text{enc}$ is found by integrating the current density $\vec{j}$:

$$\oint_C \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{s} = \mu_0 \int_A \vec{j}\cdot\hat{n}\,dA$$

$A$ = cross-sectional area enclosed by loop $C$; $\hat{n}$ is defined by the same RHR.

📊 Uniform Current Density — Wire of Radius $R$

Total current $i$, uniform density $j = i/\pi R^2$.

Inside: $r \leq R$

$i_\text{enc} = j\cdot\pi r^2 = i\cdot(r/R)^2$

$$B(r) = \frac{\mu_0 i}{2\pi R^2}\,r$$

Grows linearly with $r$

Outside: $r > R$

$i_\text{enc} = i$ (all current inside)

$$B(r) = \frac{\mu_0 i}{2\pi r}$$

Falls off as $1/r$

Key: at $r = R$ both expressions agree — $B$ is continuous

$$B(R) = \frac{\mu_0 i}{2\pi R}$$

Solenoids & Toroids
Using Ampère's law on highly symmetric geometries
🌀 Solenoid

A tightly wound coil (like a slinky) with $n$ turns per meter, current $i$. In the ideal limit (tight, long windings): $B$ is uniform inside, $B \approx 0$ outside.

Choose a rectangular Amperian loop with leg 3 inside (length $L$) and leg 1 outside:

  • Leg 3 (inside): $\vec{B} \parallel d\vec{s}$ → contributes $BL$
  • Legs 2, 4: $\vec{B} \perp d\vec{s}$ → contribute 0
  • Leg 1 (outside): $B \approx 0$ → contributes 0
$$BL = \mu_0\,n\,i\,L \implies \boxed{B = \mu_0 n i} \quad\text{(ideal solenoid)}$$

$n$ = turns/meter. Note $B$ is independent of position inside — truly uniform.

🍩 Toroid

A solenoid bent into a closed donut. $N$ = total number of turns. $\vec{B}$ is circular and confined inside the donut.

Choose a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$ (inside toroid, $R_i < r < R_o$). Each of the $N$ turns penetrates the loop, so $i_\text{enc} = Ni$:

$$B\cdot 2\pi r = \mu_0 N i \implies B(r) = \frac{\mu_0 N i}{2\pi r}$$

vs. Solenoid

Solenoid: $B = \mu_0 n i$ — uniform inside

Toroid: $B(r) = \mu_0 Ni/2\pi r$ — varies with $r$

Note: solenoid $n$ = turns/m; toroid $N$ = total turns

B limits in toroid

$B_\text{max} = \mu_0 N i / 2\pi R_i$ (inner radius)

$B_\text{min} = \mu_0 N i / 2\pi R_o$ (outer radius)

$B = 0$ outside the donut entirely

Current Loop as a Magnetic Dipole
On-axis B-field, far-field limit, torque in external fields
🎯 B on the Axis of a Current Loop

A circular loop of radius $R$, current $i$, centered at the origin in the $xy$-plane. The B-field along the $z$-axis:

$$\vec{B}(z) = \frac{\mu_0 i R^2}{2(R^2+z^2)^{3/2}}\,\hat{z}$$

At the center ($z=0$): $B = \mu_0 i / 2R$. For $N$ loops: multiply by $N$.

Far field: $z \gg R$

$$\vec{B}(z) \approx \frac{\mu_0}{2\pi}\frac{\vec{\mu}}{z^3} \quad\text{where}\quad \vec{\mu} = Ni\pi R^2\,\hat{z} = NiA\,\hat{z}$$

$\vec{\mu}$ is the magnetic dipole moment. Same $1/r^3$ fall-off as electric dipole, same field shape along the axis.

🔃 Torque & Potential Energy in External $\vec{B}$

A current loop with dipole moment $\vec{\mu}$ in an external field $\vec{B}_\text{ext}$:

Torque

$$\vec{\tau} = \vec{\mu}\times\vec{B}_\text{ext}$$

Coil rotates so $\vec{\mu}$ aligns with $\vec{B}_\text{ext}$ (stable equilibrium at $\theta=0$)

Potential Energy

$$U = -\vec{\mu}\cdot\vec{B}_\text{ext} = -\mu B\cos\theta$$

Minimum at $\theta=0$ (aligned), maximum at $\theta=\pi$

The current loop looks exactly like a bar magnet when viewed from far away along the axis — $\vec{\mu}$ points from S-pole to N-pole (inside the magnet convention).
