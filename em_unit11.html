Radiation from an Oscillating Dipole
Time-varying sources · LC oscillator antenna · ω⁴ power law · polarized emission
⚡ From Static to Time-Varying Sources

Everything we have done so far has involved static or steady-state distributions — charges that sit still, or currents that flow steadily. For those cases, a static electric dipole's E-field falls off as $1/r^3$, and so does a magnetic dipole's B-field. More complicated distributions fall off even faster: $\sim r^{-n}$. None of these fields "escape" to infinity in a meaningful way.

But when charges or currents vary in time, something fundamentally new happens. The field at a distant point $P$ cannot respond instantaneously — it must depend on where the source charge was at an earlier time, specifically the time it took light to travel from the source to $P$. This "retarded" response is what allows energy to propagate outward as radiation:

$$\vec{E}_P = \sum_i \vec{E}\!\left(\vec{r}_i,\; t - \frac{|\vec{r}_i|}{c}\right)$$

The field at $P$ is set by where each charge was at the retarded time $t - r_i/c$ — not where it is now. The delay $r/c$ is the light-travel time. This retardation is the physical origin of radiation: it allows fields to "detach" from their sources and travel outward indefinitely.

📡 The LC Oscillator as a Dipole Antenna

Recall from electrostatics: an electric dipole is a pair of equal and opposite charges $+q$ and $-q$ separated by a distance $d$. The dipole moment $\vec{p} = qd\,\hat{n}$ points from the negative to the positive charge and has magnitude $p = qd$. It tells you both the strength and orientation of the charge separation. A static dipole just sits there. But if the charges oscillate back and forth, the dipole moment oscillates too — and a time-varying dipole moment is what generates radiation.

The simplest way to create this is with an LC circuit driving an antenna. Recall that $L$ is the inductance of a coil (its "magnetic inertia," measured in Henrys) and $C$ is the capacitance of the capacitor (how much charge it stores per volt, in Farads). In an LC circuit the energy sloshes back and forth between the capacitor's E-field and the inductor's B-field at the resonance frequency $\omega = 1/\sqrt{LC}$. Connect this to a straight-wire antenna and the sloshing charge creates an oscillating dipole moment — a radio transmitter.

$$\vec{p}(t) = p_0\cos(\omega t)\,\hat{y}, \qquad \omega = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$$

$p_0 = qd$ is the dipole amplitude: $q$ is the charge magnitude and $d$ is the effective antenna length. The oscillation frequency $\omega$ is set entirely by the LC values — this is how a radio transmitter is tuned to a specific frequency. Higher $\omega$ → dramatically more power radiated (see below).

The radiated power is found by computing how $\vec{p}(t)$ accelerates the charges. Let $U$ denote the total energy carried away by the wave per unit time (i.e., the radiated power $dU/dt$). The result from Maxwell's equations is striking:

$$\frac{dU}{dt} \propto \left(\frac{d^2\vec{p}}{dt^2}\right)^{\!2} \propto \omega^4$$

Why ω⁴? Since $\vec{p} = p_0\cos\omega t$, the second derivative is $\ddot{\vec{p}} = -p_0\omega^2\cos\omega t$. So $(\ddot{\vec{p}})^2 \propto \omega^4$. Doubling the frequency multiplies the radiated power by 16. This is also why blue light ($\omega$ large) is scattered much more strongly than red light ($\omega$ small) — Rayleigh scattering, and why the sky is blue.

🌊 Polarized Radiation

The radiation from a dipole antenna is polarized: the E-field has a definite, fixed orientation in space (parallel to the dipole axis). This is in contrast to unpolarized light from, say, a heated filament, where independent atoms radiate with randomly oriented dipoles.

For radiation propagating perpendicular to the dipole axis (the direction of maximum emission), with the antenna along $\hat{y}$:

$$\vec{E} \parallel \hat{y},\quad \vec{B} \perp \vec{E},\quad \text{propagation direction} = \frac{\vec{E}\times\vec{B}}{|\vec{E}\times\vec{B}|}$$

E and B are always perpendicular to each other and both perpendicular to the propagation direction. The wave travels in the direction of $\vec{E}\times\vec{B}$ — this cross product will reappear as the Poynting vector. No radiation is emitted along the antenna axis ($\hat{y}$) itself.

Traveling Waves
$f(x - vt)$ formalism · wavenumber $k$ · angular frequency $\omega$ · dispersion relation $\omega = kv$
→ Making Any Function Travel

To describe an EM wave mathematically we need a function that moves through space. The key insight is deceptively simple: given any snapshot $f(x)$, you can make it travel to the right at speed $v$ by replacing every $x$ with $(x - vt)$. The function's shape is frozen; it just shifts rightward by $vt$ every second.

$$f(x)\;\xrightarrow{\text{travels right at }v}\;f(x - vt)$$
$$f(x)\;\xrightarrow{\text{travels left at }v}\;f(x + vt)$$

Why it works: The peak of $f(x-vt)$ occurs where $x - vt = \text{const}$, i.e. $x = vt + \text{const}$. As $t$ increases, the peak position increases at rate $v$ — it moves right. The minus sign is the key: $+vt$ in the argument means leftward travel, $-vt$ means rightward.

〜 Periodic (Sinusoidal) Waves

For EM radiation, the source oscillates sinusoidally, so the spatial snapshot is $E(x) = E_0\sin(2\pi x/\lambda)$ — a sine wave with spatial period $\lambda$ (the wavelength). Applying the traveling-wave substitution gives the full space-time wave field. This introduces a compact set of parameters that every physicist should have memorized cold:

ParameterSymbol & definitionUnitsPlain meaning
Wavelength$\lambda$mDistance between adjacent peaks
Wavenumber$k = 2\pi/\lambda$rad/mSpatial oscillations per radian-length
Frequency$\nu$ (or $f$)HzFull oscillations per second
Angular frequency$\omega = 2\pi\nu$rad/sRadians of phase per second
Period$T = 1/\nu$sTime for one full oscillation
Wave speed$v = \lambda\nu = \omega/k$m/sSpeed of phase propagation
$$E(x,t) = E_0\sin(kx - \omega t) \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \omega = kv \;\text{ (dispersion relation)}$$

Reading the wave: $E_0$ = amplitude. $kx$ measures how many radians of phase you are through spatially — it completes a full cycle ($2\pi$) every $\lambda = 2\pi/k$ meters. $-\omega t$ measures phase accumulated in time — the minus sign means the pattern moves right at $v = \omega/k$. Standing at $x_0$, you undergo one full oscillation in time $T = 2\pi/\omega = 1/\nu$.

The equation $\omega = kv$ linking frequency and wavenumber is called the dispersion relation — the word "dispersion" comes from the fact that in some media, different frequencies travel at different speeds (causing light to "disperse" into colors, as in a prism). In vacuum, all frequencies travel at the same $c$, so the dispersion relation is simply $\omega = kc$. It is not just a definition — it is a constraint that any valid EM wave must satisfy. A mathematical "solution" that violates it is not a physical traveling wave.

🔗 Worked Example: FM Radio

FM radio station broadcasts at $\nu = 100\,\text{MHz} = 10^8\,\text{Hz}$. Let's find all the wave parameters for this EM wave in air ($v \approx c$).

$$\omega = 2\pi\nu = 2\pi\times10^8 \approx 6.28\times10^8\,\text{rad/s}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{c}{\nu} = \frac{3\times10^8}{10^8} = 3.0\,\text{m} \qquad k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} = \frac{2\pi}{3} \approx 2.09\,\text{rad/m}$$
$$T = \frac{1}{\nu} = 10^{-8}\,\text{s} = 10\,\text{ns}$$

A 3-meter wavelength explains why FM antennas (often quarter-wave) are about 75 cm long. AM radio at 1 MHz has $\lambda = 300\,\text{m}$ — requiring much larger antennas or tower arrays.

The Wave Equation from Maxwell's Laws
Faraday + Ampère–Maxwell → $\partial^2 E/\partial x^2 = c^{-2}\,\partial^2 E/\partial t^2$ · solution $E_m\sin(kx-\omega t)$ · $B_m = E_m/c$
∂² Deriving the Wave Equation

Recall two fundamental constants from earlier in the course: $\varepsilon_0 \approx 8.85\times10^{-12}\,\text{C}^2/(\text{N·m}^2)$ is the permittivity of free space — it appeared in Coulomb's law and Gauss's law, setting the strength of electric forces in vacuum. And $\mu_0 = 4\pi\times10^{-7}\,\text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space — it appeared in Ampère's Law, setting the strength of magnetic forces. These two constants look unrelated. Maxwell's triumph was showing they are not: their product gives the speed of light, $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0} \approx 3\times10^8\,\text{m/s}$.

One more ingredient to recall: the Ampère–Maxwell law. Recall Ampère's Law: $\oint\vec{B}\cdot d\vec{\ell} = \mu_0 I_\text{enc}$. Maxwell added a crucial term — the displacement current $\varepsilon_0\,d\Phi_E/dt$ — because a changing electric flux (even with no real current) also creates a circulating B-field. The full law is $\oint\vec{B}\cdot d\vec{\ell} = \mu_0(I_\text{enc} + \varepsilon_0\,d\Phi_E/dt)$. Without this addition, the math below would not close, and EM waves could not exist.

To derive the wave equation, consider a wave propagating in the $x$-direction with E pointing along $\hat{z}$ and B pointing along $\hat{y}$. Apply Faraday's law and the Ampère–Maxwell law to infinitesimal rectangular loops — the same loop-integral technique from Faraday's Law derivations:

Path 1 — Faraday's Law ($xz$-plane loop, height $h$, width $dx$):

$$\oint\vec{E}\cdot d\vec{\ell} = h[E(x+dx)-E(x)] = h\frac{\partial E}{\partial x}dx = -\frac{\partial B}{\partial t}h\,dx$$
$$\Longrightarrow \quad \frac{\partial E}{\partial x} = -\frac{\partial B}{\partial t}$$

Path 2 — Ampère–Maxwell Law ($xy$-plane loop, height $L$, width $dx$):

$$\oint\vec{B}\cdot d\vec{\ell} = L[B(x)-B(x+dx)] = -L\frac{\partial B}{\partial x}dx = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{\partial E}{\partial t}L\,dx$$
$$\Longrightarrow \quad -\frac{\partial B}{\partial x} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{\partial E}{\partial t}$$

Differentiate the first equation with respect to $x$, the second with respect to $t$, then substitute to eliminate $B$:

$$\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial x^2} = -\frac{\partial^2 B}{\partial x\,\partial t} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial t^2}$$
$$\boxed{\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial x^2} - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial t^2} = 0} \qquad c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}} \approx 3\times10^8\,\text{m/s}$$

This is the classical wave equation for 1D EM radiation. The same equation holds for $B$. The speed $c$ emerges purely from the electrostatic constant $\varepsilon_0$ and the magnetic constant $\mu_0$ — these were measured in static lab experiments, yet their product gives the speed of light.

✓ Verifying the Solution

We can verify $E = E_m\sin(kx - \omega t)$ is a solution by direct substitution. Taking the two derivatives and plugging into the wave equation:

$$\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial x^2} = -k^2 E_m\sin(kx-\omega t), \qquad \frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial t^2} = -\omega^2 E_m\sin(kx-\omega t)$$
$$\Rightarrow \; -k^2 E_m\sin + \frac{\omega^2}{c^2}E_m\sin = 0 \; \Rightarrow \; k^2 = \frac{\omega^2}{c^2} \; \Rightarrow \; \boxed{\omega = kc}\;\checkmark$$

The dispersion relation $\omega = kc$ is confirmed. Using Path 1 ($\partial E/\partial x = -\partial B/\partial t$) with the sinusoidal solution:

$$kE_m\cos(kx-\omega t) = \omega B_m\cos(kx-\omega t) \;\Rightarrow\; B_m = \frac{k}{\omega}E_m = \frac{E_m}{c}$$

$B_m = E_m/c$: in any plane EM wave, the B-field amplitude is always $1/c \approx 3\times10^{-9}$ times the E-field amplitude. Though tiny in SI units, B carries exactly as much energy as E (they share it equally — proven in the Poynting section).

📐 The Plane Wave

The solution we derived is a plane wave: the fields $E$ and $B$ depend only on $x$ and $t$, not on $y$ or $z$. This means the amplitude is completely uniform across any plane perpendicular to the propagation direction ($y$–$z$ plane). It's an idealization, but an extraordinarily useful one — real waves from distant sources look exactly like this locally.

$$\vec{E}(x,t) = E_m\sin(kx-\omega t)\,\hat{y},\quad \vec{B}(x,t) = B_m\sin(kx-\omega t)\,\hat{z}$$

Wave propagates in $+\hat{x}$, confirmed by $\hat{y}\times\hat{z} = \hat{x}$. Both fields oscillate with the same phase $(kx-\omega t)$ — E and B peak and zero simultaneously. $\vec{E}\perp\vec{B}$ and both $\perp$ to propagation: EM waves are transverse.

🔵 Huygens's Principle and Plane Waves

A few new terms to define before going further. A wavefront is a surface on which all points of a wave have the same phase — for example, a surface where the E-field is simultaneously at its peak. The wavefront is always perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. A spherical wave is what you get from a point source (like a single dipole antenna or a tiny light bulb): the wavefronts are expanding spheres centered on the source, with intensity falling off as $1/r^2$ as the energy spreads over an ever-larger surface.

Far from the source, the spheres have grown so large that any small patch of a wavefront looks essentially flat — a plane wave. Huygens's principle gives a precise geometric rule for how wavefronts evolve: every point on a wavefront acts as a new point source of tiny spherical "wavelets," and the surface that is tangent to all those wavelets (their envelope) is the next wavefront. It is a way of predicting where the wave goes without solving Maxwell's equations from scratch.

Spherical → Plane Wave (far field):

Spherical wavefront radius grows as $r = ct$. At position $x_0$ far from the source, the curvature $\sim 1/r \to 0$ as $r\to\infty$. The amplitude across the $y$-direction becomes essentially constant. We write $E(y) = \text{const}$ for all $y$ at fixed $x_0$ — the plane wave approximation.

$$r\to\infty: \quad E(y,x_0,t) \approx \text{const in }y \quad\Longrightarrow\quad\text{plane wave}$$

The Poynting Vector
Energy transport · $\vec{S} = \vec{E}\times\vec{B}/\mu_0$ · $\langle S\rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E_m^2 c$ · intensity = power per area
☀️ EM Waves Carry Energy

An EM wave transports energy from source to receiver — this is how the sun heats the Earth, how antennas communicate, how laser beams cut metal. The quantity that describes this energy flow is the Poynting vector $\vec{S}$. Before defining it, define intensity: the power delivered per unit area of wavefront, in units of W/m². A laser beam has high intensity; candlelight at arm's length has low intensity. The Poynting vector $\vec{S}$ is the vectorized version of intensity: its magnitude is the instantaneous power per unit area at that point, and its direction is the direction of energy flow.

$$\vec{S} \equiv \frac{1}{\mu_0}\vec{E}\times\vec{B} \qquad [\vec{S}] = \text{W/m}^2$$

Reading $\vec{S}$: The direction of $\vec{S}$ is the direction of energy flow (same as $\vec{E}\times\vec{B}$, same as the wave propagation direction). The magnitude $|\vec{S}|$ is the power per unit area crossing a surface perpendicular to the wave — i.e., the intensity. $\mu_0$ appears because the cross product needs to be divided by a constant to get the right units and magnitude.

📐 Simplifying S Using $B = E/c$

For a plane EM wave, we can simplify $|\vec{S}|$ using two things we already know: (1) $B = E/c$ from the wave equation derivation, and (2) a useful algebraic identity that follows directly from $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}$. Squaring both sides: $c^2 = 1/(\mu_0\varepsilon_0)$, so $\mu_0 = 1/(c^2\varepsilon_0)$, and therefore $1/\mu_0 = c^2\varepsilon_0$. This is not a new fact — it is just a rearrangement of the speed-of-light relation:

$$|\vec{S}| = \frac{1}{\mu_0}EB = \frac{1}{\mu_0}\cdot E\cdot\frac{E}{c} = \frac{E^2}{\mu_0 c} = c\varepsilon_0 E^2$$

Using $1/\mu_0 = c^2\varepsilon_0$: $S = (c^2\varepsilon_0/c)E^2 = c\varepsilon_0 E^2$. Both E and B store equal energy densities ($u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2 = u_B = B^2/2\mu_0$ when $B=E/c$), and $S$ = (total energy density) × $c$.

Since $E = E_m\sin(kx-\omega t)$, $S$ oscillates. The measurable quantity is the time-averaged intensity $\langle S\rangle$, found using $\langle\sin^2\rangle = \frac{1}{2}$:

$$\langle S\rangle = c\varepsilon_0\langle E^2\rangle = c\varepsilon_0\cdot\frac{E_m^2}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E_m^2 c$$

$\langle S\rangle$ is what power meters and photodetectors actually measure. The $\frac{1}{2}$ comes from averaging $\sin^2$ over a full cycle. Doubling $E_m$ quadruples $\langle S\rangle$ — intensity scales as amplitude squared, just like in acoustics and optics.

Quick derivation from energy in a cube:

Consider a thin slab (area $A$, thickness $c\,dt$). In time $dt$, a wave traveling at $c$ sweeps this entire volume past the right face. The energy inside is $dU = u_\text{tot}\cdot A\,c\,dt$ where $u_\text{tot} = \varepsilon_0 E^2$ (both E and B contribute equally). So $S = (1/A)(dU/dt) = c\varepsilon_0 E^2$. ✓

Polarization
Polarized vs unpolarized light · wire-grid polarizer · Malus's law $S_T = S_0\cos^2\theta$ · unpolarized → $\tfrac{1}{2}S_0$
〰️ Polarized vs Unpolarized Radiation

A dipole antenna radiates polarized light: the E-field always points in the same direction (along the antenna axis). But most natural light sources — the sun, incandescent bulbs, candles — are unpolarized: they are composed of many independent atoms, each radiating as a dipole with a randomly oriented dipole moment. The result is an ensemble of waves with all possible E-field orientations in the plane perpendicular to propagation.

Polarized wave (e.g., from a single dipole antenna along $\hat{y}$):

$\vec{E} = E_m\sin(kx-\omega t)\,\hat{y}$ — definite, fixed orientation. Any angle $\theta$ for $\vec{E}$ in the $yz$-plane is acceptable as long as $\vec{E}\times\vec{B}$ points in the propagation direction.

Unpolarized wave (e.g., sunlight):

$\vec{E}$ has all possible orientations in the $yz$-plane with equal probability, each with its companion $\vec{B}$ at right angles. The E-field "spins" randomly rather than oscillating in a fixed plane.

||| How a Wire-Grid Polarizer Works

A metal wire grid with spacing $d \leq \lambda$ acts as a polarizer. When an EM wave hits the grid, the component of $\vec{E}$ parallel to the wires drives free electrons up and down the wires, inducing an oscillating current. This current re-radiates a field that exactly cancels the incoming parallel component — so it is absorbed/reflected. The component of $\vec{E}$ perpendicular to the wires cannot drive any current (electrons can't move across gaps), so it passes through unimpeded.

$$\vec{E}_\parallel\;\text{(to wires)} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{absorbed/reflected} \qquad \vec{E}_\perp\;\text{(to wires)} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{transmitted}$$

Output E-field = projection of input $\vec{E}$ onto the direction perpendicular to the wires. If input E makes angle $\theta$ with the wire-perpendicular direction: $E_\text{trans} = E_m\cos\theta$.

📐 Malus's Law

Since intensity scales as $E^2$ (from the Poynting vector), and the transmitted amplitude is $E_m\cos\theta$, the transmitted intensity for a polarized input beam is:

$$S_T = S_0\cos^2\theta \qquad \text{(Malus's Law — polarized input)}$$

$\theta$ is the angle between the incident E-field direction and the axis perpendicular to the polarizer wires (the "transmission axis"). Check: $\theta=0$ → full transmission ($S_T = S_0$); $\theta = 90°$ → zero transmission (crossed polarizers); $\theta = 45°$ → half transmission.

For unpolarized radiation, all angles $\theta$ are equally likely, so we average $\cos^2\theta$ over a full circle:

$$\langle S_T\rangle = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}S_0\cos^2\theta\,d\theta = \frac{S_0}{2}$$

50% of unpolarized radiation passes through a polarizer, regardless of the polarizer's orientation. The output is now polarized (perpendicular to the wires). Passing through a second polarizer at angle $\phi$ then gives $S = \frac{S_0}{2}\cos^2\phi$.
