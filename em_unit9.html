Unit 9 · Section 1
Faraday's Law & Lenz's Law
Changing magnetic flux drives an EMF — and Nature always pushes back
⚡ The Big Idea

Everything up to now has been about static fields — charges producing E-fields, steady currents producing B-fields. Faraday's Law shatters that picture. A changing magnetic flux through a circuit is enough to drive a current, with no battery required. This is the operating principle behind every power generator, every transformer, and every wireless charger on the planet.

The key quantity is the magnetic flux $\Phi_B$ through a surface bounded by the circuit. Think of it as counting how many B-field lines pass through the loop — not just the number, but weighted by the cosine of the angle they make with the surface's normal.

Faraday's Law

$$\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} \qquad \Phi_B = \int \vec{B}\cdot\hat{n}\,dA$$

Reading the equation: $\mathcal{E}$ is the induced EMF (volts). $\Phi_B$ is the magnetic flux through any surface bounded by the circuit. The minus sign is not cosmetic — it encodes Lenz's Law (see below). If $\vec{B}$ is uniform and the loop has area $A$ at angle $\theta$ to $\vec{B}$:

$$\Phi_B = BA\cos\theta$$

$\Phi_B$ changes whenever $B$, $A$, or $\theta$ changes with time. All three routes can induce an EMF.

For a coil of $N$ turns, each turn of wire is threaded by the same flux $\Phi_B$. We say the field lines are linked to each turn — they loop through it. The flux linkage is the total flux counted once per turn it passes through: $N\Phi_B$. Because all $N$ turns are wired in series, Faraday's Law sums over all of them:

$$\mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -\frac{d(N\Phi_B)}{dt}$$

Doubling the turns doubles the EMF for the same changing flux — each turn contributes independently.

🔄 Lenz's Law

The minus sign in Faraday's Law is the mathematical statement of Lenz's Law: the induced current flows in the direction that opposes the change in flux that caused it. This is Nature's version of inertia — the system resists change.

There are two equivalent ways to think about why this must be true:

View 1: Magnetic Dipole

The induced current creates its own magnetic dipole moment. When a magnet (N-pole first) approaches a loop, the loop's induced current makes it look like another N-pole facing the incoming magnet — repelling it, opposing the approach.

Pull the magnet away, and the loop flips to S-pole, attracting and opposing the removal.

View 2: Flux Cancellation

The induced current creates a B-field through the loop. This induced B-field points opposite to the change in external flux — it tries to keep $\Phi_B$ constant.

Increasing external flux → induced B opposes it (subtracts). Decreasing flux → induced B aids it (adds). Always fighting the change.

Quick Rule for Current Direction

  • Find the direction of $d\Phi_B/dt$ — is flux through the loop increasing or decreasing, and in which direction?
  • The induced current must create a B-field that opposes the change
  • Use the right-hand rule on that opposing B to find which way current flows in the loop
📐 Worked Example: Magnet into Loop

A bar magnet with its N-pole pointing down is pushed downward into a horizontal conducting loop. The B-field points downward through the loop (let's call that the $-\hat{z}$ direction). As the magnet approaches, $|\Phi_B|$ increases in the downward direction.

  • 1$\Phi_B$ is increasing in the $-\hat{z}$ direction (downward through the loop)
  • 2By Lenz's Law, the induced B must oppose this — it must point upward ($+\hat{z}$) through the loop
  • 3By RHR on that upward B: curl fingers in the direction of current, thumb up → current flows counterclockwise when viewed from above
  • 4This CCW current makes the loop look like an upward-pointing magnet (N-pole up), which repels the incoming N-pole of the bar magnet ✓

Motional EMF
A loop moving through a B-field — flux change, force analysis, and power
🏃 When Does Motion Induce an EMF?

Faraday's Law says $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi_B/dt$. Motion by itself isn't enough — what matters is whether the flux through the circuit is changing. A loop moving entirely within a uniform B-field sees constant flux, so $\mathcal{E} = 0$ even though the loop is moving. But a loop that is entering or exiting a field region has changing flux, and this drives a real current.

The canonical setup: a rectangular loop of width $\ell$ (perpendicular to motion) and resistance $R$ is pulled to the right out of a uniform field $\vec{B}$ (into the page) at constant velocity $v$. If $y$ is the length of loop still inside the field:

$$\Phi_B = B\ell y \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -B\ell\frac{dy}{dt} = B\ell v$$

The minus sign (Lenz) tells us current direction; the magnitude is $|\mathcal{E}| = B\ell v$. This is the motional EMF.

Induced current

$$i = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R} = \frac{B\ell v}{R}$$

Direction by Lenz: flux (into page) is decreasing as loop exits, so induced current creates flux into the page → CW when viewed from front.

Concept check

Loop fully inside a uniform field, moving at speed $v$: $y$ is constant, $d\Phi_B/dt = 0$, $\mathcal{E} = 0$, $i = 0$. No force required to maintain motion.

⚖️ Force Analysis & Energy Conservation

The induced current flows through a conductor sitting in a B-field, so there must be forces. Let's track them carefully — the result is a beautiful confirmation of energy conservation.

The right-hand segment (still inside the field, length $\ell$) carries current $i$ upward (CW loop). The force on it from the field is:

$$\vec{F}_1 = i\vec{\ell}\times\vec{B} = i\ell B \;\hat{y}\times(-\hat{z}) = -i\ell B\,\hat{x} \quad\text{(leftward — opposing motion)}$$

The top and bottom segments have equal and opposite forces that cancel ($\vec{F}_2 + \vec{F}_3 = 0$). So the only net magnetic force on the loop is $F_1$ pointing left — opposing the hand's pull. This is Lenz's Law in mechanical form.

The hand must apply $F_h = i\ell B$ rightward to maintain constant $v$. The power the hand delivers:

$$P_\text{hand} = F_h v = i\ell B v = \frac{B\ell v}{R}\cdot\ell B v = \frac{B^2\ell^2 v^2}{R}$$
$$P_R = i^2 R = \left(\frac{B\ell v}{R}\right)^2 R = \frac{B^2\ell^2 v^2}{R} \quad \Rightarrow \quad P_\text{hand} = P_R \;\checkmark$$

Mechanical energy in = electrical energy dissipated as Joule heat. Perfect conversion.

Induced E-Field & Maxwell's Equations
A changing B-field creates an E-field even without a circuit
🌀 E-Field Without a Wire

Here's something profound: Faraday's Law doesn't require a physical circuit. Experiment shows that whenever $d\vec{B}/dt \ne 0$, an electric field is induced in space — even in vacuum, even with no charges or wires present. The EMF we've been calculating is really just the line integral of this induced E-field around a closed loop:

$$\mathcal{E} = \oint_C \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{\ell} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \vec{B}\cdot\hat{n}\,dA$$

This is the integral form of Faraday's Law — one of Maxwell's four equations. The induced E-field is not like the electrostatic E-field: it forms closed loops in space and does not begin or end on charges. There is no potential $V$ associated with it (the path integral depends on which path you take).

📏 E(r) Inside and Outside the B Region

Consider a circular region of radius $R$ containing a uniform, time-varying B-field (say $\vec{B}$ points into the page and $dB/dt > 0$). By symmetry, the induced E-field must circle the region. We choose a circular Amperian-style loop of radius $r$ and apply Faraday's Law:

Inside ($r < R$)

$$\oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{\ell} = E\cdot 2\pi r = -\pi r^2\frac{dB}{dt}$$
$$\boxed{E(r) = -\frac{r}{2}\frac{dB}{dt}} \quad (r < R)$$

E grows linearly with $r$, like the B-field inside a solenoid (the spatial analog).

Outside ($r > R$)

$$E\cdot 2\pi r = -\pi R^2\frac{dB}{dt}$$
$$\boxed{E(r) = -\frac{R^2}{2r}\frac{dB}{dt}} \quad (r > R)$$

E falls off as $1/r$ — exactly like the B-field outside a long wire (symmetry in the math is no coincidence).

The minus sign and direction

With B into the page and $dB/dt > 0$: $E$ is negative, meaning it circulates clockwise when viewed from the front. This drives a clockwise current — which creates B out of the page inside the loop — opposing the increasing B into the page. Lenz's Law confirmed.

⚙️ The AC Electric Generator

A generator turns mechanical rotation into electrical current using Faraday's Law. To see how, we build it up in three steps: the coil geometry, what spinning does to the flux, and then the EMF.

Step 1 — The coil and its area

The coil is a flat rectangle with sides $a$ and $b$, so its face area is $A = ab$. It has $N$ turns of wire wound around that rectangle. The key quantity is the normal vector $\hat{n}$: an arrow pointing straight out of the coil's face, perpendicular to it.

Face-on view
Side view — tilted at angle θ

Step 2 — What spinning does to the flux

Recall that magnetic flux through a flat loop is $\Phi_B = BA\cos\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{B}$ and the normal $\hat{n}$. When the coil is face-on to $\vec{B}$ ($\theta = 0$), flux is maximum: $\Phi_B = Bab$. When it's edge-on ($\theta = 90°$), flux is zero.

Now spin the coil at a steady rotational frequency $\nu$ (revolutions per second). The angle advances uniformly: $\theta = 2\pi\nu t$, since $\nu$ full turns per second means $2\pi\nu$ radians per second. So:

$$\Phi_B(t) = Bab\cos(2\pi\nu t)$$

The flux oscillates between $+Bab$ (face-on) and $-Bab$ (face-on the other way), passing through zero each time the coil is edge-on to $\vec{B}$.

Step 3 — Apply Faraday's Law

For $N$ turns the total flux linkage is $N\Phi_B$. Differentiate and negate:

$$\mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = NBab\cdot 2\pi\nu\sin(2\pi\nu t) = \mathcal{E}_0\sin(2\pi\nu t)$$
$$\mathcal{E}_0 = NBab\cdot 2\pi\nu \quad \text{(peak EMF)}$$

The $\sin$ comes from differentiating $\cos$ — EMF is maximum precisely when the flux is changing fastest (coil edge-on to $\vec{B}$), and zero when the coil is face-on (flux momentarily at its peak, not changing). $\mathcal{E}_0$ grows with every factor you can engineer: more turns $N$, stronger field $B$, larger coil $ab$, faster spin $\nu$.

Why it's called AC

The $\sin$ function swings positive and negative — the EMF (and therefore the current) reverses direction every half-turn. That is alternating current. The brushes are sliding contacts that connect the spinning coil to the stationary external circuit while accommodating this reversal.

🌊 Eddy Currents

When a solid conductor (like a copper sheet) moves through a magnetic field, Faraday's Law applies to every possible loop within the bulk of the conductor — not just the outer boundary. The result is a continuous distribution of circulating currents called eddy currents. They are an unavoidable consequence of induction in extended conductors.

Consider a copper sheet being pulled out of a B-field region (field into the page). As it exits, the flux through loops inside the sheet decreases. By Lenz's Law:

Effect 1 — Magnetic braking force

The eddy current loops form a CW circulation (to add flux back). The portion of the current inside the field region carries $i\vec{\ell}\times\vec{B}$, which points leftward — opposing the rightward pull. This is eddy-current braking, used in maglev trains and roller-coaster brakes.

Effect 2 — Joule heating

The eddy currents dissipate energy as $i^2R$ heat in the resistive metal. This is exploited in induction cooktops (the pot is the conductor) and undesirable in transformer cores (which is why cores are laminated to break up eddy current paths).

🛡️ Electromagnetic Shielding

A conducting sheet of thickness $s$ and conductivity $\sigma$ can shield a region from time-varying magnetic fields. Here is how: the changing external $B$ induces an E-field in the sheet (Faraday), which drives a current density $j = \sigma E$, which creates its own opposing B-field inside the sheet. At a point P on the axis:

  • 1At radius $r$ in the sheet: $E = \tfrac{r}{2}\,dB/dt$ (induced E, from Faraday)
  • 2Current density: $j = \sigma E = \tfrac{\sigma r}{2}\,dB/dt$
  • 3A thin annular ring of width $dr$, thickness $s$ carries $di = j\cdot s\,dr = \tfrac{\sigma s}{2}r\,dB/dt\,dr$
  • 4Each ring's B at center: $dB_\text{shield} = \tfrac{\mu_0\,di}{2r} = \tfrac{\mu_0\sigma s}{4}\,dB/dt\,dr$

Integrating from 0 to R:

$$B_0 = \int_0^R dB_\text{shield} = \frac{\mu_0\sigma s R}{4}\frac{dB}{dt}$$

$B_0$ is the shielding field that opposes the external $B$ at point P. When $dB/dt$ is very large (high-frequency signal), $B_0$ becomes large enough that $B(P) \approx 0$. This is why metal enclosures (Faraday cages) block high-frequency EM interference. At DC ($dB/dt = 0$), there is no shielding.

🔃 Self-Inductance — A Coil That Fights Its Own Current

Recall from Faraday's Law: a changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an EMF. Up until now we've been thinking about that flux coming from an external source — a magnet moving nearby or an external field turning on. But a coil carrying current creates its own B-field, and therefore its own flux through itself. So: if the current in the coil changes, the flux changes, and Faraday's Law says there must be an induced EMF — in the very same coil that's causing it. This is self-induction.

That induced EMF always opposes the change (Lenz's Law). So if you try to increase the current, the coil pushes back. If you try to decrease it, the coil tries to keep it going. It behaves like magnetic inertia — resisting any change in current, just as mass resists any change in velocity.

Defining inductance $L$

Recall that flux linkage $N\Phi_B$ was the total flux threading all $N$ turns of a coil. For a given coil geometry, if you double the current $i$, you double the B-field everywhere, which doubles $\Phi_B$. So $N\Phi_B$ is always directly proportional to $i$. We name that proportionality constant $L$, the inductance:

$$N\Phi_B = L\,i \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \mathcal{E}_\text{back} = -\frac{d(N\Phi_B)}{dt} = -L\frac{di}{dt}$$

Reading this: $L$ (in Henrys, H) measures how much flux the coil links per amp of current. The back-EMF is $-L\,di/dt$ — the minus sign (Lenz) means it always opposes the rate of change of current. Large $L$ → strong opposition to change. The current $i$ plays the role that charge $q$ played in capacitors.

Analogy: inductor ↔ capacitor

For a capacitor: $q = CV$ (charge proportional to voltage), $C$ is the geometry constant. For an inductor: $N\Phi_B = Li$ (flux proportional to current), $L$ is the geometry constant. Both $C$ and $L$ depend only on shape and size — not on the voltage or current itself.

📐 Calculating $L$ for Two Geometries

To find $L$ for any geometry: assume a current $i$ flows, use what you know to find the B-field it creates, compute the flux linkage $N\Phi_B$, then divide by $i$. The $i$ cancels and you're left with a purely geometric result.

Solenoid

A solenoid is a tightly wound helix of wire — essentially a long cylinder of $N$ turns. Recall from Ampère's Law: the B-field inside a solenoid with $n$ turns per meter carrying current $i$ is uniform and equal to $B = \mu_0 n i$ (pointing along the axis). Outside the solenoid, $B \approx 0$.

With total turns $N = n\ell$ (turn density × length), cross-section area $A$, flux per turn $= BA = \mu_0 n i A$:

$$N\Phi_B = (n\ell)(\mu_0 n i A) = \mu_0 n^2 \ell A\, i \quad\Rightarrow\quad \boxed{L_\text{solenoid} = \mu_0 n^2 \ell A}$$

$L$ grows with the square of turn density $n^2$ — doubling turns per meter quadruples inductance. It also scales with volume $\ell A$: bigger solenoids store more flux.

Toroid

A toroid is a donut-shaped (ring) core with $N$ turns of wire wound uniformly around it. Think of bending a solenoid into a circle so the two ends meet. The B-field is confined entirely inside the donut, and by Ampère's Law it varies with radial distance $r$ from the center of the donut: $B(r) = \mu_0 N i / (2\pi r)$. The inner radius is $a$, outer radius $b$, and height $h$.

To find total flux: integrate over the cross-section from $r=a$ to $r=b$ (B isn't uniform here — it's stronger near the inner edge):

$$\Phi_B = \int_a^b \frac{\mu_0 N i}{2\pi r}\,h\,dr = \frac{\mu_0 N i h}{2\pi}\ln\frac{b}{a} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \boxed{L_\text{toroid} = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 h}{2\pi}\ln\frac{b}{a}}$$
📈 The LR Circuit — Building Up Current

Now put an inductor $L$, a resistor $R$, and a battery $V$ together in a single loop and close the switch at $t = 0$. What happens?

Immediately, the battery tries to push current around the loop. But the inductor resists — its back-EMF $\mathcal{E}_L = -L\,di/dt$ opposes the rising current. So current starts at zero and climbs gradually. As it climbs, the inductor's opposition weakens (because $di/dt$ gets smaller). Eventually current stops changing altogether and the inductor's back-EMF drops to zero — it becomes just a wire. At that steady state, all the battery voltage is across the resistor, so $i_\infty = V/R$.

To find exactly how $i$ grows, apply Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL): the sum of all voltage drops around a closed loop equals the source voltage. Going around: the battery provides $+V$; the resistor drops $iR$; the inductor drops $L\,di/dt$. They must balance:

$$\underbrace{V}_{\text{battery}} = \underbrace{iR}_{\text{resistor drop}} + \underbrace{L\frac{di}{dt}}_{\text{inductor back-EMF}}$$

At $t=0$: $i=0$ so $iR=0$, meaning the full battery voltage lands across the inductor. The inductor gets hit with $V$ volts and $di/dt = V/L$ — current starts rising as fast as the inductance allows. As $i$ grows, $iR$ claims more of $V$, leaving less for the inductor, so $di/dt$ slows down.

The solution to this equation is an exponential approach to the steady-state value:

$$i(t) = \frac{V}{R}\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau_L}\right)$$
$$\tau_L = \frac{L}{R} \quad \text{(inductive time constant, units: seconds)}$$

Note on notation: $\tau$ (Greek letter tau) is reused across physics. In mechanics it means torque. Here it means time constant — a completely different quantity. The subscript $L$ specifies it's the inductive time constant. RC circuits use $\tau_C = RC$ for the same idea.

What "time constant" means: A time constant is the characteristic timescale of an exponential process — it tells you how fast the curve is rising or falling. Specifically, after one time constant $\tau_L$ has elapsed, the quantity has covered $1 - e^{-1} \approx 63\%$ of the distance to its final value. After $2\tau_L$ it has covered $\approx 86\%$, after $5\tau_L$ it is within $1\%$ of the final value and effectively done. The bigger $\tau_L$ is, the slower the approach — a sluggish circuit. A small $\tau_L$ means fast response. It shows up as the number in the exponent: $e^{-t/\tau_L}$ — when $t = \tau_L$, the exponent is $-1$, giving $e^{-1} \approx 0.37$, meaning $37\%$ remains to go.

Reading $\tau_L = L/R$: Large $L$ means stronger magnetic inertia → slower rise. Large $R$ means the resistor burns off energy faster → faster approach to steady state. At $t = \tau_L$, the current has reached $63\%$ of its final value $V/R$. After $\sim 5\tau_L$ it's essentially at $V/R$.

📉 The LR Circuit — Current Dying Away

Now suppose the circuit has been connected long enough that the current has settled to its steady-state value $i_0 = V/R$ — the inductor is just acting like a wire. You disconnect the battery (open the switch). The current can't stop instantly — the inductor now acts as the source, trying to maintain what it had. Energy stored in the inductor's B-field drives the current through the resistor until it's all dissipated as heat.

With no battery in the loop, KVL gives: the inductor's voltage must equal the resistor's drop. Setting the sum to zero:

$$L\frac{di}{dt} + iR = 0$$

This says: the rate of decrease of current is proportional to the current itself — classic exponential decay.

$$i(t) = \frac{V}{R}\,e^{-t/\tau_L}, \qquad \tau_L = \frac{L}{R}$$

Starts at $i_0 = V/R$ (whatever it was at steady state), decays with the same time constant $\tau_L = L/R$. The inductor is not a source of new energy — it's releasing the energy that was stored in its B-field.

The inertia analogy in full

Current $i$ ↔ velocity $v$. Inductance $L$ ↔ mass $m$. Resistance $R$ ↔ friction/drag. Battery $V$ ↔ applied force. Just as a massive object pushed by a force accelerates slowly and coasts when the force is removed, an inductor in a circuit causes current to rise slowly and then sustain itself after the battery is gone. The time constant $\tau_L = L/R$ is like mass/drag — how long it takes friction to kill the motion.

🔋 Energy Stored in an Inductor

Just as a capacitor stores energy in its electric field ($U_E = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{q^2}{2C}$), an inductor stores energy in its magnetic field. To find the stored energy, track the power delivered to the inductor during the charging process.

Multiply both sides of the LR charging equation by $i$:

$$Vi = i^2 R + Li\frac{di}{dt}$$

Power from battery = Joule heat in $R$ + power delivered to the inductor. Integrating the last term:

$$\int L i\,\frac{di}{dt}\,dt = \int Li\,di = \frac{1}{2}Li^2$$
$$U_B = \frac{1}{2}Li^2 \qquad \text{(energy stored in inductor's B-field)}$$

Compare: $U_E = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$. For the inductor, current $i$ plays the role of voltage $V$; $L$ plays the role of $C$.

When the circuit is opened (disconnected from the battery), this stored energy $\frac{1}{2}Li^2$ must go somewhere — it is all dumped into the resistor as Joule heat. This is why opening an inductive circuit abruptly can cause a large voltage spike: $V_L = L\,di/dt$ becomes huge if $di/dt$ is large.

🧲 Magnetic Energy Density

The energy in the inductor is stored in the B-field that permeates the space inside the coil. For a solenoid (the cleanest geometry), we can find how much energy is stored per unit volume — the magnetic energy density.

Solenoid: $L = \mu_0 n^2 \ell A$, volume $= \ell A$, and $B = \mu_0 ni$:

$$U_B = \frac{1}{2}Li^2 = \frac{1}{2}\mu_0 n^2 \ell A\, i^2$$
$$u_B \equiv \frac{U_B}{\ell A} = \frac{1}{2}\mu_0 n^2 i^2 = \frac{(\mu_0 ni)^2}{2\mu_0} = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}$$
$$\boxed{u_B = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}} \qquad \longleftrightarrow \qquad u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$$

This result is general — it holds everywhere in space, not just inside solenoids. The total magnetic energy in any region is $U_B = \int \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}\,dV$.

🔌 Coaxial Cable — L from Energy

A coaxial cable has an inner conductor (radius $a$) carrying current $i$ and an outer conductor (radius $b$) returning it. By Ampère's Law, B exists only between the conductors: $B(r) = \mu_0 i/(2\pi r)$ for $a \leq r \leq b$. We can find $L$ by computing the stored energy directly.

$$dV = 2\pi r\,dr\,\ell \quad \Rightarrow \quad U_B = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int_a^b B^2\,2\pi r\,dr\,\ell = \frac{\mu_0 i^2 \ell}{4\pi}\ln\frac{b}{a}$$

Setting $U_B = \frac{1}{2}Li^2$ and solving:

$$L = \frac{\mu_0\,\ell}{2\pi}\ln\frac{b}{a}$$

The energy method avoids having to integrate $N\Phi_B/i$ — often much faster for geometries where B is known but flux linkage is hard to count directly.
