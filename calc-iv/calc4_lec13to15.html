Surface Integrals · Stokes' Theorem · The Divergence Theorem
RECALL Parametric surfaces from L12 — the machinery we need — click to expand

In L12, a parametric surface was a map $\vec{r}(u,v) = \langle x(u,v), y(u,v), z(u,v)\rangle$ from a parameter domain $D\subset\mathbb{R}^2$ to a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The key object was the cross product of tangent vectors:

The area element — from L12
$$\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v \quad\text{is normal to the surface at each point, and}\quad dS = |\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v|\,dA$$ For a graph $z = g(x,y)$: $\vec{r}(x,y) = \langle x,y,g\rangle$, so $\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y = \langle -g_x,-g_y,1\rangle$ and $dS = \sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}\,dA$.

L13 takes this further: instead of just measuring area ($\iint_S dS$), we now integrate functions and vector fields over surfaces.

The Big Idea
Two types of surface integrals
Scalar surface integral — integrating a function over a surface
You have a surface $S$ and a function $f(x,y,z)$ (think: temperature, density). The scalar surface integral $\iint_S f\,dS$ adds up the values of $f$ weighted by tiny area patches. It generalizes a double integral the same way a line integral generalizes a single integral.
Vector surface integral (flux) — integrating a vector field through a surface
You have a vector field $\vec{F}$ (think: fluid velocity, electric field) and an oriented surface $S$ with a chosen normal direction. The flux integral $\iint_S \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$ measures how much of $\vec{F}$ passes through $S$ in the normal direction — positive if it flows with the normal, negative if against.

These two integrals are related but distinct. The scalar one doesn't need orientation; the vector one does. The formulas below show why.

Scalar Surface Integrals
The formula
Scalar surface integral — Stewart §16.7
If $S$ is parametrized by $\vec{r}(u,v)$ over domain $D$: $$\iint_S f(x,y,z)\,dS = \iint_D f(\vec{r}(u,v))\,|\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v|\,dA$$ The factor $|\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v|$ converts flat area in parameter space to actual surface area — same idea as $|\vec{\gamma}'(t)|$ converting parameter length to arc length in line integrals.
Special case: graph $z = g(x,y)$
$$\iint_S f\,dS = \iint_D f(x,y,g(x,y))\sqrt{1 + g_x^2 + g_y^2}\,dA$$ The region $D$ is the projection of $S$ onto the $xy$-plane.

The key skill: parametrize the surface (or recognize it as a graph), compute $|\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v|$, substitute, and integrate over the parameter domain $D$. Everything reduces to a standard double integral.

Vector Surface Integrals — Flux
Orientation and the flux formula

A surface $S$ is oriented by choosing a continuous unit normal $\hat{n}$ at each point. For a closed surface (sphere, box, etc.) we typically choose the outward normal. For an open surface like a paraboloid, the problem specifies "upward" or "downward."

Flux integral — definition and formula
$$\iint_S \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \iint_S \vec{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = \iint_D \vec{F}(\vec{r}(u,v))\cdot(\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v)\,dA$$ Note: $d\vec{S} = (\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v)\,dA$ is a vector area element — it encodes both the size of the patch and its normal direction. The orientation of $\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v$ (which way it points) is determined by the parametrization order.
Special case: upward-oriented graph $z = g(x,y)$
For $\vec{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$ and $S$ oriented upward ($\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y = \langle -g_x,-g_y,1\rangle$ points up): $$\iint_S \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \iint_D \bigl(-P\,g_x - Q\,g_y + R\bigr)\,dA$$ Flip the sign for downward orientation (swap $\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y$ to $\vec{r}_y\times\vec{r}_x$).
Orientation warning
If you parametrize and get the cross product pointing the wrong way, just negate the integral. The formula $\iint_D \vec{F}\cdot(\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v)\,dA$ gives $+$ flux if $\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v$ agrees with the chosen normal, $-$ if it's opposite.
Quick Check — True or False
T/F

The scalar surface integral $\iint_S f\,dS$ depends on the orientation of $S$.
FALSE

Orientation only matters for the flux (vector) surface integral $\iint_S \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$. The scalar integral uses $dS = |\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v|\,dA$, which is always positive — it's a magnitude. Flipping the parametrization changes the sign of $\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v$ but not its magnitude, so the scalar integral is unchanged.

T/F

If $\vec{F}$ is everywhere tangent to a surface $S$, then $\iint_S \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = 0$.
TRUE

The flux integral measures the component of $\vec{F}$ in the normal direction: $\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \vec{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS$. If $\vec{F}$ is tangent to $S$ everywhere, then $\vec{F}\perp\hat{n}$ everywhere, so $\vec{F}\cdot\hat{n} = 0$ pointwise, and the integral is zero. Physically: no fluid is flowing through the surface, only along it.

T/F

For a graph $z = g(x,y)$ oriented upward, $d\vec{S} = \langle g_x, g_y, -1\rangle\,dA$.
FALSE

The upward orientation gives $d\vec{S} = \langle -g_x, -g_y, 1\rangle\,dA$. This comes from $\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y$ where $\vec{r}(x,y)=\langle x,y,g\rangle$: $\vec{r}_x = \langle 1,0,g_x\rangle$ and $\vec{r}_y=\langle 0,1,g_y\rangle$, so $\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y = \langle -g_x,-g_y,1\rangle$, which has a positive $z$-component (points upward) ✓. The version given, $\langle g_x, g_y, -1\rangle$, is the downward orientation.

Worked Example — Scalar Then Flux
Worked Example — Scalar integral over a cylinder, then flux through a paraboloid

Part A (Scalar). Evaluate $\iint_S z\,dS$ where $S$ is the lateral surface of the cylinder $x^2+y^2=4$ between $z=0$ and $z=3$.

  • 1Parametrize. $\vec{r}(\theta,z)=\langle 2\cos\theta, 2\sin\theta, z\rangle$, $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$, $z\in[0,3]$.
  • 2Cross product magnitude. $\vec{r}_\theta = \langle -2\sin\theta,2\cos\theta,0\rangle$, $\vec{r}_z = \langle 0,0,1\rangle$. So $\vec{r}_\theta\times\vec{r}_z = \langle 2\cos\theta,2\sin\theta,0\rangle$ and $|\vec{r}_\theta\times\vec{r}_z|=2$.
  • 3Integrate. $\iint_S z\,dS = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^3 z\cdot 2\,dz\,d\theta = 2\cdot 2\pi\cdot\frac{9}{2} = \boxed{18\pi}$.

Part B (Flux). Evaluate $\iint_S \langle x,y,z\rangle\cdot d\vec{S}$ over the paraboloid $z=1-x^2-y^2$ above $z=0$, oriented upward.

  • 1Graph formula. $g(x,y)=1-x^2-y^2$, $g_x=-2x$, $g_y=-2y$. Upward: $d\vec{S}=\langle 2x,2y,1\rangle\,dA$.
  • 2Dot product. $\langle x,y,z\rangle\cdot\langle 2x,2y,1\rangle = 2x^2+2y^2+z = 2(x^2+y^2)+(1-x^2-y^2)=1+x^2+y^2$.
  • 3Polar over $D: x^2+y^2\leq 1$. $\iint_D(1+r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^1(r+r^3)\,dr = 2\pi\bigl[\frac{r^2}{2}+\frac{r^4}{4}\bigr]_0^1 = 2\pi\cdot\frac{3}{4}=\boxed{\dfrac{3\pi}{2}}$.

E13.1

Evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_S (x+y)\,dS$ where $S$ is the part of the plane $x+y+z=1$ that lies in the first octant.
Solution

Write as a graph. $z = 1-x-y$, so $g_x = -1$, $g_y = -1$. The area element is $dS = \sqrt{1+1+1}\,dA = \sqrt{3}\,dA$. The projection $D$ is the triangle $\{x\geq 0,\;y\geq 0,\;x+y\leq 1\}$.

Set up the integral. $$\iint_S(x+y)\,dS = \sqrt{3}\iint_D(x+y)\,dA = \sqrt{3}\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}(x+y)\,dy\,dx$$

Inner integral. $\int_0^{1-x}(x+y)\,dy = \bigl[xy+\frac{y^2}{2}\bigr]_0^{1-x} = x(1-x)+\frac{(1-x)^2}{2}$.

Outer integral. Expand: $x(1-x)+\frac{(1-x)^2}{2} = x-x^2+\frac{1-2x+x^2}{2} = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{x^2}{2}$. $$\sqrt{3}\int_0^1\Bigl(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{x^2}{2}\Bigr)\,dx = \sqrt{3}\Bigl[\frac{x}{2}-\frac{x^3}{6}\Bigr]_0^1 = \sqrt{3}\Bigl(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{6}\Bigr) = \sqrt{3}\cdot\frac{1}{3} = \boxed{\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}}$$

E13.2

Evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_S z^2\,dS$ where $S$ is the part of the cone $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ that lies between the planes $z=1$ and $z=3$.
Solution

Parametrize the cone. Use polar: $\vec{r}(r,\theta) = \langle r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, r\rangle$ with $r\in[1,3]$, $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$ (since $z=r$ on the cone).

Cross product. $\vec{r}_r = \langle\cos\theta,\sin\theta,1\rangle$, $\vec{r}_\theta = \langle -r\sin\theta,r\cos\theta,0\rangle$. $$\vec{r}_r\times\vec{r}_\theta = \langle -r\cos\theta,\;-r\sin\theta,\;r\rangle, \quad |\vec{r}_r\times\vec{r}_\theta| = r\sqrt{2}$$

Integrate. On the cone, $z=r$, so $z^2=r^2$: $$\iint_S z^2\,dS = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_1^3 r^2\cdot r\sqrt{2}\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\sqrt{2}\int_1^3 r^3\,dr = 2\pi\sqrt{2}\Bigl[\frac{r^4}{4}\Bigr]_1^3 = 2\pi\sqrt{2}\cdot\frac{80}{4} = \boxed{40\sqrt{2}\,\pi}$$

E13.3

Evaluate the flux $\displaystyle\iint_S \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$ where $\vec{F}(x,y,z)=\langle 0,0,z\rangle$ and $S$ is the paraboloid $z=4-x^2-y^2$ above $z=0$, oriented upward.
Solution

Graph formula. $g(x,y)=4-x^2-y^2$, $g_x=-2x$, $g_y=-2y$. Upward orientation: $d\vec{S}=\langle 2x,2y,1\rangle\,dA$.

Dot product. $\vec{F} = \langle 0,0,4-x^2-y^2\rangle$, so: $$\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = 0\cdot 2x + 0\cdot 2y + (4-x^2-y^2)\cdot 1 = 4-x^2-y^2 = 4-r^2$$

Domain. $D$ is the disk $x^2+y^2\leq 4$ (where $z\geq 0$). In polar: $$\iint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2(4-r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^2(4r-r^3)\,dr = 2\pi\Bigl[2r^2-\frac{r^4}{4}\Bigr]_0^2 = 2\pi(8-4) = \boxed{8\pi}$$

E13.4

Find the flux of $\vec{F}(x,y,z) = \langle x, y, z\rangle$ outward through the closed surface consisting of the cylinder $x^2+y^2=1$, $0\leq z\leq 1$, plus its top ($z=1$) and bottom ($z=0$) disks.
Solution — Three Pieces

Lateral surface ($x^2+y^2=1$, outward normal $\hat{n}=\langle\cos\theta,\sin\theta,0\rangle$): Parametrize: $\vec{r}(\theta,z)=\langle\cos\theta,\sin\theta,z\rangle$. $|\vec{r}_\theta\times\vec{r}_z|=1$ (outward direction confirmed). $\vec{F}\cdot(\vec{r}_\theta\times\vec{r}_z) = \langle\cos\theta,\sin\theta,z\rangle\cdot\langle\cos\theta,\sin\theta,0\rangle = \cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta = 1$. Flux$_\text{lat} = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 1\,dz\,d\theta = 2\pi$.

Top disk ($z=1$, upward normal $\hat{n}=\vec{k}$): $d\vec{S}=\langle 0,0,1\rangle\,dA$. $\vec{F}\cdot\hat{n} = z = 1$. Flux$_\text{top} = \iint_D 1\,dA = \pi$.

Bottom disk ($z=0$, downward normal $\hat{n}=-\vec{k}$): $\vec{F}\cdot\hat{n} = -z = 0$. Flux$_\text{bot} = 0$.

Total flux: $2\pi + \pi + 0 = \boxed{3\pi}$.

Note: div$\,\vec{F} = 1+1+1=3$. By the Divergence Theorem (coming in L15): $\iiint_E 3\,dV = 3\cdot\pi(1)^2(1) = 3\pi$ ✓ — a quick sanity check.

Stewart §16.7 — HW07 Problems
HW16.7.4

Suppose that $f(x,y,z) = g\!\left(\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}\right)$, where $g$ is a function of one variable such that $g(2) = -5$. Evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_S f(x,y,z)\,dS$, where $S$ is the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$.
Solution

Key observation. On the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$, every point satisfies $\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}=2$. Therefore $f(x,y,z)=g(2)=-5$ is constant on $S$ — the integrand doesn't depend on where you are on the sphere.

Factor out the constant. $$\iint_S f\,dS = \iint_S (-5)\,dS = -5\iint_S dS = -5\cdot A(S)$$

Surface area of the sphere. For $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$ (radius $a=2$): $A = 4\pi a^2 = 4\pi(4) = 16\pi$.

$$\iint_S f\,dS = -5\cdot 16\pi = \boxed{-80\pi}$$

The power of this problem: you never need to parametrize the sphere or compute any integral, because the function is constant on $S$. Always check whether the integrand simplifies on the surface before computing.

HW16.7.20

Evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_S (x^2+y^2+z^2)\,dS$, where $S$ is the part of the cylinder $x^2+y^2=9$ between the planes $z=0$ and $z=2$, together with its top and bottom disks.
Solution — Three Pieces

Lateral surface ($x^2+y^2=9$, $0\leq z\leq 2$): Parametrize $\vec{r}(\theta,z)=\langle 3\cos\theta,3\sin\theta,z\rangle$, $|\vec{r}_\theta\times\vec{r}_z|=3$. On this surface, $x^2+y^2=9$, so $x^2+y^2+z^2=9+z^2$: $$I_\text{lat}=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2(9+z^2)\cdot 3\,dz\,d\theta = 2\pi\cdot 3\int_0^2(9+z^2)\,dz = 6\pi\Bigl[9z+\frac{z^3}{3}\Bigr]_0^2 = 6\pi\!\left(18+\frac{8}{3}\right)=6\pi\cdot\frac{62}{3}=124\pi$$

Top disk ($z=2$, $x^2+y^2\leq 9$): $dS=dA$, and $x^2+y^2+z^2=r^2+4$ in polar: $$I_\text{top}=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^3(r^2+4)\,r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\Bigl[\frac{r^4}{4}+2r^2\Bigr]_0^3 = 2\pi\!\left(\frac{81}{4}+18\right)=2\pi\cdot\frac{153}{4}=\frac{153\pi}{2}$$

Bottom disk ($z=0$, $x^2+y^2\leq 9$): $x^2+y^2+z^2=r^2$: $$I_\text{bot}=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^3 r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\Bigl[\frac{r^4}{4}\Bigr]_0^3 = 2\pi\cdot\frac{81}{4}=\frac{81\pi}{2}$$

Total: $124\pi + \dfrac{153\pi}{2} + \dfrac{81\pi}{2} = 124\pi + \dfrac{234\pi}{2} = 124\pi + 117\pi = \boxed{241\pi}$

HW16.7.32

Evaluate the surface integral $\displaystyle\iint_S \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$ (i.e., find the flux) for $\vec{F}(x,y,z) = y\,\vec{i} + (z-y)\,\vec{j} + x\,\vec{k}$, where $S$ is the surface of the tetrahedron with vertices $(0,0,0)$, $(1,0,0)$, $(0,1,0)$, and $(0,0,1)$, with outward orientation.
Solution — Four Faces

The tetrahedron has four triangular faces. We compute the flux through each with outward normals.

Face 1 — $x=0$ plane: outward normal $-\vec{i}$; $\vec{F}\cdot(-\vec{i})=-y$. Region: $y\geq 0$, $z\geq 0$, $y+z\leq 1$. $$\int_0^1\!\int_0^{1-y}(-y)\,dz\,dy = \int_0^1(-y)(1-y)\,dy = -\int_0^1(y-y^2)\,dy = -\!\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{3}\right)=-\frac{1}{6}$$

Face 2 — $y=0$ plane: outward normal $-\vec{j}$; $\vec{F}\cdot(-\vec{j}) = -(z-y)\big|_{y=0}=-z$. Region: $x\geq 0$, $z\geq 0$, $x+z\leq 1$. $$\int_0^1\!\int_0^{1-x}(-z)\,dz\,dx = -\int_0^1\frac{(1-x)^2}{2}\,dx = -\frac{1}{6}$$

Face 3 — $z=0$ plane: outward normal $-\vec{k}$; $\vec{F}\cdot(-\vec{k}) = -x$. Region: $x\geq 0$, $y\geq 0$, $x+y\leq 1$. $$\int_0^1\!\int_0^{1-x}(-x)\,dy\,dx = -\int_0^1 x(1-x)\,dx = -\!\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{3}\right)=-\frac{1}{6}$$

Face 4 — $x+y+z=1$ plane: Parametrize as $\vec{r}(x,y)=\langle x,y,1-x-y\rangle$. Then $\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y = \langle 1,1,1\rangle$ (outward — points away from origin). $\vec{F}=\langle y,\;(1-x-y)-y,\;x\rangle=\langle y,1-x-2y,x\rangle$. $$\vec{F}\cdot\langle 1,1,1\rangle = y+(1-x-2y)+x = 1-y$$ Inner integral: $\int_0^{1-x}(1-y)\,dy = \bigl[y-y^2/2\bigr]_0^{1-x} = (1-x)-\tfrac{(1-x)^2}{2}$. Outer (let $u=1-x$): $$\iint_D(1-y)\,dA = \int_0^1\!\Bigl[(1-x)-\frac{(1-x)^2}{2}\Bigr]dx = \int_0^1\!\Bigl[u-\frac{u^2}{2}\Bigr]du = \frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{6}=\frac{1}{3}$$

Total flux: $-\dfrac{1}{6} - \dfrac{1}{6} - \dfrac{1}{6} + \dfrac{1}{3} = -\dfrac{1}{2} + \dfrac{1}{3} = \boxed{-\dfrac{1}{6}}$

RECALL Green's Theorem from L10 — Stokes' is the 3D upgrade — click to expand

Green's Theorem (L10) said: for a flat region $D$ in the $xy$-plane with boundary $C$, $$\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA$$ The right side is the $z$-component of $\text{curl}\,\vec{F}$ integrated over $D$, and $dA = \hat{k}\,dA = d\vec{S}$ for a flat surface in the $xy$-plane. So Green's theorem was always just a 2D special case of Stokes'.

Green's as a special case of Stokes'
Green's: $\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_D(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot\vec{k}\,dA$. Stokes': $\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_S(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S}$. The only difference: $S$ can now be any surface in 3D, not just a flat region.
The Big Idea
What Stokes' Theorem says
The core idea
You have an oriented surface $S$ in 3D with a boundary curve $C$. Stokes' Theorem says:

The line integral of $\vec{F}$ around $C$ = the flux of curl $\vec{F}$ through $S$.

The boundary curve captures the same information as the surface — but in different language. Just like Green's Theorem let you trade a loop integral for an area integral, Stokes' trades a 3D loop integral for a surface integral.
Stokes' Theorem — Stewart §16.8
Let $S$ be an oriented piecewise-smooth surface bounded by a simple closed curve $C$. If $\vec{F}$ has continuous partial derivatives on an open region containing $S$, then: $$\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_S (\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S}$$ The orientation of $C$ is determined by the orientation of $S$ via the right-hand rule.
Orientation — The Right-Hand Rule
How $C$ and $\hat{n}$ are linked
Right-hand rule for Stokes'
Point your right thumb in the direction of $\hat{n}$ (the chosen normal to $S$). Your fingers curl in the direction of the positive orientation of $C$. Equivalently: if you walk along $C$ with your head pointing in the $\hat{n}$ direction, the surface $S$ is on your left.
Common orientation mistake
If the problem says $C$ is oriented clockwise as viewed from a specific direction, determine which way the normal must point using the right-hand rule, and use that normal for $d\vec{S}$ in the surface integral. Getting this backwards flips the sign of the answer.

For a flat disk in the $xy$-plane: if $C$ is counterclockwise viewed from above, the normal is $+\vec{k}$ (upward). If $C$ is clockwise viewed from above, the normal is $-\vec{k}$ (downward).

Freedom to Choose Any Surface
The most powerful feature of Stokes'

Stokes' Theorem says the integral only depends on the boundary $C$, not on the specific surface $S$ that fills it in. This means:

Key strategy — replace a hard surface with an easy one
If the surface $S$ in $\iint_S\text{curl}\,\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$ is complicated, find any other surface $S'$ with the same boundary $C$ and same orientation, then compute $\iint_{S'}\text{curl}\,\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$ instead. The curved paraboloid becomes a flat disk. The twisted surface becomes a flat triangle. Same answer.
Key strategy — replace a hard line integral with a surface integral
If $\oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$ is hard to compute directly (awkward curve in 3D), find a convenient surface $S$ bounded by $C$, compute $\text{curl}\,\vec{F}$, and evaluate $\iint_S\text{curl}\,\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$ instead.

Deciding which direction to go depends on which is easier: the line integral or the surface integral.

Quick Check — True or False
T/F

If $\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \vec{0}$ everywhere on a surface $S$, then $\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = 0$ for any closed curve $C$ in that region.
TRUE (with a caveat)

By Stokes': $\oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_S(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S} = \iint_S\vec{0}\cdot d\vec{S} = 0$. The caveat: this requires that $C$ bounds a surface $S$ on which $\text{curl}\,\vec{F}=\vec{0}$, and the domain must be simply connected. For a simply connected domain, $\text{curl}\,\vec{F}=\vec{0}$ is equivalent to $\vec{F}$ being conservative, which implies zero circulation on all closed loops — exactly as expected.

T/F

Stokes' Theorem applies only to surfaces that are graphs $z = f(x,y)$.
FALSE

Stokes' Theorem holds for any piecewise-smooth oriented surface $S$ bounded by a simple closed curve $C$. The surface can be a hemisphere, a twisted patch, a cone, a piece of a cylinder — anything with a well-defined boundary and a consistent normal orientation. Restricting to graphs is just one convenient special case.

T/F

For the same boundary curve $C$, the value of $\iint_S\text{curl}\,\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$ can differ depending on which surface $S$ you choose to fill in $C$.
FALSE

By Stokes' Theorem, $\iint_S\text{curl}\,\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$ for any surface $S$ with boundary $C$ (with compatible orientation). The right side depends only on $C$, not on $S$. So all valid choices of $S$ give the same answer — this is the whole point of the theorem and the reason we can replace hard surfaces with easy ones.

Worked Example — Both Directions
Worked Example — Line integral converted to surface integral

Evaluate $\oint_C (-y^2)\,dx + x\,dy + z^2\,dz$ where $C$ is the circle $x^2+y^2=4$, $z=2$, counterclockwise viewed from above.

  • 1Identify $\vec{F}$. $\vec{F}=\langle -y^2, x, z^2\rangle$. Computing the line integral directly would require parametrizing $C$ and dealing with $z^2$ terms. Let's use Stokes' instead.
  • 2Choose surface $S$. The natural choice: the disk $D: x^2+y^2\leq 4$, $z=2$. Counterclockwise boundary from above $\Rightarrow$ normal $\hat{n}=+\vec{k}$, so $d\vec{S}=\langle 0,0,1\rangle\,dA$.
  • 3Compute curl. $$\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \left\langle R_y-Q_z,\; P_z-R_x,\; Q_x-P_y\right\rangle = \langle 0-0,\; 0-0,\; 1-(-2y)\rangle = \langle 0,\;0,\;1+2y\rangle$$
  • 4Flux of curl. $\text{curl}\,\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = (1+2y)\,dA$. By symmetry of the disk, $\iint_D 2y\,dA=0$. $$\iint_D(1+2y)\,dA = \iint_D 1\,dA = \pi(2)^2 = \boxed{4\pi}$$

E14.1

Use Stokes' Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_S (\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S}$ where $\vec{F}=\langle y^2,x,z^2\rangle$ and $S$ is the part of the paraboloid $z=1-x^2-y^2$ above $z=0$, oriented upward.
Solution — Surface integral → line integral

Boundary $C$. The paraboloid meets $z=0$ where $x^2+y^2=1$. So $C$ is the unit circle $x^2+y^2=1$, $z=0$. Upward orientation on $S$ $\Rightarrow$ $C$ is counterclockwise viewed from above. Parametrize: $\vec{r}(t) = \langle\cos t,\sin t,0\rangle$, $t\in[0,2\pi]$.

Line integral. $\vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) = \langle\sin^2 t,\cos t, 0\rangle$ and $\vec{r}'(t)=\langle-\sin t,\cos t,0\rangle$: $$\oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \int_0^{2\pi}\bigl(-\sin^3 t + \cos^2 t\bigr)\,dt$$

Evaluate. $\int_0^{2\pi}(-\sin^3 t)\,dt = 0$ (odd function over full period). $\int_0^{2\pi}\cos^2 t\,dt = \pi$. $$\iint_S(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S} = \boxed{\pi}$$

E14.2

Use Stokes' Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C y\,dx + z\,dy + x\,dz$ where $C$ is the triangle with vertices $(1,0,0)$, $(0,1,0)$, $(0,0,1)$, traversed counterclockwise as viewed from the origin.
Solution — Line integral → surface integral

Identify $\vec{F}$ and compute curl. $\vec{F}=\langle y,z,x\rangle$. $$\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \langle R_y-Q_z,\;P_z-R_x,\;Q_x-P_y\rangle = \langle 0-1,\;0-1,\;0-1\rangle = \langle -1,-1,-1\rangle$$

Choose surface $S$. The triangle itself: the plane $x+y+z=1$ in the first octant. Counterclockwise from the origin (looking outward) $\Rightarrow$ the compatible normal is $\hat{n}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\langle 1,1,1\rangle$ (pointing away from origin). With $z=1-x-y$: $\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y=\langle 1,1,1\rangle$, which points away from origin. ✓

Flux of curl. $$\iint_S(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S} = \iint_D\langle -1,-1,-1\rangle\cdot\langle 1,1,1\rangle\,dA = -3\iint_D dA = -3\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \boxed{-\frac{3}{2}}$$ (Area of the triangle $D: x+y\leq 1$, $x,y\geq 0$ in the $xy$-plane is $\frac{1}{2}$.)

E14.3

Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$ where $\vec{F}=\langle xz,\;yz,\;xy\rangle$ and $C$ is the boundary of the triangle with vertices $(1,0,0)$, $(0,2,0)$, $(0,0,3)$, counterclockwise when viewed from above.
Solution

Compute curl. $P=xz$, $Q=yz$, $R=xy$. $$\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \langle x-y,\;x-y,\;0\rangle$$ Check: $R_y-Q_z = x - y$; $P_z-R_x = x - y$; $Q_x-P_y = 0$. ✓

Choose surface $S$: the triangular plane. The three vertices lie on the plane $\frac{x}{1}+\frac{y}{2}+\frac{z}{3}=1$, i.e., $6x+3y+2z=6$. Normal $\vec{n}=\langle 6,3,2\rangle$, $|\vec{n}|=7$. Counterclockwise from above $\Rightarrow$ upward-pointing normal. The $z$-component of $\langle 6,3,2\rangle$ is positive, so this is the correct orientation. ✓

With $z = \frac{6-6x-3y}{2}$: $z_x=-3$, $z_y=-3/2$. So $\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y = \langle 3,\frac{3}{2},1\rangle$ (positive $z$ ✓) — wait, let me redo: $\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y = \langle -z_x,-z_y,1\rangle = \langle 3,\frac{3}{2},1\rangle$. $$\text{curl}\,\vec{F}\cdot(\vec{r}_x\times\vec{r}_y) = (x-y)\cdot 3 + (x-y)\cdot\frac{3}{2} + 0 = \frac{9}{2}(x-y)$$ $$\oint_C = \iint_D\frac{9}{2}(x-y)\,dA$$ where $D$ is the triangle in the $xy$-plane with vertices $(1,0)$, $(0,2)$, $(0,0)$. The boundary: $2x+y=2$.

By symmetry/odd: $\iint_D x\,dA - \iint_D y\,dA$. For this triangle, $\bar{x}=1/3$ and $\bar{y}=2/3$, and Area $=1$. So $\iint_D x\,dA = 1/3$ and $\iint_D y\,dA = 2/3$. $$\oint_C = \frac{9}{2}\!\left(\frac{1}{3}-\frac{2}{3}\right) = \frac{9}{2}\!\left(-\frac{1}{3}\right) = \boxed{-\frac{3}{2}}$$

Stewart §16.8 — HW07 Problems
HW16.8.6

Use Stokes' Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_S (\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S}$, where $\vec{F}(x,y,z) = e^{xy}\,\vec{i} + e^{xz}\,\vec{j} + x^2 z\,\vec{k}$ and $S$ is the half of the ellipsoid $4x^2+y^2+4z^2=4$ that lies to the right of the $xz$-plane ($y\geq 0$), oriented in the direction of the positive $y$-axis.
Solution — surface integral → line integral

Find the boundary $C$. The half-ellipsoid meets the $xz$-plane ($y=0$) along $4x^2+4z^2=4$, i.e., $x^2+z^2=1$. So $C$ is the unit circle in the $xz$-plane: $x^2+z^2=1$, $y=0$.

Orientation of $C$. The surface normal points in the $+y$ direction. By the right-hand rule (thumb in $+y$, fingers curl): viewed from the $+y$ side looking toward $-y$, the curve goes counterclockwise in the $xz$-plane. In standard $xz$ coordinates (with $x$ horizontal and $z$ vertical), counterclockwise means $x$ decreasing when $z>0$... Let's parametrize consistently: $\vec{r}(t)=\langle\cos t,\;0,\;\sin t\rangle$, $t\in[0,2\pi]$. At $t=0$: $(1,0,0)$; at $t=\pi/2$: $(0,0,1)$. This is counterclockwise viewed from $+y$. ✓

Evaluate the line integral. On $C$: $x=\cos t$, $y=0$, $z=\sin t$. So: $$\vec{F}(C(t)) = \langle e^{(\cos t)(0)},\; e^{(\cos t)(\sin t)},\; \cos^2 t\cdot\sin t\rangle = \langle 1,\; e^{\frac{1}{2}\sin 2t},\; \cos^2 t\sin t\rangle$$ $\vec{r}'(t)=\langle-\sin t,\;0,\;\cos t\rangle$. The $y$-component of $d\vec{r}$ is $0$, so $\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}=(1)(-\sin t)+(e^{\frac{1}{2}\sin 2t})(0)+(\cos^2 t\sin t)(\cos t)$: $$\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = -\sin t\,dt + \cos^3 t\sin t\,dt$$

Integrate over $[0,2\pi]$. $$\int_0^{2\pi}(-\sin t)\,dt = 0, \qquad \int_0^{2\pi}\cos^3 t\sin t\,dt = \Bigl[-\frac{\cos^4 t}{4}\Bigr]_0^{2\pi} = 0$$ $$\iint_S(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S} = \oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \boxed{0}$$

Both integrals are zero because $e^{\frac{1}{2}\sin 2t}$ never appears (the $y$-component of $d\vec{r}$ is zero on $C$), and the remaining terms integrate to zero over a full period by symmetry.

HW16.8.12

Use Stokes' Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$, where $\vec{F}(x,y,z) = ze^x\,\vec{i} + (z^3-y^3)\,\vec{j} + (x^2-z^3)\,\vec{k}$ and $C$ is the circle $y^2+z^2=4$, $x=3$, oriented clockwise as viewed from the origin.
Solution — line integral → surface integral

Choose surface $S$. The circle $y^2+z^2=4$, $x=3$ bounds the disk $S: y^2+z^2\leq 4$, $x=3$. This is the natural choice.

Orientation. $C$ is clockwise as viewed from the origin, i.e., clockwise when looking in the $+x$ direction. By the right-hand rule (or: clockwise from $+x$ = counterclockwise from $-x$), the compatible surface normal is $\hat{n}=-\vec{i}$, so $d\vec{S}=\langle -1,0,0\rangle\,dA$.

Compute curl $\vec{F}$. With $P=ze^x$, $Q=z^3-y^3$, $R=x^2-z^3$: $$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_x = R_y-Q_z = 0-3z^2 = -3z^2$$ $$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_y = P_z-R_x = e^x - 2x$$ $$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_z = Q_x-P_y = 0-0 = 0$$ $$\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \langle -3z^2,\;e^x-2x,\;0\rangle$$

Flux of curl. On $S$, $x=3$, so $e^x-2x = e^3-6$: $$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S} = \langle -3z^2,\;e^3-6,\;0\rangle\cdot\langle -1,0,0\rangle\,dA = 3z^2\,dA$$

Integrate over the disk. Use $y=r\cos\theta$, $z=r\sin\theta$, $r\in[0,2]$: $$\oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_D 3z^2\,dA = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^2 3r^2\sin^2\theta\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta$$ $$= 3\underbrace{\int_0^{2\pi}\sin^2\theta\,d\theta}_{=\pi}\;\underbrace{\int_0^2 r^3\,dr}_{=4} = 3\cdot\pi\cdot 4 = \boxed{12\pi}$$

RECALL Green's flux form from L11 — the 2D preview of this theorem — click to expand

In L11, Green's Theorem had a second "flux form": $$\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,ds = \iint_D \text{div}\,\vec{F}\,dA$$ where $\hat{n}$ is the outward normal to a 2D region $D$ with boundary $C$, and the right side integrates divergence over the region. The Divergence Theorem (Gauss's Theorem) is the exact 3D upgrade: replace the boundary curve $C$ with a closed surface $S$, replace the 2D region $D$ with a solid $E$, and replace $dA$ with $dV$.

The upgrade chain
Green's (2D): $\oint_C\vec{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,ds = \iint_D\text{div}\,\vec{F}\,dA$
Divergence Theorem (3D): $\oiint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \iiint_E\text{div}\,\vec{F}\,dV$

Same structure. One dimension higher. The $\oiint$ notation means $S$ is a closed surface.
The Big Idea
What the Divergence Theorem says
The core idea — sources inside = flux out
Think of $\vec{F}$ as a fluid velocity field. Divergence measures how much fluid is being created (or destroyed) at each interior point. The total rate of fluid being created inside a solid $E$ must equal the total rate of fluid flowing out through the boundary surface $S$. That's all the theorem says.
Divergence Theorem — Stewart §16.9
Let $E$ be a solid region bounded by a closed, piecewise-smooth surface $S$ oriented with the outward normal. If $\vec{F}$ has continuous partial derivatives on $E$, then: $$\oiint_S \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \iiint_E \text{div}\,\vec{F}\,dV$$

The Divergence Theorem always converts between a surface integral and a volume integral. Going left-to-right: replace a flux computation with a volume integral of div $\vec{F}$. Going right-to-left: if div $\vec{F}$ has a nice form, express a hard volume integral as a cleaner flux integral.

The Grand Unification
Green's, Stokes', and Divergence — one theorem

All three major theorems of vector calculus are special cases of the same idea: the integral of a derivative over a region equals the integral of the original over its boundary.

  • FTC$\int_a^b f'(x)\,dx = f(b)-f(a)$. Region: $[a,b]$. Boundary: $\{a,b\}$. Derivative: $f'$.
  • Green's$\iint_D(\partial_x Q - \partial_y P)\,dA = \oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy$. Region: $D$. Boundary: $C$. Derivative: curl in 2D.
  • Stokes'$\iint_S\text{curl}\,\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$. Region: surface $S$. Boundary: $C$. Derivative: curl.
  • Div Thm$\iiint_E\text{div}\,\vec{F}\,dV = \oiint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$. Region: $E$. Boundary: $S$. Derivative: divergence.
The Closing-Up Trick
When the surface isn't closed

The Divergence Theorem requires a closed surface. If your surface $S$ is open (like a paraboloid cap or a hemisphere), the strategy is:

Closing-up strategy
Add a simple surface $S_1$ (typically a flat disk) to close off $S$, forming a closed surface $S\cup S_1$ bounding a solid $E$. Apply the Divergence Theorem to get $\iint_S + \iint_{S_1} = \iiint_E\text{div}\,\vec{F}\,dV$. Then compute $\iint_{S_1}$ directly (usually easy — it's flat) and subtract.
Orientation warning for closing-up
The outward normal for the closed surface $S\cup S_1$ means: $S$ keeps its original orientation (upward = outward from top), and $S_1$ (the closing disk at the bottom) gets the downward orientation (pointing away from the interior $E$). Getting $S_1$'s orientation wrong flips your correction term.
Quick Check — True or False
T/F

If $\text{div}\,\vec{F} = 0$ everywhere inside a solid $E$, then the total flux of $\vec{F}$ through any closed surface bounding $E$ is zero.
TRUE

By the Divergence Theorem: $\oiint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \iiint_E\text{div}\,\vec{F}\,dV = \iiint_E 0\,dV = 0$. Physically: if no fluid is being created or destroyed anywhere inside $E$, then whatever flows in must flow out — net flux is zero. This is the 3D version of "conservative $\Rightarrow$ zero circulation" (Green's/Stokes').

T/F

You can apply the Divergence Theorem directly to a hemisphere (half-sphere) without closing it up.
FALSE

The Divergence Theorem requires a closed surface — one that completely encloses a solid region with no boundary curve. A hemisphere has a circular boundary (where it meets the $xy$-plane), so it's not closed. You must add the corresponding disk to form a closed surface, apply the theorem to the combined surface, then subtract the disk's contribution.

T/F

The outward normal on a closed surface always points away from the origin.
FALSE

"Outward" means pointing away from the interior of the solid $E$, not away from the origin. For a sphere centered at the origin, these coincide. But for a donut-shaped region, a box in the corner of space, or a non-origin-centered sphere, the outward normal is determined by the geometry of $E$ — not by the position relative to the origin.

Worked Example — Div Thm Shortcut
Worked Example — Flux through a closed surface using div

Find the outward flux of $\vec{F}=\langle x^3, y^3, z^3\rangle$ through the surface of the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=a^2$.

  • 1Check it's closed. The sphere is a closed surface enclosing the ball $E: x^2+y^2+z^2\leq a^2$. Divergence Theorem applies directly.
  • 2Compute div. $\text{div}\,\vec{F} = 3x^2 + 3y^2 + 3z^2 = 3(x^2+y^2+z^2) = 3\rho^2$ in spherical.
  • 3Volume integral in spherical. $$\iiint_E 3\rho^2\,dV = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^\pi\!\int_0^a 3\rho^2\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta = 3\cdot 2\pi\cdot 2\cdot\frac{a^5}{5} = \boxed{\dfrac{12\pi a^5}{5}}$$

Direct computation would require parametrizing the sphere and evaluating three separate flux integrals — a much harder calculation.

E15.1

Use the Divergence Theorem to find the outward flux of $\vec{F}=\langle x^2, y^2, z^2\rangle$ through the surface of the unit cube $[0,1]^3$.
Solution

Compute divergence. $\text{div}\,\vec{F} = 2x + 2y + 2z$.

Apply Div Thm. $$\oiint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \iiint_E(2x+2y+2z)\,dV = \int_0^1\!\int_0^1\!\int_0^1(2x+2y+2z)\,dz\,dy\,dx$$

By symmetry: $\iiint_E 2x\,dV = \iiint_E 2y\,dV = \iiint_E 2z\,dV$. Each equals $2\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot 1\cdot 1=1$. Total: $$= 1+1+1 = \boxed{3}$$

Compare the direct approach: 6 faces, each requiring a separate 2D integral. Divergence Theorem collapses all six into one clean integral.

E15.2

Find the upward flux of $\vec{F}=\langle xz, yz, z^2\rangle$ through the upper hemisphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=1$, $z\geq 0$.
Solution — closing-up trick

Close the surface. Add $S_1$: the disk $x^2+y^2\leq 1$, $z=0$, with downward normal $-\vec{k}$ (outward from the half-ball $E$). The closed surface $S\cup S_1$ bounds the upper half-ball.

Divergence. $\text{div}\,\vec{F} = z + z + 2z = 4z$.

Volume integral over upper half-ball. In spherical ($\rho\in[0,1]$, $\phi\in[0,\pi/2]$, $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$), $z=\rho\cos\phi$: $$\iiint_E 4z\,dV = 4\int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^{\pi/2}\!\int_0^1\rho\cos\phi\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta = 4\cdot 2\pi\cdot\Bigl[\tfrac{\sin^2\phi}{2}\Bigr]_0^{\pi/2}\cdot\Bigl[\tfrac{\rho^4}{4}\Bigr]_0^1 = 8\pi\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{1}{4}=\pi$$

Flux through $S_1$. On $z=0$: $\vec{F}=\langle 0,0,0\rangle$, so $\iint_{S_1}\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}=0$.

Solve for the hemisphere. $$\iint_S + \iint_{S_1} = \pi \implies \iint_S = \pi - 0 = \boxed{\pi}$$

E15.3

Use the Divergence Theorem to compute the outward flux of $\vec{F}=\langle 3xy^2, xe^z, z^3\rangle$ through the closed surface bounding the solid $E: x^2+y^2\leq 4$, $0\leq z\leq 1$.
Solution

Divergence. $\text{div}\,\vec{F} = 3y^2 + 0 + 3z^2$.

Volume integral in cylindrical ($r\in[0,2]$, $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$, $z\in[0,1]$, $y=r\sin\theta$): $$\iiint_E(3y^2+3z^2)\,dV = 3\iiint_E y^2\,dV + 3\iiint_E z^2\,dV$$

$\iiint_E y^2\,dV = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^2\!\int_0^1 r^2\sin^2\theta\cdot r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta = \underbrace{\int_0^{2\pi}\sin^2\theta\,d\theta}_{=\pi}\underbrace{\int_0^2 r^3\,dr}_{=4}\underbrace{\int_0^1 dz}_{=1}=4\pi$.

$\iiint_E z^2\,dV = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^2\!\int_0^1 z^2\cdot r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\underbrace{\int_0^2 r\,dr}_{=2}\underbrace{\int_0^1 z^2\,dz}_{=1/3}=\dfrac{4\pi}{3}$.

$$\oiint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = 3\!\left(4\pi+\frac{4\pi}{3}\right)=3\cdot\frac{16\pi}{3}=\boxed{16\pi}$$

HW16.9.20

Let $\vec{F}(x,y,z) = z\tan^{-1}(y^2)\,\vec{i} + z^3\ln(x^2+1)\,\vec{j} + z\,\vec{k}$. Find the flux of $\vec{F}$ across the part of the paraboloid $x^2+y^2+z=2$ that lies above the plane $z=1$, oriented upward.

Hint: the surface is not closed — close it up by adding the disk $x^2+y^2\leq 1$, $z=1$.
Solution — closing-up trick

Identify the geometry. The paraboloid $z = 2-x^2-y^2$ meets $z=1$ where $x^2+y^2=1$. Let $S$ = the paraboloid cap (oriented upward). Add $S_1$ = the disk $x^2+y^2\leq 1$, $z=1$, with downward normal $-\vec{k}$ (outward from the solid $E$ below). The solid $E$ is bounded above by $S$, below by $S_1$: $$E = \{(x,y,z): x^2+y^2\leq 1,\; 1\leq z\leq 2-x^2-y^2\}$$

Compute divergence. $$\text{div}\,\vec{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\bigl(z\tan^{-1}(y^2)\bigr) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\bigl(z^3\ln(x^2+1)\bigr) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(z) = 0 + 0 + 1 = 1$$ The messy-looking $x$ and $y$ components don't depend on $x$ and $y$ respectively — they vanish.

Volume integral. $\text{div}\,\vec{F}=1$, so the integral equals $\text{Vol}(E)$: $$\iiint_E 1\,dV = \iint_{x^2+y^2\leq 1}(2-x^2-y^2 - 1)\,dA = \iint_{x^2+y^2\leq 1}(1-r^2)\,dA$$ $$= \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^1(1-r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\Bigl[\frac{r^2}{2}-\frac{r^4}{4}\Bigr]_0^1 = 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{4}=\frac{\pi}{2}$$

Flux through $S_1$. On $z=1$, $d\vec{S}=-\vec{k}\,dA$ (downward, outward from $E$). $\vec{F}|_{z=1}=\langle\tan^{-1}(y^2),\;\ln(x^2+1),\;1\rangle$. $$\iint_{S_1}\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}=\iint_{x^2+y^2\leq 1}(1)(-1)\,dA = -\pi(1)^2 = -\pi$$

Solve for the paraboloid flux. $$\iint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} + \iint_{S_1}\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \frac{\pi}{2}$$ $$\iint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} + (-\pi) = \frac{\pi}{2} \implies \iint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} = \boxed{\dfrac{3\pi}{2}}$$

HW16.9.26

Use the Divergence Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_S (2x+2y+z^2)\,dS$, where $S$ is the unit sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=1$.
Solution — scalar integral via Div Thm

Key insight — rewrite as a flux integral. On the unit sphere, the outward unit normal is $\hat{n}=\langle x,y,z\rangle$. So $d\vec{S}=\hat{n}\,dS=\langle x,y,z\rangle\,dS$. We need a vector field $\vec{F}$ such that $\vec{F}\cdot\hat{n}=2x+2y+z^2$ on $S$. Try $\vec{F}=\langle 2,2,z\rangle$: on $S$, $\vec{F}\cdot\hat{n}=2x+2y+z^2$ ✓.

Convert to flux. $$\iint_S(2x+2y+z^2)\,dS = \iint_S\vec{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = \iint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}$$

Apply the Divergence Theorem. $\text{div}\,\vec{F}=\partial_x(2)+\partial_y(2)+\partial_z(z)=0+0+1=1$. $$\iint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}=\iiint_E 1\,dV=\text{Vol(unit ball)}=\frac{4\pi}{3}$$

Sanity check by symmetry. $\iint_S 2x\,dS=0$ and $\iint_S 2y\,dS=0$ (odd functions). For $\iint_S z^2\,dS$: by symmetry $\iint_S x^2\,dS=\iint_S y^2\,dS=\iint_S z^2\,dS$, so $3\iint_S z^2\,dS=\iint_S 1\,dS=4\pi$, giving $\iint_S z^2\,dS=4\pi/3$. Total: $\boxed{4\pi/3}$. ✓

L13 — Surface Integrals
R1

Evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_S (x^2+y^2)\,dS$ where $S$ is the cone $z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ below the plane $z=2$.
Solution

Parametrize. Use polar-style: $\vec{r}(r,\theta)=\langle r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, r\rangle$, $r\in[0,2]$ (since $z=r\leq 2$), $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$.

Cross product magnitude. $\vec{r}_r=\langle\cos\theta,\sin\theta,1\rangle$, $\vec{r}_\theta=\langle-r\sin\theta,r\cos\theta,0\rangle$. $$\vec{r}_r\times\vec{r}_\theta = \langle -r\cos\theta,\;-r\sin\theta,\;r\rangle,\quad |\vec{r}_r\times\vec{r}_\theta|=r\sqrt{2}$$

Integrate. On $S$, $x^2+y^2=r^2$: $$\iint_S(x^2+y^2)\,dS = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^2 r^2\cdot r\sqrt{2}\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\sqrt{2}\int_0^2 r^3\,dr = 2\pi\sqrt{2}\cdot\frac{16}{4} = \boxed{8\sqrt{2}\,\pi}$$

R2

Find the outward flux of $\vec{F}=\langle x,\;y,\;z^2\rangle$ through the closed surface of the cylinder $x^2+y^2=1$, $0\leq z\leq 1$ (including the top and bottom disks).
Solution — use the Divergence Theorem for speed

Divergence. $\text{div}\,\vec{F}=1+1+2z=2+2z$.

Volume integral over solid cylinder ($r\in[0,1]$, $z\in[0,1]$): $$\iiint_E(2+2z)\,dV = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^1\!\int_0^1(2+2z)\,r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\!\left(\int_0^1 r\,dr\right)\!\!\left(\int_0^1(2+2z)\,dz\right)$$ $$= 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot\Bigl[2z+z^2\Bigr]_0^1 = \pi\cdot 3 = \boxed{3\pi}$$

For reference, the direct approach (three surface pieces: lateral, top, bottom) gives the same answer but requires three separate parametrizations.

L14 — Stokes' Theorem
R3

Use Stokes' Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C(-y^2)\,dx + x\,dy + z^2\,dz$ where $C$ is the circle $x^2+y^2=4$, $z=3$, traversed counterclockwise when viewed from above.
Solution

Set up. $\vec{F}=\langle -y^2,x,z^2\rangle$. Choose $S$: the disk $x^2+y^2\leq 4$, $z=3$, with upward normal $\hat{n}=\vec{k}$ (consistent with counterclockwise $C$). Then $d\vec{S}=\langle 0,0,1\rangle\,dA$.

Curl. $$\text{curl}\,\vec{F}=\langle R_y-Q_z,\;P_z-R_x,\;Q_x-P_y\rangle=\langle 0-0,\;0-0,\;1-(-2y)\rangle=\langle 0,\;0,\;1+2y\rangle$$

Flux of curl. $\text{curl}\,\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}=(1+2y)\,dA$. By symmetry, $\iint_D 2y\,dA=0$. $$\oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_D(1+2y)\,dA = \iint_D 1\,dA = \pi(2)^2 = \boxed{4\pi}$$

R4

Use Stokes' Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_S(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S}$ where $\vec{F}=\langle y,-x,xyz\rangle$ and $S$ is the upper hemisphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=1$, $z\geq 0$, oriented upward.
Solution — convert to line integral

Boundary. $C$ is the unit circle $x^2+y^2=1$, $z=0$, counterclockwise from above (consistent with upward normal on $S$). Parametrize: $\vec{r}(t)=\langle\cos t,\sin t,0\rangle$, $t\in[0,2\pi]$.

$\vec{F}$ on $C$. $\vec{F}|_{z=0}=\langle\sin t,-\cos t,0\rangle$. And $\vec{r}'(t)=\langle-\sin t,\cos t,0\rangle$.

Dot product and integrate. $$\vec{F}\cdot\vec{r}'=\sin t(-\sin t)+(-\cos t)(\cos t)+0=-\sin^2 t-\cos^2 t=-1$$ $$\iint_S(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot d\vec{S}=\oint_C\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}=\int_0^{2\pi}(-1)\,dt=\boxed{-2\pi}$$

Note: computing curl $\vec{F}$ directly and integrating over the curved hemisphere would be significantly harder. The line integral over a circle is one of the cleanest calculations in the course.

L15 — Divergence Theorem
R5

Use the Divergence Theorem to find the outward flux of $\vec{F}=\langle x+y^2,\;y+z^2,\;z+x^2\rangle$ through the surface of the unit ball $x^2+y^2+z^2\leq 1$.
Solution

Divergence. $\text{div}\,\vec{F} = 1+1+1=3$.

Apply Div Thm. $$\oiint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}=\iiint_E 3\,dV=3\cdot\text{Vol(unit ball)}=3\cdot\frac{4\pi}{3}=\boxed{4\pi}$$

The seemingly complicated $y^2$, $z^2$, $x^2$ terms in $\vec{F}$ all differentiate into constants. This is the Divergence Theorem's magic: only the "diagonal" derivatives $\partial P/\partial x$, $\partial Q/\partial y$, $\partial R/\partial z$ survive.

R6

Find the upward flux of $\vec{F}=\langle x^2,\;y^2,\;z\rangle$ through the upper hemisphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$, $z\geq 0$.
Solution — closing-up trick

Close the surface. Add $S_1$: disk $x^2+y^2\leq 4$, $z=0$, with downward normal $-\vec{k}$ (outward from the upper half-ball $E$). The Divergence Theorem applies to $S\cup S_1$.

Divergence. $\text{div}\,\vec{F}=2x+2y+1$.

Volume integral over upper half-ball ($\rho\leq 2$, $0\leq\phi\leq\pi/2$). $$\iiint_E(2x+2y+1)\,dV = \underbrace{\iiint_E 2x\,dV}_{=0}+\underbrace{\iiint_E 2y\,dV}_{=0}+\iiint_E 1\,dV$$ The first two are zero by symmetry ($x$ and $y$ are odd over the half-ball). For the last: $$\iiint_E 1\,dV = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{4\pi}{3}(2)^3=\frac{16\pi}{3}$$

Flux through $S_1$. On $z=0$: $\vec{F}=\langle x^2,y^2,0\rangle$, $d\vec{S}=\langle 0,0,-1\rangle\,dA$. So $\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}=0$ and $\iint_{S_1}\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}=0$.

Result. $$\iint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}+0=\frac{16\pi}{3}\implies\iint_S\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S}=\boxed{\dfrac{16\pi}{3}}$$
