The Central Theorem of Complex Analysis
What the Cauchy Integral Formula actually says

By Lecture 18, we established that analytic functions satisfy $\oint_{\partial D} f(z)\,dz = 0$ (Cauchy's theorem). That told us integrals vanish when the integrand is analytic everywhere inside the contour. But what happens when the integrand has a singularity inside — say, $f(w)/(w - z_0)$ where $z_0$ is inside $D$?

The Cauchy Integral Formula (CIF) answers this precisely. Rather than vanishing, the integral exactly recovers the value of $f$ at the singular point $z_0$. This is remarkable: the behavior of $f$ on a boundary curve determines the values of $f$ at every interior point. Analyticity forces the interior to be completely determined by the boundary.

Theorem — Cauchy Integral Formula (Gamelin IV.4, eq. 4.1)

Let $D$ be a bounded domain with piecewise smooth boundary $\partial D$, and let $f$ be analytic on $\overline{D} = D \cup \partial D$. Then for every $z \in D$:

$$f(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{\partial D} \frac{f(w)}{w - z}\,dw$$

where $\partial D$ is traversed counterclockwise. In other words, $\oint_{\partial D} \frac{f(w)}{w-z}\,dw = 2\pi i \cdot f(z)$ for any $z$ inside the contour.

Notice what this immediately gives us: if you want to evaluate $\oint \frac{g(w)}{w - z_0}\,dw$ for some $g$ analytic inside $|w| = R$, just identify $f(w) = g(w)$ and read off $2\pi i \cdot g(z_0)$. Most contour integral problems in the course reduce to this pattern.

The $\varepsilon$-Disk Proof
How the proof works — shrinking to a tiny disk

The key idea is to reduce the integral over $\partial D$ to one over a tiny circle around $z$, which we can compute exactly. Start by writing the integrand as:

$$\frac{f(w)}{w-z} = \frac{f(z)}{w-z} + \frac{f(w)-f(z)}{w-z}$$

The function $h(w) = \frac{f(w)-f(z)}{w-z}$ is actually analytic near $z$ (the apparent singularity is removable because $f$ is differentiable there), so by Cauchy's theorem its integral over $\partial D$ is $0$. We just need to handle the $\frac{f(z)}{w-z}$ piece.

Step 1 — Deform to a disk $D_\varepsilon$

Since $h(w)$ is analytic everywhere in $\overline{D} \setminus \{z\}$, by deformation invariance we can replace $\partial D$ with a small circle $|w - z| = \varepsilon$ for any $\varepsilon$ small enough that $D_\varepsilon \subset D$. So:

$$\oint_{\partial D} \frac{f(w)}{w-z}\,dw = \oint_{|w-z|=\varepsilon} \frac{f(w)}{w-z}\,dw$$
Step 2 — Parametrize the small circle

On $|w - z| = \varepsilon$, write $w = z + \varepsilon e^{i\theta}$, $dw = i\varepsilon e^{i\theta}\,d\theta$, $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$:

$$\oint_{|w-z|=\varepsilon} \frac{f(w)}{w-z}\,dw = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{f(z + \varepsilon e^{i\theta})}{\varepsilon e^{i\theta}} \cdot i\varepsilon e^{i\theta}\,d\theta = i\int_0^{2\pi} f(z + \varepsilon e^{i\theta})\,d\theta$$
Step 3 — Take $\varepsilon \to 0$

As $\varepsilon \to 0$, the integrand $f(z + \varepsilon e^{i\theta}) \to f(z)$ uniformly (since $f$ is continuous). So:

$$i\int_0^{2\pi} f(z + \varepsilon e^{i\theta})\,d\theta \;\xrightarrow{\varepsilon \to 0}\; i\int_0^{2\pi} f(z)\,d\theta = i \cdot f(z) \cdot 2\pi = 2\pi i\,f(z)$$

Dividing both sides by $2\pi i$ gives the CIF. The proof is elegant: all the work is done by analyticity (which allows deformation) and continuity (which allows passing the limit through the integral).

Using the Formula — The Key Pattern
Identifying $f(w)$ and $z_0$ in a given integral

Every problem using CIF has the same structure: find the pole (where the denominator vanishes), check whether it lies inside the contour, then read off $f(z_0)$. The difficult step is often algebraic: isolating exactly one factor of $(w - z_0)$ in the denominator so you can identify what plays the role of $f(w)$.

Strategy for CIF integrals

1. Find all zeros of the denominator. These are the candidate poles.

2. For each pole $z_0$, check: is $|z_0 - \text{center}| < \text{radius}$ of contour?

3. For each pole inside the contour, write $\text{integrand} = \frac{f(w)}{w - z_0}$ where $f(w)$ is analytic inside. Apply CIF: $\oint = 2\pi i \cdot f(z_0)$.

4. If multiple poles are inside, use partial fractions to split the integral into a sum of CIF terms (one per pole).

Worked Example 1 — Single Pole Inside
Compute $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{z^n}{z-1}\,dz$ (lecture example)

The integrand is $\frac{z^n}{z-1}$. The only singularity is at $z = 1$, where the denominator vanishes. Since $|1| = 1 < 2$, the pole lies strictly inside $|z| = 2$.

The integrand is already in the form $\frac{f(w)}{w - z_0}$ with $f(w) = w^n$ (analytic everywhere) and $z_0 = 1$. Apply CIF directly:

CIF Application
$$\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{z^n}{z-1}\,dz = 2\pi i \cdot f(1) = 2\pi i \cdot 1^n = 2\pi i$$

The answer is $2\pi i$ for every integer $n \geq 0$. The $z^n$ factor in the numerator evaluates to $1$ at $z_0 = 1$ regardless of $n$.

Worked Example 2 — Partial Fractions with Multiple Poles
Compute $\displaystyle\oint_{|z-1|=2} \frac{dz}{z^2(z^2+1)e^z}$ (lecture example)

First identify all singularities of the integrand. The denominator $z^2(z^2+1)e^z$ vanishes when $z^2 = 0$ (a double pole at $z = 0$) or $z^2 = -1$ (simple poles at $z = \pm i$). The contour is $|z-1|=2$, centered at $1$ with radius $2$. Check each pole:

  • $z = 0$: $|0 - 1| = 1 < 2$ — inside
  • $z = i$: $|i - 1| = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.41 < 2$ — inside
  • $z = -i$: $|-i - 1| = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.41 < 2$ — inside

All three singularities are inside the contour. The partial fraction decomposition of $\frac{1}{z^2(z^2+1)}$ has the form:

$$\frac{1}{z^2(z^2+1)} = \frac{A}{z} + \frac{B}{z^2} + \frac{C}{z-i} + \frac{D}{z+i}$$

Multiplying through by $z^2(z^2+1)$ and matching coefficients: $A = 0$, $B = 1$, $C = i/2$, $D = -i/2$. So the integral splits as:

$$\oint \frac{dz}{z^2(z^2+1)e^z} = \oint \frac{dz}{z^2 e^z} + \frac{i}{2}\oint \frac{dz}{(z-i)e^z} + \frac{-i}{2}\oint \frac{dz}{(z+i)e^z}$$

The first term uses the higher derivative formula (next tab) since $z=0$ is a double pole. For the second and third terms, CIF gives:

Second and third terms via CIF

$\frac{i}{2}\oint \frac{e^{-z}}{z-i}\,dz = \frac{i}{2} \cdot 2\pi i \cdot e^{-i} = -\pi e^{-i} = -\pi(\cos 1 - i\sin 1)$

$\frac{-i}{2}\oint \frac{e^{-z}}{z+i}\,dz = \frac{-i}{2} \cdot 2\pi i \cdot e^{i} = \pi e^{i} = \pi(\cos 1 + i\sin 1)$

The first term $\oint \frac{dz}{z^2 e^z}$ is handled by the higher derivative formula — see the next tab. Combining all three pieces gives the final answer $-2\pi i(1 - \sin 1)$.

Differentiating Under the Integral Sign
From CIF to the higher derivative formula

The Cauchy Integral Formula $f(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint \frac{f(w)}{w-z}\,dw$ is remarkable not just for what it says, but for what it implies: the right-hand side is a complex function of $z$ that we can differentiate by differentiating under the integral sign. Since $\frac{f(w)}{w-z}$ is smooth in $z$ (for $z$ away from $\partial D$), we can differentiate as many times as we like.

Differentiating once with respect to $z$: $\frac{d}{dz}\frac{f(w)}{w-z} = \frac{f(w)}{(w-z)^2}$. Differentiating $m$ times: $\frac{d^m}{dz^m}\frac{f(w)}{w-z} = \frac{m!\, f(w)}{(w-z)^{m+1}}$. This gives the higher derivative formula.

Theorem — Higher Derivative Formula (Gamelin IV.4, eq. 4.2)

Under the same hypotheses as the CIF, for every $z \in D$ and every integer $m \geq 0$:

$$f^{(m)}(z) = \frac{m!}{2\pi i} \oint_{\partial D} \frac{f(w)}{(w-z)^{m+1}}\,dw$$

Equivalently: if $f$ is analytic on $\overline{D}$, then $\oint_{\partial D} \frac{f(w)}{(w-z)^{m+1}}\,dw = \frac{2\pi i}{m!}\,f^{(m)}(z)$.

For $m=0$ this is just the CIF. For $m=1$ it gives $\oint \frac{f(w)}{(w-z)^2}\,dw = 2\pi i\, f'(z)$. The formula is essential whenever the denominator has a repeated factor $(w - z_0)^{m+1}$ — that signals a pole of order $m+1$, and you apply this formula with that $m$.

Key Corollary — Analytic Implies Infinitely Differentiable
A theorem with no real-variable analogue

In real calculus, differentiable does not imply twice-differentiable. You can construct a function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ that is differentiable but whose derivative is not continuous, let alone differentiable again. The situation in complex analysis is completely different.

Corollary (Gamelin IV.4)

If $f$ is analytic on a domain $D$, then $f$ has complex derivatives of all orders on $D$. Moreover, all higher derivatives are themselves analytic on $D$.

Proof: The higher derivative formula gives $f^{(m)}(z) = \frac{m!}{2\pi i}\oint \frac{f(w)}{(w-z)^{m+1}}\,dw$ for every $m$, and this integral is well-defined and differentiable in $z$ for all $m$. So differentiability at order $1$ bootstraps to all orders.

This is a major structural result. It says complex differentiability is much more rigid than real differentiability — once you're analytic, you get all derivatives for free. Combined with the Cauchy-Riemann equations, this also means: if $f$ is analytic, then its real and imaginary parts $u$ and $v$ are harmonic functions (they satisfy $\Delta u = 0$, $\Delta v = 0$) with continuous partial derivatives of all orders.

Using the Higher Derivative Formula
Pattern recognition — spotting an $(m+1)$-th order pole

The key skill is recognizing when the higher derivative formula applies, rather than the basic CIF. Look at the power of $(w - z_0)$ in the denominator. If you see $(w - z_0)^{m+1}$ for some $m \geq 1$ (after factoring), you need the higher derivative formula with that $m$, applied to whatever analytic function remains in the numerator.

How to apply the formula

1. Identify the pole location $z_0$ and its order: if the integrand is $\frac{g(w)}{(w-z_0)^{m+1}}$ with $g$ analytic, apply the formula with this $m$.

2. Compute $g^{(m)}(z_0)$ by differentiating $m$ times and evaluating at $z_0$.

3. Apply: $\oint \frac{g(w)}{(w-z_0)^{m+1}}\,dw = \frac{2\pi i}{m!}\,g^{(m)}(z_0)$.

Worked Example — Double Pole
Compute $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=1} \frac{dz}{z^2(z^2+4)e^z}$ (lecture example)

The denominator is $z^2(z^2+4)e^z$. The singularities are at $z = 0$ (double pole), $z = \pm 2i$ (simple poles). Since the contour is $|z| = 1$, check which poles are inside:

  • $z = 0$: $|0| = 0 < 1$ — inside (double pole, order 2)
  • $z = 2i$: $|2i| = 2 > 1$ — outside
  • $z = -2i$: $|-2i| = 2 > 1$ — outside

Only $z = 0$ is inside, and it is a double pole (order $2 = m+1$, so $m = 1$). Rewrite the integrand:

$$\frac{1}{z^2(z^2+4)e^z} = \frac{f(w)}{w^{2}}, \quad \text{where } f(w) = \frac{1}{(w^2+4)e^w}$$

Here $f(w)$ is analytic at $w = 0$ (since $z^2 + 4 = 4 \neq 0$ at $w=0$, and $e^w \neq 0$). Apply the higher derivative formula with $m = 1$ and $z_0 = 0$:

$$\oint_{|z|=1} \frac{f(w)}{w^2}\,dw = \frac{2\pi i}{1!}\,f'(0)$$

Now compute $f'(w)$ at $w = 0$. Using the quotient rule on $f(w) = \frac{1}{(w^2+4)e^w}$:

$$f'(w) = -\frac{(2w)e^w + (w^2+4)e^w}{(w^2+4)^2 e^{2w}} = -\frac{e^w(2w + w^2 + 4)}{(w^2+4)^2 e^{2w}} = -\frac{w^2 + 2w + 4}{(w^2+4)^2 e^w}$$
Evaluating $f'(0)$ and the final answer
$$f'(0) = -\frac{0 + 0 + 4}{(4)^2 \cdot 1} = -\frac{4}{16} = -\frac{1}{4}$$ $$\oint_{|z|=1} \frac{dz}{z^2(z^2+4)e^z} = 2\pi i \cdot f'(0) = 2\pi i \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{4}\right) = -\frac{\pi i}{2}$$

Bounded Entire Functions Are Constant
What Liouville's Theorem says and why it matters

A function analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$ is called entire. The complex exponential $e^z$, polynomials, $\sin z$, $\cos z$ — all entire. Liouville's theorem places a stark constraint on entire functions: if they are also bounded (i.e., $|f(z)| \leq M$ for all $z \in \mathbb{C}$), they must be constant. Unlike real analysis, you cannot have a bounded non-constant entire function. For comparison, $\sin x$ is bounded and non-constant on $\mathbb{R}$, but $\sin z$ is not bounded on $\mathbb{C}$ (it blows up along the imaginary axis).

Theorem — Liouville's Theorem

If $f: \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}$ is entire (analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$) and bounded ($|f(z)| \leq M$ for all $z$), then $f$ is constant.

The proof uses the higher derivative formula in a beautiful way. If we can show $f'(z) = 0$ for all $z$, then $f$ is constant. We estimate $|f'(z)|$ using the CIF with a contour of radius $R$ centered at $z$, then let $R \to \infty$.

The Proof via CIF
Showing $f'(z) = 0$ for all $z$

Fix any point $z \in \mathbb{C}$ and any large radius $R$. By the higher derivative formula with $m = 1$ applied on the circle $|w - z| = R$:

$$f'(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|w-z|=R} \frac{f(w)}{(w-z)^2}\,dw$$

Take absolute values and apply the ML-estimate ($|\oint h\,dw| \leq M_h \cdot L$ where $L$ is arc length, $M_h$ is the max of $|h|$ on the contour):

ML-estimate on $|f'(z)|$

On $|w-z| = R$: the integrand has absolute value $\leq \frac{M}{R^2}$ (since $|f(w)| \leq M$ and $|w-z|^2 = R^2$). The length of the contour is $2\pi R$. So:

$$|f'(z)| \leq \frac{1}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{M}{R^2} \cdot 2\pi R = \frac{M}{R}$$

This holds for every $R > 0$. As $R \to \infty$, the bound $M/R \to 0$. Since $|f'(z)|$ is a fixed non-negative number squeezed between $0$ and $M/R$ for all $R$, we conclude $f'(z) = 0$. Since $z$ was arbitrary, $f' \equiv 0$ on $\mathbb{C}$, so $f$ is constant.

Famous Consequence — Fundamental Theorem of Algebra
Every non-constant polynomial has a root in $\mathbb{C}$

Liouville's theorem implies the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. Suppose $p(z)$ is a non-constant polynomial with no roots in $\mathbb{C}$. Then $g(z) = \frac{1}{p(z)}$ would be entire (analytic everywhere). For large $|z|$, $|p(z)| \to \infty$, so $|g(z)| \to 0$. Since $g$ is also continuous and hence bounded on any large disk, $g$ would be bounded and entire. By Liouville, $g$ is constant — contradiction. So $p$ must have a root.

Why this is surprising

This is a purely analytic proof of a purely algebraic theorem. The existence of roots in $\mathbb{C}$ follows from the rigidity imposed by analyticity, not from any algebraic manipulation. This is one of the deepest and most surprising connections in mathematics.

Problems 1–3 — Cauchy-Riemann equations (from Lecture 18)

The first three HW09 problems cover the Cauchy-Riemann equations from Lecture 18. They build the foundational skill of translating between "$f$ is analytic" and the concrete partial-derivative conditions on $u$ and $v$. Read $f = u + iv$, compute all four partials, check $u_x = v_y$ and $u_y = -v_x$.

P1 Check Cauchy-Riemann for two functions

(a) $f(z) = 4x^2 + 5x - 4y^2 + 9 + i(8xy + 5y - 1)$. Does this satisfy the C-R equations?

(b) $g(z) = x^3 + 3xy^2 - x + i(y^3 + 3x^2 y - y)$. Does this satisfy the C-R equations?

Solution (a) — $f$ satisfies CR

Identify $u = 4x^2 + 5x - 4y^2 + 9$ and $v = 8xy + 5y - 1$. Compute:

$u_x = 8x + 5$, $\quad v_y = 8x + 5$ $\quad\Rightarrow u_x = v_y$ ✓

$u_y = -8y$, $\quad -v_x = -8y$ $\quad\Rightarrow u_y = -v_x$ ✓

Both CR equations hold everywhere, so $f$ is analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$. Its derivative is $f'(z) = u_x + iv_x = (8x+5) + 8iy = 8(x+iy) + 5 = 8z + 5$.

Solution (b) — $g$ fails CR

Identify $u = x^3 + 3xy^2 - x$ and $v = y^3 + 3x^2y - y$. Compute:

$u_x = 3x^2 + 3y^2 - 1$, $\quad v_y = 3y^2 + 3x^2 - 1$ $\quad\Rightarrow u_x = v_y$ ✓

$u_y = 6xy$, $\quad -v_x = -6xy$ $\quad\Rightarrow u_y = -v_x$ requires $6xy = -6xy$, i.e., $12xy = 0$.

This only holds when $x = 0$ or $y = 0$, not everywhere. So $g$ is not analytic (except along the axes).

P2 Find $a, b$ so that $f$ is complex differentiable

Find real constants $a$ and $b$ such that $f(z) = 3x - y + 5 + i(ax + by - 3)$ is complex differentiable (analytic) on $\mathbb{C}$.

Here $u = 3x - y + 5$ and $v = ax + by - 3$.

Solution

Compute partials: $u_x = 3$, $u_y = -1$, $v_x = a$, $v_y = b$.

CR equations require:

$u_x = v_y \Rightarrow 3 = b$, so $b = 3$.

$u_y = -v_x \Rightarrow -1 = -a$, so $a = 1$.

With $a = 1$, $b = 3$: $f(z) = 3x - y + 5 + i(x + 3y - 3)$. Derivative: $f'(z) = u_x + iv_x = 3 + i$. This is constant, meaning $f$ is a complex-linear (affine) map — an $\mathbb{R}$-linear function that is also $\mathbb{C}$-linear.

P3 If $f$ and $\overline{f}$ are both analytic, then $f' = 0$

Let $f(z) = u + iv$ be a complex function. Suppose both $f(z)$ and $\overline{f(z)} = u - iv$ are analytic on a domain $D$. Show that $f'(z) = 0$ on $D$ (i.e., $f$ is constant).

Solution

Since $f = u + iv$ is analytic, the CR equations hold: $u_x = v_y$ and $u_y = -v_x$.

Since $\overline{f} = u - iv$ is analytic, apply CR to the function with real part $u$ and imaginary part $-v$: $u_x = (-v)_y = -v_y$ and $u_y = -(-v)_x = v_x$.

From the first CR system: $u_x = v_y$. From the second: $u_x = -v_y$. Adding: $2u_x = 0$, so $u_x = 0$. Then $v_y = u_x = 0$.

From the first CR system: $u_y = -v_x$. From the second: $u_y = v_x$. Adding: $2u_y = 0$, so $u_y = 0$. Then $v_x = -u_y = 0$.

All four partials vanish: $u_x = u_y = v_x = v_y = 0$. Therefore $f'(z) = u_x + iv_x = 0 + 0 = 0$. On a connected domain, $f' = 0$ implies $f$ is constant. $\square$

P4 Cauchy Integral Formula — single pole

Compute $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{\cos z}{3z - \pi}\,dz$.

The contour is the circle $|z| = 2$, traversed counterclockwise. The integrand has a singularity where the denominator vanishes: $3z - \pi = 0 \Rightarrow z = \pi/3$.

Solution

Step 1 — Check the pole location. Is $z_0 = \pi/3$ inside $|z| = 2$? Yes: $|\pi/3| \approx 1.047 < 2$. ✓

Step 2 — Factor to standard CIF form.

$$\frac{\cos z}{3z - \pi} = \frac{\cos z}{3(z - \pi/3)} = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{\cos z}{z - \pi/3}$$

Step 3 — Apply CIF with $f(w) = \cos w$ (analytic everywhere) and $z_0 = \pi/3$:

$$\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{\cos z}{3z-\pi}\,dz = \frac{1}{3}\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{\cos z}{z - \pi/3}\,dz = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 2\pi i \cdot \cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{2\pi i}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \boxed{\dfrac{\pi i}{3}}$$
P5 CIF — identify which pole is inside

Compute $\displaystyle\oint_{|z-2i|=2} \frac{\sin z}{z^2 + \pi^2}\,dz$.

The contour is a circle of radius $2$ centered at $2i$. The denominator $z^2 + \pi^2 = (z - i\pi)(z + i\pi)$ has zeros at $z = i\pi$ and $z = -i\pi$.

Solution

Step 1 — Check which poles are inside the contour.

$|i\pi - 2i| = |\pi - 2| \cdot |i| = \pi - 2 \approx 1.14 < 2$ — inside ✓

$|-i\pi - 2i| = |\pi + 2| \approx 5.14 > 2$ — outside ✗

Step 2 — Factor and apply CIF. Write $z^2 + \pi^2 = (z - i\pi)(z + i\pi)$, so:

$$\frac{\sin z}{z^2+\pi^2} = \frac{\sin z / (z + i\pi)}{z - i\pi} = \frac{f(z)}{z - i\pi}, \quad f(z) = \frac{\sin z}{z + i\pi}$$

Here $f(z)$ is analytic at $z = i\pi$ (the factor $z + i\pi$ is nonzero there: $i\pi + i\pi = 2i\pi \neq 0$). Apply CIF:

$$\oint \frac{\sin z}{z^2+\pi^2}\,dz = 2\pi i \cdot f(i\pi) = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{\sin(i\pi)}{i\pi + i\pi} = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{\sin(i\pi)}{2i\pi}$$

Step 3 — Evaluate $\sin(i\pi)$. Recall $\sin(i\theta) = i\sinh(\theta)$ (from the definition $\sin z = (e^{iz} - e^{-iz})/2i$). So $\sin(i\pi) = i\sinh(\pi)$.

$$= 2\pi i \cdot \frac{i\sinh(\pi)}{2i\pi} = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{\sinh(\pi)}{2\pi} = \boxed{i\sinh(\pi)}$$
Core Formulas — Lecture 19
Cauchy Integral Formula
$$f(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{\partial D} \frac{f(w)}{w-z}\,dw \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \oint_{\partial D} \frac{f(w)}{w-z_0}\,dw = 2\pi i\,f(z_0)$$

Requires: $f$ analytic on $\overline{D}$, $z_0 \in D$ (inside), $\partial D$ counterclockwise.

Higher Derivative Formula
$$f^{(m)}(z) = \frac{m!}{2\pi i}\oint_{\partial D} \frac{f(w)}{(w-z)^{m+1}}\,dw \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \oint_{\partial D}\frac{f(w)}{(w-z_0)^{m+1}}\,dw = \frac{2\pi i}{m!}\,f^{(m)}(z_0)$$

Use when the pole has order $m+1 \geq 2$. For $m=0$ this reduces to the CIF.

Liouville's Theorem

$f$ entire and $|f(z)| \leq M$ for all $z \in \mathbb{C}$ $\Rightarrow$ $f$ is constant.

Proof: $|f'(z)| \leq \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot\frac{M}{R^2}\cdot 2\pi R = \frac{M}{R} \to 0$ as $R \to \infty$.

$e^{i\theta}$ $= \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$

Common Mistakes
  • Forgetting to check if the pole is inside the contour. Always compute the distance from the pole to the center and compare with the radius.
  • Wrong order of pole. If the denominator has $(w-z_0)^2$, the order is $2$ (use $m=1$ in the higher derivative formula), not $1$.
  • Forgetting the $1/m!$ factor. The formula gives $\frac{2\pi i}{m!}f^{(m)}(z_0)$, not $2\pi i\,f^{(m)}(z_0)$.
  • Not factoring the constant. In P4, $3z - \pi = 3(z - \pi/3)$ — the factor of $3$ must come out before applying CIF.
  • Using Cauchy's theorem when there's a singularity inside. Cauchy's theorem requires $f$ analytic inside — $\frac{f(w)}{w-z_0}$ is not analytic at $z_0$.
