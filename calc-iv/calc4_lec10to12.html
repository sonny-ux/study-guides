The Big Idea
What Green's Theorem says in plain English
The core idea
You have a closed curve $C$ (a loop) in the plane. Walking counterclockwise around it, you trace the boundary of some region $D$ in the interior. Green's Theorem says:

A line integral around the boundary = a double integral over the interior.

Sometimes the loop integral is a nightmare and the area integral is trivial. Sometimes the reverse. Green's Theorem lets you swap whichever way saves work.
Green's Theorem — Stewart §16.4
Let $C$ be a positively oriented (counterclockwise), piecewise-smooth, simple closed curve bounding region $D$. If $P(x,y)$ and $Q(x,y)$ have continuous partial derivatives on $D$, then: $$\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) dA$$

The $\oint$ symbol (circle on the integral) just means you're integrating around a closed loop. The $P\,dx + Q\,dy$ is the same vector line integral from Lecture 8 — nothing new on the left side.

What "positively oriented" means
Orientation rule
Walk along $C$. If the region $D$ stays on your left, you're going counterclockwise — that's positive orientation. If $D$ is on your right, you're going clockwise, which is negative orientation and would flip the sign of the integral.

Intuition: imagine you're mowing a lawn. Positive orientation means the grass you haven't mowed yet is always on your left.
Sign flip warning
If the problem gives you $C$ traversed clockwise, you have two choices: (1) flip the sign of the whole result, or (2) reverse $C$ to make it counterclockwise before applying Green's. Either works — just don't forget.
The Shortcut Quantity $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y$
Why this combination appears

You've seen the curl test from Lecture 9: $\vec{F} = \langle P, Q\rangle$ is conservative on a simply connected domain iff $\partial_y P = \partial_x Q$, i.e., $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y = 0$. Green's Theorem says: if this quantity is not zero, it accumulates over the region $D$ and equals the boundary loop integral.

Connection to conservative fields
If $\vec{F}$ is conservative, then $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y = 0$ everywhere. Green's Theorem then says $\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = 0$ — which is exactly the statement that conservative fields do zero work on closed loops. Green's Theorem proves this from a new angle.
The shortcut
Compute $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y$ first. If it simplifies to a constant or a simple function, you've turned a painful loop integral into a routine double integral over $D$.
Area Formula
Computing area using a line integral

A beautiful consequence: if you pick $P$ and $Q$ so that $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y = 1$, then the right side becomes $\iint_D 1\,dA = \text{Area}(D)$. Several choices work:

Area formulas via Green's Theorem
$$A(D) = \oint_C x\,dy = -\oint_C y\,dx = \frac{1}{2}\oint_C x\,dy - y\,dx$$

The last one (the $\frac{1}{2}$ version) comes from averaging the first two, and it's symmetric. This is actually how planimeters — old mechanical devices for measuring land area — work.

Quick Check — True or False
T/F

Green's Theorem applies to any closed curve $C$, even if it crosses itself.
FALSE

Green's Theorem requires $C$ to be a simple closed curve — meaning it does not cross itself. A self-intersecting loop doesn't bound a single well-defined region $D$, so the right-hand double integral isn't well-defined. Green's does extend to multiply-connected regions, but that's a more careful setup involving multiple boundary curves.

T/F

If $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y = 3$ everywhere on $D$, then $\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = 3 \cdot \text{Area}(D)$.
TRUE

By Green's Theorem: $\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D 3\,dA = 3\iint_D dA = 3\cdot\text{Area}(D)$. This is the key shortcut — when the combination $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y$ is a constant, Green's reduces the loop integral to a simple area calculation. See E10.4 for an explicit example.

Worked Example — Step by Step
Worked Example — Region between two parabolas

Evaluate $\oint_C y\,dx + x^2\,dy$ where $C$ is the boundary of the region between $y = x^2$ and $y = x$ (from $(0,0)$ to $(1,1)$ along $y=x$, then back along $y=x^2$), traversed counterclockwise.

  • 1Identify $P$ and $Q$. Here $P = y$ and $Q = x^2$.
  • 2Compute the key quantity. $\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 2x - 1$.
  • 3Set up the double integral over $D$. The region between $y = x^2$ and $y = x$ for $0 \le x \le 1$: $$\iint_D (2x-1)\,dA = \int_0^1\int_{x^2}^{x}(2x-1)\,dy\,dx$$
  • 4Integrate inner first. $\int_{x^2}^{x}(2x-1)\,dy = (2x-1)(x - x^2) = (2x-1)x(1-x)$.
  • 5Integrate outer. Expand $(2x-1)x(1-x) = 2x^2 - 2x^3 - x + x^2 = 3x^2 - 2x^3 - x$. $$\int_0^1(3x^2 - 2x^3 - x)\,dx = \Bigl[x^3 - \tfrac{x^4}{2} - \tfrac{x^2}{2}\Bigr]_0^1 = 1 - \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{2} = 0$$

The answer is $0$ — not because $\vec{F}$ is conservative (check: $\partial_y P = 1 \ne 2x = \partial_x Q$ in general), but because the positive and negative contributions of $2x-1$ over $D$ happen to cancel.

Practice — Green's Theorem (Exercise 10 + Stewart §16.4)
E10.1

Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C (y + e^{\sqrt{x}})\,dx + (2x + \cos y^2)\,dy$ where $C$ is the boundary of the region enclosed between $y = x^2$ and $x = y^2$, traversed counterclockwise.
Solution

Step 1 — Extract $P$ and $Q$. $P = y + e^{\sqrt{x}}$ and $Q = 2x + \cos y^2$. Notice that $P$ involves $e^{\sqrt{x}}$ which is a nightmare to integrate directly — that's your cue to use Green's.

Step 2 — Compute the curl quantity. $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 2 - 1 = 1$$ The painful terms $e^{\sqrt{x}}$ and $\cos y^2$ vanish when you differentiate with respect to the other variable. This is the payoff.

Step 3 — Apply Green's Theorem. Since the integrand is $1$, the double integral equals the area of $D$: $$\oint_C = \iint_D 1\,dA = \text{Area}(D)$$

Step 4 — Find Area$(D)$. The region between $y = x^2$ and $x = y^2$ (equivalently $y = \sqrt{x}$) for $0 \le x \le 1$: $$\text{Area} = \int_0^1(\sqrt{x} - x^2)\,dx = \Bigl[\frac{2}{3}x^{3/2} - \frac{x^3}{3}\Bigr]_0^1 = \frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{3} = \boxed{\frac{1}{3}}$$

E10.2

Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C (3 + e^{x^2})\,dx + (\arctan y + 3x^2)\,dy$ where $C$ is the boundary of the quarter-annulus in the first quadrant between $r = 1$ and $r = 2$, traversed counterclockwise.
Solution

Step 1 — Extract $P$ and $Q$. $P = 3 + e^{x^2}$ (no $y$), $Q = \arctan y + 3x^2$.

Step 2 — Compute curl quantity. $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 6x - 0 = 6x$$ Again, the dangerous terms $e^{x^2}$ and $\arctan y$ drop out.

Step 3 — Set up polar double integral. $D$ is the quarter-annulus $1 \le r \le 2$, $0 \le \theta \le \pi/2$. With $x = r\cos\theta$ and $dA = r\,dr\,d\theta$: $$\iint_D 6x\,dA = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_1^2 6r\cos\theta \cdot r\,dr\,d\theta = 6\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos\theta\,d\theta\int_1^2 r^2\,dr$$

Step 4 — Evaluate. $$= 6\cdot\Bigl[\sin\theta\Bigr]_0^{\pi/2}\cdot\Bigl[\frac{r^3}{3}\Bigr]_1^2 = 6\cdot 1\cdot\frac{8-1}{3} = 6\cdot\frac{7}{3} = \boxed{14}$$

E10.3

Let $\vec{F}(x,y) = \langle y\cos x - xy\sin x,\; xy + x\cos x\rangle$. Evaluate $\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$ around the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(0,4)$, $(2,0)$, oriented counterclockwise.
Solution

Step 1 — Extract $P$ and $Q$. $P = y\cos x - xy\sin x$, $Q = xy + x\cos x$.

Step 2 — Compute curl quantity. $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = y + \cos x - x\sin x$$ $$\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \cos x - x\sin x$$ $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = (y + \cos x - x\sin x) - (\cos x - x\sin x) = y$$

Step 3 — Describe triangle $D$. The hypotenuse goes from $(0,4)$ to $(2,0)$, i.e. $y = 4 - 2x$ for $0 \le x \le 2$. So for fixed $x \in [0,2]$, $y$ runs from $0$ to $4-2x$: $$\iint_D y\,dA = \int_0^2\int_0^{4-2x} y\,dy\,dx$$

Step 4 — Evaluate. $$\int_0^2\frac{(4-2x)^2}{2}\,dx = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^2(4-2x)^2\,dx$$ Let $u = 4-2x$, $du = -2\,dx$: $$= \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\int_0^4 u^2\,du = \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{64}{3} = \boxed{\frac{16}{3}}$$

E10.4

Let $\vec{F}(x,y) = \langle x^2 + y,\; 3x - y^2\rangle$ and let $C$ be a simple closed curve bounding a region $D$ with $\text{Area}(D) = 6$. Evaluate $\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$ without knowing the specific shape of $D$.
Solution

Step 1 — Compute curl quantity. $P = x^2 + y$, $Q = 3x - y^2$. $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 3 - 1 = 2$$ The curl density is a constant $2$ regardless of where you are in $D$.

Step 2 — Apply Green's Theorem. $$\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_D 2\,dA = 2\,\text{Area}(D) = 2\cdot 6 = \boxed{12}$$

The specific shape of $D$ is completely irrelevant — only its area matters. This is a powerful feature of Green's Theorem when the curl density is constant.

Stewart §16.4 — HW06 Problems
HW16.4.14

Use Green's Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$ where $\vec{F}(x,y) = \langle y^2\cos x,\ x^2 + 2y\sin x\rangle$ and $C$ is the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(2,6)$, and $(2,0)$, oriented counterclockwise.
Solution

Step 1 — Compute the curl quantity. $P = y^2\cos x$, $Q = x^2 + 2y\sin x$. $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = (2x + 2y\cos x) - (2y\cos x) = 2x$$

Step 2 — Describe the region $D$. The triangle has vertices $(0,0)$, $(2,0)$, $(2,6)$. The edges are: bottom $y=0$ (from $x=0$ to $x=2$), right $x=2$ (from $y=0$ to $y=6$), and hypotenuse from $(0,0)$ to $(2,6)$, which is $y = 3x$. So for fixed $x \in [0,2]$, $y$ runs from $0$ to $3x$.

Step 3 — Evaluate. $$\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_D 2x\,dA = \int_0^2\int_0^{3x} 2x\,dy\,dx = \int_0^2 2x(3x)\,dx = \int_0^2 6x^2\,dx$$ $$= \left[2x^3\right]_0^2 = 16 = \boxed{16}$$

HW16.4.16

Use Green's Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C (1 - y^3)\,dx + (x^3 + e^{y^2})\,dy$, where $C$ is the boundary of the region between the circles $x^2+y^2=4$ and $x^2+y^2=9$, oriented so that the region lies to the left.
Solution

Step 1 — Compute the curl quantity. $P = 1 - y^3$, $Q = x^3 + e^{y^2}$. $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 3x^2 - (-3y^2) = 3x^2 + 3y^2 = 3r^2$$

Step 2 — The region $D$. $D$ is the annulus $2 \le r \le 3$. The orientation means the outer boundary is counterclockwise and the inner boundary is clockwise — precisely the standard positive orientation for an annular region.

Step 3 — Evaluate in polar. $$\oint_C = \iint_D 3(x^2+y^2)\,dA = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_2^3 3r^2 \cdot r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\int_2^3 3r^3\,dr$$ $$= 2\pi\left[\frac{3r^4}{4}\right]_2^3 = 2\pi\cdot\frac{3}{4}(81-16) = 2\pi\cdot\frac{195}{4} = \boxed{\dfrac{195\pi}{2}}$$

RECALL The $3\times 3$ determinant and cross product — click to expand

Curl is computed as a $3\times 3$ determinant. The cross product of two 3D vectors $\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2,a_3\rangle$ and $\vec{b} = \langle b_1,b_2,b_3\rangle$ is:

Cross product via determinant
$$\vec{a}\times\vec{b} = \begin{vmatrix}\vec{i} & \vec{j} & \vec{k}\\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3\\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3\end{vmatrix} = \vec{i}(a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2) - \vec{j}(a_1 b_3 - a_3 b_1) + \vec{k}(a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)$$

The $\vec{i}$ component is the $2\times 2$ minor of the last two rows excluding column 1. The $\vec{j}$ component gets a minus sign (cofactor expansion). The $\vec{k}$ component is the minor excluding column 3. Remember: $+$, $-$, $+$ for the three terms.

Curl — the Rotation Detector
What curl is — in words first
Physical meaning
Imagine dropping a tiny paddle wheel into a fluid whose velocity field is $\vec{F}$. If the wheel spins, the curl is nonzero. If the fluid flows uniformly past the wheel and it doesn't rotate, the curl is zero. Curl measures the local swirling tendency or rotation of a field around each point.

For $\vec{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$ in 3D, the curl is a new vector field defined as the "cross product" of the nabla operator $\nabla = \langle \partial_x, \partial_y, \partial_z \rangle$ with $\vec{F}$:

Curl — Definition
$$\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \nabla\times\vec{F} = \begin{vmatrix}\vec{i} & \vec{j} & \vec{k}\\ \partial_x & \partial_y & \partial_z\\ P & Q & R\end{vmatrix}$$ $$= \left\langle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z},\;\; \frac{\partial P}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial R}{\partial x},\;\; \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right\rangle$$

Notice the last component: $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y$ — that's exactly the quantity from Green's Theorem. So Green's Theorem is really computing the $z$-component of curl integrated over $D$.

Key Theorem — gradient fields have zero curl
If $\vec{F} = \nabla f$ (i.e. $\vec{F}$ is conservative), then $\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \nabla\times\nabla f = \vec{0}$.

Why? Because curl produces things like $\partial_y\partial_z f - \partial_z\partial_y f$, and mixed partials are equal (Clairaut's theorem), so each component is zero.
Converse — on simply connected domains
If $\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \vec{0}$ everywhere on a simply connected domain (no holes, no weird topology), then $\vec{F}$ is conservative — a potential function exists. This is the 3D version of the curl test from Lecture 9.
Quick Check — True or False
T/F

If $\vec{F} = \nabla f$ for some scalar $f$, then $\nabla\times\vec{F} = \vec{0}$.
TRUE

This is the theorem $\text{curl}(\nabla f) = \vec{0}$. Each component of $\nabla\times\nabla f$ looks like $\partial_y\partial_z f - \partial_z\partial_y f$, which is zero by Clairaut's theorem on the equality of mixed partials. Geometrically: gradient fields point in the direction of steepest ascent of a height function, and they can't swirl around any axis.

T/F

If $\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \vec{0}$ on a domain $D$, then $\vec{F}$ must be conservative.
FALSE (in general)

The converse only holds on simply connected domains (no holes). On a domain with holes, zero curl does not guarantee a potential exists. The same subtlety as in 2D: $\vec{F} = \frac{1}{x^2+y^2}\langle -y, x, 0\rangle$ has zero curl on $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{z\text{-axis}\}$, but is not conservative there.

Divergence — the Source Detector
What divergence is — in words first
Physical meaning
Put a tiny sphere of gas at a point in a velocity field $\vec{F}$. If the gas is expanding (flow points outward), the divergence is positive — that point is a source. If the gas is contracting (flow points inward), divergence is negative — that's a sink. If the total flow in equals total flow out, divergence is zero — the field is incompressible at that point.
Divergence — Definition
For $\vec{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$: $$\text{div}\,\vec{F} = \nabla\cdot\vec{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}$$ This is a scalar, not a vector. You add up the rates of change in each component's own direction.
Key Theorem — div(curl F) = 0 always
For any smooth vector field $\vec{F}$: $$\text{div}(\text{curl}\,\vec{F}) = \nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\vec{F}) = 0$$ This is always true — no conditions needed. Why? When you expand $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\vec{F})$, every term appears twice with opposite signs (again, Clairaut's theorem on mixed partials). They cancel.

The mnemonic: $\text{curl}(\text{grad}) = 0$ and $\text{div}(\text{curl}) = 0$. These are like the vector calculus analogs of "the boundary of a boundary is empty."

Green's Theorem Rewritten via Curl and Divergence
Two forms of Green's Theorem

Green's Theorem has two equivalent forms, each with a different physical meaning:

Circulation Form (what we used in L10)
$$\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_D (\text{curl}\,\vec{F})\cdot\vec{k}\,dA$$ The left side measures the net circulation (tangential flow) around $C$. The right side integrates the $z$-component of curl — the local rotational intensity — over $D$.
Flux Form (normal form)
$$\oint_C \vec{F}\cdot\vec{n}\,ds = \iint_D \text{div}\,\vec{F}\,dA$$ Here $\vec{n}$ is the outward unit normal to $C$ and $\vec{F}\cdot\vec{n}\,ds$ measures flow across (normal to) the boundary. The right side integrates divergence over $D$ — total source strength inside equals total flux out. This is a 2D preview of the Divergence Theorem (coming in L13–14).
Quick Check — True or False
T/F

$\text{div}(\text{curl}\,\vec{F}) = 0$ only if $\vec{F}$ is conservative.
FALSE

The identity $\text{div}(\text{curl}\,\vec{F}) = \vec{0}$ holds for any smooth vector field $\vec{F}$, regardless of whether it's conservative. It has nothing to do with conservativity — it's a purely algebraic identity that follows from the symmetry of mixed partial derivatives.

How to approach these problems
Strategy for identities
These are proofs, not computations. Write out both sides component by component using the definitions of div and curl, apply the product rule for derivatives, and show they match. Don't panic — every identity here follows from three tools: (1) the definition of div as $\partial_x P + \partial_y Q + \partial_z R$, (2) the definition of curl as the $3\times 3$ determinant, and (3) the Leibniz product rule $\partial_x(fg) = f\partial_x g + g\partial_x f$.
Practice — Curl and Divergence Identities (Exercise 11 + Stewart §16.5)
E11.1

Identity proof. Prove that $\text{div}(\vec{F} + \vec{G}) = \text{div}\,\vec{F} + \text{div}\,\vec{G}$ for vector fields $\vec{F} = \langle P_1, Q_1, R_1\rangle$ and $\vec{G} = \langle P_2, Q_2, R_2\rangle$.
Solution — Linearity of Divergence

Write out the left side. $\vec{F} + \vec{G} = \langle P_1+P_2,\; Q_1+Q_2,\; R_1+R_2\rangle$, so: $$\text{div}(\vec{F}+\vec{G}) = \frac{\partial(P_1+P_2)}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial(Q_1+Q_2)}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial(R_1+R_2)}{\partial z}$$

Apply linearity of partial derivatives. Since differentiation is linear ($\partial_x(f+g) = \partial_x f + \partial_x g$): $$= \frac{\partial P_1}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial P_2}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q_1}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial Q_2}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R_1}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial R_2}{\partial z}$$

Regroup. $$= \underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial P_1}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial Q_1}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial R_1}{\partial z}\right)}_{\text{div}\,\vec{F}} + \underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial P_2}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial Q_2}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial R_2}{\partial z}\right)}_{\text{div}\,\vec{G}} = \text{div}\,\vec{F} + \text{div}\,\vec{G} \qquad\square$$

E11.2

Identity proof. Prove that $\text{curl}(\vec{F} + \vec{G}) = \text{curl}\,\vec{F} + \text{curl}\,\vec{G}$.
Solution — Linearity of Curl

Focus on one component — the $\vec{i}$ component. The $x$-component of $\text{curl}(\vec{F}+\vec{G})$ is: $$\frac{\partial(R_1+R_2)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial(Q_1+Q_2)}{\partial z}$$

By linearity of partial derivatives: $$= \frac{\partial R_1}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q_1}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial R_2}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q_2}{\partial z} = (\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_x + (\text{curl}\,\vec{G})_x$$

Same argument for $\vec{j}$ and $\vec{k}$ components. Each component of curl is a difference of two partial derivatives, and linearity of partial derivatives splits each one. Combining all three components: $$\text{curl}(\vec{F}+\vec{G}) = \text{curl}\,\vec{F} + \text{curl}\,\vec{G} \qquad\square$$

E11.3

Identity proof. Prove the product rule for divergence: $\text{div}(f\vec{F}) = f\,\text{div}\,\vec{F} + \vec{F}\cdot\nabla f$, where $f$ is a scalar function and $\vec{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$.
Solution — Product Rule for Divergence

Write out $f\vec{F}$. $f\vec{F} = \langle fP,\; fQ,\; fR\rangle$, so: $$\text{div}(f\vec{F}) = \frac{\partial(fP)}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial(fQ)}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial(fR)}{\partial z}$$

Apply the scalar product rule to each term. For example, $\partial_x(fP) = f\partial_x P + P\partial_x f$. Doing all three: $$= f\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + P\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + f\frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + Q\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} + f\frac{\partial R}{\partial z} + R\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}$$

Regroup. $$= f\underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial R}{\partial z}\right)}_{\text{div}\,\vec{F}} + \underbrace{P\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}+Q\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}+R\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}}_{\vec{F}\cdot\nabla f} = f\,\text{div}\,\vec{F} + \vec{F}\cdot\nabla f \qquad\square$$

E11.4

Identity proof. Prove the product rule for curl: $\text{curl}(f\vec{F}) = f\,\text{curl}\,\vec{F} + (\nabla f)\times\vec{F}$.
Solution — Product Rule for Curl

Focus on the $\vec{i}$ (x) component of $\text{curl}(f\vec{F})$.

$f\vec{F} = \langle fP, fQ, fR\rangle$. The $x$-component of its curl is: $$\frac{\partial(fR)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial(fQ)}{\partial z}$$

Apply the product rule to each term: $$= f\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} + R\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - f\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z} - Q\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}$$

Regroup: $$= f\underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\right)}_{(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_x} + \underbrace{R\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - Q\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}}_{((\nabla f)\times\vec{F})_x}$$

Check the last piece: $(\nabla f)\times\vec{F} = \langle \partial_x f, \partial_y f, \partial_z f\rangle\times\langle P,Q,R\rangle$. Its $x$-component is $(\partial_y f)R - (\partial_z f)Q$ — which matches. The $\vec{j}$ and $\vec{k}$ components work identically. Therefore: $$\text{curl}(f\vec{F}) = f\,\text{curl}\,\vec{F} + (\nabla f)\times\vec{F} \qquad\square$$

Stewart §16.5 — HW06 Problems
HW16.5.20

Let $\vec{F}(x,y,z) = \langle xyz,\ x^2 y,\ x^2 z^2 \rangle$. Find $\text{curl}\,\vec{F}$ and $\text{div}\,\vec{F}$.
Solution

Curl. $P = xyz$, $Q = x^2 y$, $R = x^2 z^2$. $$\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \left\langle R_y - Q_z,\; P_z - R_x,\; Q_x - P_y\right\rangle$$

$R_y = 0$, $Q_z = 0$ $\Rightarrow$ first component: $0 - 0 = 0$.

$P_z = xy$, $R_x = 2xz^2$ $\Rightarrow$ second component: $xy - 2xz^2$.

$Q_x = 2xy$, $P_y = xz$ $\Rightarrow$ third component: $2xy - xz$.

$$\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \langle 0,\; xy - 2xz^2,\; 2xy - xz\rangle$$

Divergence. $$\text{div}\,\vec{F} = P_x + Q_y + R_z = yz + x^2 + 2x^2 z$$

Note that $\text{curl}\,\vec{F} \neq \vec{0}$, confirming $\vec{F}$ is not conservative (as expected for a field with $x$-dependence in the rotation). The divergence $yz + x^2 + 2x^2 z$ is nonzero, so $\vec{F}$ has sources and sinks throughout the domain.

RECALL Parametric curves from L7–9 — the same idea, now in 3D — click to expand

In Lectures 7–9, a parametric curve used one parameter $t$ to trace out a 1D object (a path) in 2D or 3D space: $\vec{\gamma}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t), z(t)\rangle$.

Now we upgrade: a parametric surface uses two parameters $u$ and $v$ to trace out a 2D object (a surface) in 3D space. Same logic, one dimension higher. The pair $(u,v)$ plays the role $t$ played before.

The analogy
Curve: $t \in [a,b] \longrightarrow$ point in $\mathbb{R}^3$. One parameter, traces a line-like thing.
Surface: $(u,v) \in D \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \longrightarrow$ point in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Two parameters, traces a sheet-like thing.

The formula $\vec{\gamma}'(t)$ gave us a tangent vector to the curve. For surfaces, partial derivatives $\vec{\gamma}_u$ and $\vec{\gamma}_v$ give us two tangent vectors that span the tangent plane to the surface at each point.

Parametric Surfaces — the Setup
What a parametric surface is
The core idea
A surface in 3D is a 2D thing living in 3D space. To describe it, you need two independent directions to move — so you need two parameters. A parametric surface is a map: $$\vec{\gamma}(u,v) = \langle x(u,v),\; y(u,v),\; z(u,v)\rangle, \quad (u,v)\in D\subseteq\mathbb{R}^2$$ Think of it as: pick any point $(u,v)$ in a flat parameter domain $D$, run it through $\vec{\gamma}$, and you get a point on the surface in 3D. As $(u,v)$ ranges over $D$, you trace out the whole surface.
Key examples
  • Sphere$\vec{\gamma}(\phi,\theta) = \langle \sin\phi\cos\theta,\; \sin\phi\sin\theta,\; \cos\phi\rangle$ for $\phi\in[0,\pi]$, $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$. Here $\phi$ is the polar angle from the north pole and $\theta$ is the longitude. As $\phi$ sweeps top to bottom and $\theta$ goes around, you cover the whole unit sphere.
  • Cylinder$\vec{\gamma}(\theta, z) = \langle\cos\theta,\; \sin\theta,\; z\rangle$ for $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$, $z\in[0,h]$. The $\theta$ parameter goes around the circle; $z$ goes up and down the cylinder.
  • GraphIf your surface is just $z = f(x,y)$, the simplest parametrization is $\vec{\gamma}(x,y) = \langle x,\; y,\; f(x,y)\rangle$. Here $u = x$ and $v = y$ — you use the coordinates themselves as parameters. This always works for graphs.
Surface Area Formula
Deriving the area formula — the key objects

To compute area, we need to understand what happens when you take a tiny step $du$ or $dv$ in parameter space. The partial derivatives tell you the answer:

Tangent vectors
$$\vec{\gamma}_u = \frac{\partial\vec{\gamma}}{\partial u} = \left\langle \frac{\partial x}{\partial u},\; \frac{\partial y}{\partial u},\; \frac{\partial z}{\partial u}\right\rangle$$ $$\vec{\gamma}_v = \frac{\partial\vec{\gamma}}{\partial v} = \left\langle \frac{\partial x}{\partial v},\; \frac{\partial y}{\partial v},\; \frac{\partial z}{\partial v}\right\rangle$$ These two vectors are tangent to the surface at each point — $\vec{\gamma}_u$ points in the direction the surface moves when you increase $u$, and $\vec{\gamma}_v$ when you increase $v$.
Why the cross product gives area
A tiny rectangle $[u, u+du]\times[v, v+dv]$ in parameter space maps to a tiny parallelogram on the surface with sides approximately $\vec{\gamma}_u\,du$ and $\vec{\gamma}_v\,dv$. The area of a parallelogram with sides $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ is $|\vec{a}\times\vec{b}|$. So the tiny surface area element is: $$dS = |\vec{\gamma}_u\times\vec{\gamma}_v|\,du\,dv$$ The vector $\vec{\gamma}_u\times\vec{\gamma}_v$ is also normal to the surface — this will be crucial for surface integrals in L13.
Surface Area Formula
$$A(S) = \iint_D |\vec{\gamma}_u\times\vec{\gamma}_v|\,dA$$ where $D$ is the parameter domain and $dA = du\,dv$.
Special case: graph $z = f(x,y)$

If $\vec{\gamma}(x,y) = \langle x, y, f(x,y)\rangle$, then $\vec{\gamma}_x = \langle 1, 0, f_x\rangle$ and $\vec{\gamma}_y = \langle 0, 1, f_y\rangle$. Their cross product is $\langle -f_x, -f_y, 1\rangle$, with magnitude $\sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2}$. So:

Graph surface area (the pre-midterm formula)
$$A(S) = \iint_D \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)^2}\,dA$$ The extra factor $\sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2}$ accounts for the fact that a tilted surface covers more area than its shadow on the $xy$-plane. When $f$ is constant (flat surface), this factor is $1$ and area equals the area of $D$.
Quick Check — True or False
T/F

The vector $\vec{\gamma}_u \times \vec{\gamma}_v$ is tangent to the surface $S$.
FALSE

It's the exact opposite. Both $\vec{\gamma}_u$ and $\vec{\gamma}_v$ are tangent to the surface — they lie in the tangent plane. Their cross product is therefore normal (perpendicular) to the surface, since the cross product of two vectors is perpendicular to both of them. This normal vector is what we'll use to set up oriented surface integrals in L13.

T/F

For the surface $z = f(x,y)$, the surface area is always at least as large as the area of the projection $D$ onto the $xy$-plane.
TRUE

The surface area formula is $\iint_D \sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2}\,dA$. Since $\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2} \ge \sqrt{1} = 1$ everywhere, we have $A(S) \ge \iint_D 1\,dA = \text{Area}(D)$. Equality holds only when $f_x = f_y = 0$ everywhere — i.e., when the surface is a flat, horizontal plane.

E12.1

Find a parametric representation for the half of the hyperboloid $4x^2 - 4y^2 - z^2 = 4$ that satisfies $x \ge 0$.
Solution

Solve for the variable that's convenient. Rewrite the equation: $$4x^2 = 4 + 4y^2 + z^2 \implies x^2 = 1 + y^2 + \frac{z^2}{4}$$ Since $x \ge 0$, take the positive square root: $x = \sqrt{1 + y^2 + z^2/4}$.

Use $y$ and $z$ as parameters. Let $u = y$ and $v = z$. Then: $$\vec{\gamma}(y,z) = \left\langle \sqrt{1 + y^2 + \frac{z^2}{4}},\;\; y,\;\; z\right\rangle, \quad (y,z)\in\mathbb{R}^2$$

Verification: plug back in: $4(1+y^2+z^2/4) - 4y^2 - z^2 = 4 + 4y^2 + z^2 - 4y^2 - z^2 = 4$ ✓

E12.2

Find the surface area of the part of the sphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 36$ that lies between $z = 0$ and $z = 3\sqrt{3}$.
Solution

Parametrize the sphere of radius 6. $$\vec{\gamma}(\phi,\theta) = \langle 6\sin\phi\cos\theta,\; 6\sin\phi\sin\theta,\; 6\cos\phi\rangle$$ where $\phi \in [0,\pi]$ and $\theta \in [0,2\pi]$.

Find the $\phi$ range. We need $z = 6\cos\phi \ge 0$ (i.e. $\cos\phi \ge 0$) and $z \le 3\sqrt{3}$. The condition $z \ge 0$ gives $\phi \le \pi/2$. For the upper bound: $6\cos\phi = 3\sqrt{3}$ gives $\cos\phi = \sqrt{3}/2$, so $\phi = \pi/6$. Thus $\phi\in[\pi/6, \pi/2]$.

The cross product magnitude. For a sphere of radius $a$, $|\vec{\gamma}_\phi\times\vec{\gamma}_\theta| = a^2\sin\phi$. Here $a = 6$, so $|\vec{\gamma}_\phi\times\vec{\gamma}_\theta| = 36\sin\phi$.

Compute area. $$A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/2} 36\sin\phi\,d\phi\,d\theta = 2\pi\cdot 36\Bigl[-\cos\phi\Bigr]_{\pi/6}^{\pi/2}$$ $$= 72\pi\left(-\cos\frac{\pi}{2} + \cos\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = 72\pi\left(0 + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right) = \boxed{36\sqrt{3}\,\pi}$$

E12.3

Find the area of the surface $z = \frac{2}{3}(x^{3/2} + y^{3/2})$ over the square $[0,1]\times[0,1]$.
Solution

Use the graph formula. $f(x,y) = \frac{2}{3}(x^{3/2}+y^{3/2})$, so $f_x = x^{1/2}$ and $f_y = y^{1/2}$.

Alternatively, compute tangent vectors directly. $\vec{\gamma}(x,y) = \langle x, y, f\rangle$, so $\vec{\gamma}_x = \langle 1, 0, x^{1/2}\rangle$ and $\vec{\gamma}_y = \langle 0, 1, y^{1/2}\rangle$.

Cross product: $\vec{\gamma}_x\times\vec{\gamma}_y = \langle (0)(y^{1/2})-(x^{1/2})(1),\; (x^{1/2})(0)-(1)(y^{1/2}),\; (1)(1)-(0)(0)\rangle = \langle -x^{1/2}, -y^{1/2}, 1\rangle$.

Magnitude: $|\vec{\gamma}_x\times\vec{\gamma}_y| = \sqrt{x + y + 1}$.

Integrate. $$A = \int_0^1\int_0^1\sqrt{x+y+1}\,dx\,dy$$ Inner integral: let $u = x+y+1$, $du = dx$: $$\int_0^1\sqrt{x+y+1}\,dx = \Bigl[\tfrac{2}{3}(x+y+1)^{3/2}\Bigr]_0^1 = \tfrac{2}{3}\bigl[(y+2)^{3/2} - (y+1)^{3/2}\bigr]$$ Outer integral: $$A = \frac{2}{3}\int_0^1\bigl[(y+2)^{3/2}-(y+1)^{3/2}\bigr]dy = \frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{2}{5}\Bigl[(y+2)^{5/2}-(y+1)^{5/2}\Bigr]_0^1$$ $$= \frac{4}{15}\Bigl[(3^{5/2}-2^{5/2})-(2^{5/2}-1)\Bigr] = \frac{4}{15}\bigl[3^{5/2} - 2\cdot 2^{5/2} + 1\bigr]$$ $$= \frac{4}{15}\bigl[9\sqrt{3} - 8\sqrt{2} + 1\bigr] = \boxed{\dfrac{4(9\sqrt{3}-8\sqrt{2}+1)}{15}}$$

E12.4

Find the area of the surface $z = xy$ that lies inside the cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = 1$.
Solution

Set up the area integral. $f(x,y) = xy$, so $f_x = y$ and $f_y = x$. The area element is: $$\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2} = \sqrt{1+x^2+y^2}$$

Switch to polar. The domain $D$ is the unit disk $x^2+y^2 \le 1$. With $r^2 = x^2+y^2$: $$A = \iint_{x^2+y^2\le 1}\sqrt{1+x^2+y^2}\,dA = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\sqrt{1+r^2}\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta$$

Evaluate the $r$ integral. Let $u = 1+r^2$, $du = 2r\,dr$: $$\int_0^1 r\sqrt{1+r^2}\,dr = \frac{1}{2}\int_1^2\sqrt{u}\,du = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\Bigl[u^{3/2}\Bigr]_1^2 = \frac{1}{3}(2\sqrt{2}-1)$$

Multiply by $\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta = 2\pi$. $$A = 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{3}(2\sqrt{2}-1) = \boxed{\dfrac{2\pi(2\sqrt{2}-1)}{3}}$$

Stewart §16.6 — HW06 Problems
HW16.6.2

Identify and sketch the parametric surface given by $\vec{r}(u,v) = \langle u + v,\ 3 - v,\ 1 + 4u + 5v\rangle$. What kind of surface is it?
Solution

Identify the type. Write $x = u+v$, $y = 3-v$, $z = 1+4u+5v$. These are all linear functions of $u$ and $v$ — so the surface is a plane.

Find the equation of the plane. From $y = 3-v$: $v = 3-y$. From $x = u+v$: $u = x-v = x-(3-y) = x-3+y$.

Substitute into the $z$ equation: $$z = 1 + 4(x-3+y) + 5(3-y) = 1 + 4x - 12 + 4y + 15 - 5y = 4 + 4x - y$$ $$\Rightarrow z = 4x - y + 4$$

Or equivalently: $4x - y - z + 4 = 0$. The surface is the plane with normal vector $\langle 4, -1, -1\rangle$.

Key points. At $(u,v)=(0,0)$: $\vec{r} = \langle 0, 3, 1\rangle$. The tangent vectors are $\vec{r}_u = \langle 1,0,4\rangle$ and $\vec{r}_v = \langle 1,-1,5\rangle$, and $\vec{r}_u\times\vec{r}_v = \langle 4,-1,-1\rangle$, confirming the normal.

HW16.6.48

Find the area of the part of the surface $z = 1 + 3x + 2y^2$ that lies above the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(0,1)$, and $(2,1)$.
Solution

Set up the surface area integral. For $z = f(x,y)$: $$A = \iint_D \sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2}\,dA$$ Here $f_x = 3$ and $f_y = 4y$, so: $$\sqrt{1 + 9 + 16y^2} = \sqrt{10 + 16y^2}$$

Describe the region $D$. Triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(0,1)$, $(2,1)$. The edges: $y=0$ is just the point $(0,0)$... Let us verify: the three vertices are $(0,0)$, $(0,1)$, $(2,1)$. The edges are $x=0$ (from $y=0$ to $y=1$), $y=1$ (from $x=0$ to $x=2$), and the hypotenuse from $(0,0)$ to $(2,1)$, which has equation $y = x/2$, i.e. $x = 2y$.

For fixed $y \in [0,1]$, $x$ runs from $0$ to $2y$.

Integrate. $$A = \int_0^1\int_0^{2y}\sqrt{10+16y^2}\,dx\,dy = \int_0^1 2y\sqrt{10+16y^2}\,dy$$

Let $u = 10 + 16y^2$, $du = 32y\,dy$, so $2y\,dy = du/16$. Limits: $y=0 \Rightarrow u=10$, $y=1 \Rightarrow u=26$: $$A = \frac{1}{16}\int_{10}^{26}\sqrt{u}\,du = \frac{1}{16}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\left[u^{3/2}\right]_{10}^{26} = \frac{1}{24}\bigl(26^{3/2} - 10^{3/2}\bigr)$$

$$= \frac{1}{24}\bigl(26\sqrt{26} - 10\sqrt{10}\bigr) = \boxed{\dfrac{26\sqrt{26} - 10\sqrt{10}}{24}}$$

L10 — Green's Theorem
R1

Use Green's Theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C xy\,dx + x^2y^3\,dy$ where $C$ is the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(1,2)$, oriented counterclockwise.
Solution

Compute the curl quantity. $P = xy$, $Q = x^2y^3$. $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 2xy^3 - x$$

Describe $D$. The triangle has vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(1,2)$. The left edge is the $y$-axis ($x=0$), the bottom is $y=0$, and the hypotenuse is $y = 2x$ (from $(0,0)$ to $(1,2)$). For fixed $x\in[0,1]$, $y$ runs from $0$ to $2x$: $$\iint_D(2xy^3-x)\,dA = \int_0^1\int_0^{2x}(2xy^3-x)\,dy\,dx$$

Inner integral. $$\int_0^{2x}(2xy^3-x)\,dy = \Bigl[\frac{xy^4}{2}-xy\Bigr]_0^{2x} = \frac{x(2x)^4}{2} - x(2x) = 8x^5 - 2x^2$$

Outer integral. $$\int_0^1(8x^5-2x^2)\,dx = \Bigl[\frac{8x^6}{6}-\frac{2x^3}{3}\Bigr]_0^1 = \frac{4}{3}-\frac{2}{3} = \boxed{\frac{2}{3}}$$

R2

Use the area formula from Green's Theorem to find the area enclosed by the astroid $x = \cos^3 t$, $y = \sin^3 t$, $t\in[0,2\pi]$. (The area formula: $A = \frac{1}{2}\oint_C x\,dy - y\,dx$.)
Solution

Compute $dx = -3\cos^2 t\sin t\,dt$ and $dy = 3\sin^2 t\cos t\,dt$.

The integrand $x\,dy - y\,dx$: $$= \cos^3 t\cdot 3\sin^2 t\cos t\,dt - \sin^3 t\cdot(-3\cos^2 t\sin t)\,dt$$ $$= 3\cos^4 t\sin^2 t\,dt + 3\cos^2 t\sin^4 t\,dt = 3\cos^2 t\sin^2 t\,dt$$

Apply the identity $\cos^2 t\sin^2 t = \frac{1}{4}\sin^2 2t = \frac{1}{8}(1-\cos 4t)$: $$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}3\cdot\frac{1}{8}(1-\cos 4t)\,dt = \frac{3}{16}\int_0^{2\pi}(1-\cos 4t)\,dt$$ $$= \frac{3}{16}\Bigl[t - \frac{\sin 4t}{4}\Bigr]_0^{2\pi} = \frac{3}{16}\cdot 2\pi = \boxed{\frac{3\pi}{8}}$$

L11 — Curl and Divergence
R3

Let $\vec{F}(x,y,z) = \langle xy^2z,\; x^2yz^2,\; x^2y^2 z\rangle$. Compute $\text{curl}\,\vec{F}$ and $\text{div}\,\vec{F}$. Is $\vec{F}$ conservative?
Solution

Here $P = xy^2z$, $Q = x^2yz^2$, $R = x^2y^2z$.

Divergence: $$\text{div}\,\vec{F} = \partial_x(xy^2z) + \partial_y(x^2yz^2) + \partial_z(x^2y^2z) = y^2z + x^2z^2 + x^2y^2$$

Curl: $$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_x = \partial_y R - \partial_z Q = 2x^2yz - 2x^2yz^2 \cdot\tfrac{1}{z}\cdot z$$ Wait, let's be careful: $\partial_y(x^2y^2z) = 2x^2yz$ and $\partial_z(x^2yz^2) = 2x^2yz$. So $(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_x = 2x^2yz - 2x^2yz = 0$.

$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_y = \partial_z P - \partial_x R = xy^2 - 2xy^2z$.

$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_z = \partial_x Q - \partial_y P = 2xyz^2 - 2xyz$.

$$\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \langle 0,\;\; xy^2(1-2z),\;\; 2xyz(z-1)\rangle$$

Conservative? Since $\text{curl}\,\vec{F} \ne \vec{0}$ in general (e.g., at $z=2$ the $y$-component is $-xy^2\ne 0$), $\vec{F}$ is not conservative.

R4

Verify the identity $\text{div}(\text{curl}\,\vec{F}) = 0$ explicitly for $\vec{F} = \langle xy,\; yz,\; zx\rangle$.
Solution

Compute curl. $P = xy$, $Q = yz$, $R = zx$. $$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_x = \partial_y(zx) - \partial_z(yz) = 0 - y = -y$$ $$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_y = \partial_z(xy) - \partial_x(zx) = 0 - z = -z$$ $$(\text{curl}\,\vec{F})_z = \partial_x(yz) - \partial_y(xy) = 0 - x = -x$$ $$\text{curl}\,\vec{F} = \langle -y,\; -z,\; -x\rangle$$

Compute div of the result. $$\text{div}(\text{curl}\,\vec{F}) = \partial_x(-y) + \partial_y(-z) + \partial_z(-x) = 0 + 0 + 0 = \boxed{0}\;\checkmark$$

L12 — Parametric Surfaces & Area
R5

Find the surface area of the part of the paraboloid $z = x^2 + y^2$ that lies below the plane $z = 4$.
Solution

Set up. $f(x,y) = x^2+y^2$, so $f_x = 2x$ and $f_y = 2y$. The region $D$ is $\{z \le 4\} = \{x^2+y^2 \le 4\}$, the disk of radius 2. $$A = \iint_D\sqrt{1+4x^2+4y^2}\,dA$$

Polar coordinates. $x^2+y^2 = r^2$, $dA = r\,dr\,d\theta$, $r\in[0,2]$: $$A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 \sqrt{1+4r^2}\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^2 r\sqrt{1+4r^2}\,dr$$

Substitution. Let $u = 1+4r^2$, $du = 8r\,dr$. When $r=0$: $u=1$; when $r=2$: $u=17$: $$= 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{8}\int_1^{17}\sqrt{u}\,du = \frac{\pi}{4}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\Bigl[u^{3/2}\Bigr]_1^{17} = \frac{\pi}{6}(17^{3/2}-1) = \boxed{\dfrac{\pi(17\sqrt{17}-1)}{6}}$$

R6

Parametrize the cone $z = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$, $0 \le z \le 3$. Compute $\vec{\gamma}_r\times\vec{\gamma}_\theta$ and find the surface area.
Solution

Parametrize using polar. Let $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $z = r$ (since $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2} = r$). So: $$\vec{\gamma}(r,\theta) = \langle r\cos\theta,\; r\sin\theta,\; r\rangle,\quad r\in[0,3],\;\theta\in[0,2\pi]$$

Compute tangent vectors. $$\vec{\gamma}_r = \langle\cos\theta,\;\sin\theta,\;1\rangle, \qquad \vec{\gamma}_\theta = \langle -r\sin\theta,\;r\cos\theta,\;0\rangle$$

Cross product. $$\vec{\gamma}_r\times\vec{\gamma}_\theta = \begin{vmatrix}\vec{i}&\vec{j}&\vec{k}\\\cos\theta&\sin\theta&1\\-r\sin\theta&r\cos\theta&0\end{vmatrix}$$ $$= \vec{i}(0-r\cos\theta) - \vec{j}(0-(-r\sin\theta)) + \vec{k}(r\cos^2\theta+r\sin^2\theta)$$ $$= \langle -r\cos\theta,\;-r\sin\theta,\;r\rangle$$

Magnitude. $|\vec{\gamma}_r\times\vec{\gamma}_\theta| = r\sqrt{\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta+1} = r\sqrt{2}$.

Area. $$A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^3 r\sqrt{2}\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\sqrt{2}\cdot\frac{9}{2} = \boxed{9\sqrt{2}\,\pi}$$
