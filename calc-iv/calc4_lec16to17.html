STUDY GUIDE
The Big Picture
Why complex numbers?

You already know that some polynomials have no real roots — $x^2 + 1 = 0$ has none. Complex numbers are the minimal extension of $\mathbb{R}$ that makes every polynomial splittable (the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra). But the surprise is how much geometric structure comes along for free: multiplication rotates and scales, and the exponential function ties together oscillation and growth in a single object.

The Arithmetic of $\mathbb{C}$
Definition and notation

A complex number is a pair $(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ written $z = x + iy$, where $i^2 = -1$. The real part is $\text{Re}(z) = x$ and imaginary part $\text{Im}(z) = y$.

Four operations

Add: $(x+iy) + (u+iv) = (x+u) + i(y+v)$ — just like vectors.

Multiply: $(x+iy)(u+iv) = (xu - yv) + i(xv + yu)$ — use $i^2 = -1$.

Conjugate: $\bar{z} = x - iy$. Flips the imaginary part.

Divide: Multiply numerator and denominator by $\bar{w}$: $\dfrac{z}{w} = \dfrac{z\bar{w}}{|w|^2}$.

Key identity: $z\bar{z} = x^2 + y^2 = |z|^2$. This is how division works — $\bar{z}$ is the "multiplicative partner" that kills the imaginary part.

The modulus

The modulus (absolute value) is $|z| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$. Geometrically it is the distance from $z$ to the origin in the complex plane. Key properties:

Modulus identities

$|zw| = |z||w|$ — modulus is multiplicative

$|\bar{z}| = |z|$ — conjugate doesn't change distance

$z\bar{z} = |z|^2$ — use this constantly for division

Triangle inequality
$$|z + w| \leq |z| + |w|$$

and the reverse: $|z - w| \geq \bigl||z| - |w|\bigr|$. Same as vectors — the direct path is always shortest.

Polar Form — The Key Idea
Cartesian vs. polar

Every complex number $z = x + iy \neq 0$ lives at a point in the plane. Instead of describing that point by its horizontal and vertical coordinates $(x, y)$, you can describe it by how far it is from the origin and which direction it points. That's polar form:

$$z = r e^{i\theta}, \quad r = |z| > 0, \quad \theta = \arg(z)$$

Reading each term: $r$ is the distance from $z$ to the origin — always positive, just $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$. $\theta$ is the angle from the positive real axis to the line pointing at $z$, measured counterclockwise in radians. $e^{i\theta}$ is the direction, sitting on the unit circle at that angle. Together $r \cdot e^{i\theta}$ scales that direction out to distance $r$.

The conversion back is: $x = r\cos\theta$ and $y = r\sin\theta$. The angle $\theta$ is only defined up to adding multiples of $2\pi$ — rotating a full circle lands you in the same place.

Why polar form is powerful: multiplication becomes trivial.

Multiplication in polar
$$r e^{i\theta} \cdot s e^{i\psi} = rs\, e^{i(\theta + \psi)}$$

Reading each term: $r$ and $s$ are the two moduli — they multiply like ordinary positive numbers. $\theta$ and $\psi$ are the two angles — they simply add. So multiplying two complex numbers scales distances and adds angles. This is why complex multiplication is a rotation + scaling, and why it's so much easier in polar than in rectangular.

How to actually find $\theta$ — converting to polar

The argument $\theta$ is the angle from the positive real axis to $z$, counterclockwise. The formula $\theta = \arctan(y/x)$ gives you a starting point, but has a critical problem: $\arctan$ only returns angles in $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$ — it can't see the left half of the plane. The number $-1 - i$ and the number $1 + i$ have the same ratio $y/x = 1$, but completely different angles ($-3\pi/4$ vs. $\pi/4$). You must use the quadrant to fix it.

The full method: four steps

1. Draw it. Plot $(x, y)$ and identify the quadrant. This is the most important step — do it every time.

2. Find the reference angle $\alpha = \arctan\!\left(\dfrac{|y|}{|x|}\right)$, using absolute values. This always gives $\alpha \in [0, \pi/2]$.

3. Adjust for quadrant:

Quadrant I ($x>0, y>0$): $\theta = \alpha$

Quadrant II ($x<0, y>0$): $\theta = \pi - \alpha$

Quadrant III ($x<0, y<0$): $\theta = -\pi + \alpha$

Quadrant IV ($x>0, y<0$): $\theta = -\alpha$

4. On the axes (don't use $\arctan$, just read it off):

Positive real axis: $\theta = 0$. Positive imaginary: $\theta = \pi/2$. Negative real: $\theta = \pi$. Negative imaginary: $\theta = -\pi/2$.

Worked examples

$z = 1 + i$: quadrant I, $\alpha = \arctan(1/1) = \pi/4$, so $\theta = \pi/4$. Answer: $\sqrt{2}\,e^{i\pi/4}$.

$z = -1 + i$: quadrant II, $\alpha = \arctan(1/1) = \pi/4$, so $\theta = \pi - \pi/4 = 3\pi/4$. Answer: $\sqrt{2}\,e^{i3\pi/4}$.

$z = -1 - i$: quadrant III, $\alpha = \pi/4$, so $\theta = -\pi + \pi/4 = -3\pi/4$. Answer: $\sqrt{2}\,e^{-i3\pi/4}$.

$z = 1 - i$: quadrant IV, $\alpha = \pi/4$, so $\theta = -\pi/4$. Answer: $\sqrt{2}\,e^{-i\pi/4}$.

$z = \sqrt{3} + i$: quadrant I, $\alpha = \arctan(1/\sqrt{3}) = \pi/6$, so $\theta = \pi/6$. $r = \sqrt{3+1} = 2$. Answer: $2e^{i\pi/6}$.

Shortcut: skip $\arctan$ when you can

Most exam problems use angles that sit at multiples of $\pi/6$ or $\pi/4$. If you recognize the ratio of $|y|/|x|$, you can read $\alpha$ off directly:

$|y|/|x| = 1$ → $\alpha = \pi/4$ (equal parts, 45°)

$|y|/|x| = 1/\sqrt{3}$ → $\alpha = \pi/6$ (30°)

$|y|/|x| = \sqrt{3}$ → $\alpha = \pi/3$ (60°)

If $x = 0$ or $y = 0$, you're on an axis — don't use $\arctan$ at all, just read the angle directly.

Euler's Formula — proved from power series

This is not just a definition — it is a theorem. Recall the real power series:

$$e^x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}, \quad \cos x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}, \quad \sin x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$$

Plug in $i\theta$ for $x$ in the exponential and separate even/odd terms:

$$e^{i\theta} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(i\theta)^n}{n!} = \underbrace{\sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k \theta^{2k}}{(2k)!}}_{\cos\theta} + i\underbrace{\sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k \theta^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!}}_{\sin\theta}$$
Euler's Formula
$$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

Special case $\theta = \pi$: $e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$. (Famous, but less useful than the general form.)

de Moivre's Theorem

Direct corollary of Euler's formula and the polar multiplication rule:

$$(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta)$$

This is machine for expanding $\cos(n\theta)$ and $\sin(n\theta)$ in terms of powers of $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$. Expand the left side with the binomial theorem, then match real/imaginary parts.

de Moivre for n=3 (HW08.5)
$$(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^3 = \cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta\sin^2\theta + i(3\cos^2\theta\sin\theta - \sin^3\theta)$$

So $\cos 3\theta = \cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta\sin^2\theta = 4\cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta$ (using $\sin^2 = 1 - \cos^2$), and $\sin 3\theta = 3\sin\theta - 4\sin^3\theta$.

Complex Exponential and Trig Functions
Extending $e^z$, $\cos z$, $\sin z$ to complex inputs

The power series definitions work for complex $z$ too (they converge absolutely for all $z \in \mathbb{C}$):

$$e^z = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{z^n}{n!}, \quad \cos z = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n z^{2n}}{(2n)!}, \quad \sin z = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$$

These agree with the real versions when $z$ is real, and they extend to all of $\mathbb{C}$. The key formulas remain:

$$\cos z = \frac{e^{iz} + e^{-iz}}{2}, \qquad \sin z = \frac{e^{iz} - e^{-iz}}{2i}$$
Warning: $|\cos z| \leq 1$ is FALSE for complex $z$

For real $\theta$, $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$ always. But $\cos(iy) = \cosh(y) = \frac{e^y + e^{-y}}{2}$, which grows without bound as $y \to \infty$. The bound $|\cos z| \leq 1$ is a purely real phenomenon.

Useful identities to know cold
$|e^{i\theta}|$$= 1$ for all real $\theta$ — stays on the unit circle
$\overline{e^{i\theta}}$$= e^{-i\theta}$ — conjugate reverses angle
$1/e^{i\theta}$$= e^{-i\theta}$ — inverse reverses angle
$e^{z+w}$$= e^z e^w$ — exponential law holds for complex $z, w$
$e^{i\theta}$ periodicperiod $2\pi$: $e^{i(\theta + 2\pi)} = e^{i\theta}$
$\text{Re}(e^{i\theta})$$= \cos\theta$, $\;\text{Im}(e^{i\theta}) = \sin\theta$
What is a complex function?
Functions $f: D \to \mathbb{C}$

A complex function takes a complex number as input and returns a complex number. Since $\mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{R}^2$, you can think of it as a function $\mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ — but the complex structure imposes much stronger constraints.

Write $f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y)$ where $u, v: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ are the real and imaginary parts. Complex differentiability will link $u$ and $v$ in a rigid way (Cauchy-Riemann equations — coming in the next few lectures).

Examples of complex functions

Polynomials: $f(z) = z^n$, $f(z) = z^2 + 2z + 1$

Exponential: $e^z$ — well-defined on all of $\mathbb{C}$

Trig: $\sin z$, $\cos z$ — via power series, defined on all $\mathbb{C}$

Non-example: $\sqrt{z}$ on all of $\mathbb{C}$

$\sqrt{z}$ is not well-defined as a function on all of $\mathbb{C}$: $z = 1$ has $\sqrt{1} = \pm 1$, two values. You must make a choice, but any choice creates a discontinuity somewhere — a branch cut.

Branch Cuts
Making $\sqrt{z}$ a function — the branch cut construction

The problem: going around the origin once ($\theta: 0 \to 2\pi$), the argument $\theta/2$ goes from $0$ to $\pi$, not back to $0$. We pick up a sign flip. This is the "branch point" at $z = 0$.

Fix: Remove the negative real axis from the domain. Define $D = \mathbb{C} \setminus (-\infty, 0]$ and restrict $\theta \in (-\pi, \pi)$ (the "principal branch"). Then:

$$f(z) = \sqrt{r}\, e^{i\theta/2}, \quad z = re^{i\theta}, \quad \theta \in (-\pi, \pi)$$
Why this works

On $D$, $\theta$ is a single-valued continuous function of $z$ (no full loops around origin possible), so $f$ is single-valued and continuous. The cut $(-\infty, 0]$ is called the branch cut.

Different choices of branch cut give different (but equally valid) functions. The branch cut is conventional, not intrinsic.

Intuition

Imagine the complex plane as a Riemann surface: $\sqrt{z}$ actually lives on a two-sheeted surface over $\mathbb{C}$. The branch cut is where you "glue" the two sheets together. Cutting $(-\infty, 0]$ and restricting to one sheet gives the principal branch.

Complex Differentiability
The definition — same formula, much stronger

For $f: D \to \mathbb{C}$ with $D \subset \mathbb{C}$ open, $f$ is complex differentiable at $z_0 \in D$ if:

$$f'(z_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0)}{h}$$

exists, where $h \to 0$ through complex values — from any direction. This is a much stronger condition than real differentiability!

Why it's stronger than real differentiability

In $\mathbb{R}^2$, $h$ can approach $0$ along two independent axes. In $\mathbb{C}$, $h$ is a complex number, and the limit must be the same along every approach direction (horizontal, vertical, diagonal, spiral, …). This one requirement forces $u$ and $v$ to satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations $\partial_x u = \partial_y v$ and $\partial_y u = -\partial_x v$.

Examples: differentiable functions
$f(z) = z^k$

Same power rule: $f'(z) = kz^{k-1}$. Proof: exact same algebra as real case — the binomial theorem works over $\mathbb{C}$.

$f(z) = e^z$

$(e^z)' = e^z$. The power series differentiates term-by-term (justified by uniform convergence), giving back itself.

$f(z) = \sin z$, $\cos z$

$(\sin z)' = \cos z$, $(\cos z)' = -\sin z$. Same formulas as real calculus, proved by differentiating the power series.

Non-examples: nowhere or barely differentiable
$f(z) = \bar{z}$ — nowhere complex differentiable

Try the difference quotient — since $f(z) = \bar{z}$, we have $f(z_0+h) = \overline{z_0+h} = \bar{z}_0 + \bar{h}$:

$$\frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)}{h} = \frac{(\bar{z}_0+\bar{h})-\bar{z}_0}{h} = \frac{\bar{h}}{h}$$

If $h = t \in \mathbb{R}$: $\bar{h}/h = t/t = 1$. If $h = it$: $\bar{h}/h = -it/it = -1$. Different limits $\Rightarrow$ limit doesn't exist. So $\bar{z}$ is nowhere complex differentiable.

Geometrically: conjugation reflects across the real axis, which is orientation-reversing. Complex differentiable maps must be orientation-preserving.

$f(z) = |z|^2$ — differentiable only at $z = 0$

$$\frac{|z_0+h|^2 - |z_0|^2}{h} = \frac{(z_0+h)\cdot\overline{(z_0+h)}\; -\; z_0\bar{z}_0}{h} = \bar{z}_0 + z_0\cdot\frac{\bar{h}}{h} + \bar{h}$$

Working this out: the limit is $\bar{z_0} + z_0$ if we approach along $\mathbb{R}$, but only gives a consistent answer when $z_0 = 0$. So $|z|^2$ is differentiable at exactly one point.

Analyticity — The Foundational Theorem
What "analytic" means and why it matters

We say $f: D \to \mathbb{C}$ is complex analytic (also called holomorphic) on an open set $D$ if it is complex differentiable at every point of $D$ — not just at isolated points, but everywhere on the domain simultaneously. This is the central object of the course from here on out.

The word "analytic" hints at something deeper than just having a derivative. In real analysis, a function is called analytic if it equals its own Taylor series in a neighborhood of every point — that is, if it can be written as a convergent power series locally. These are the "nicest" real functions, a small and special subclass. What makes complex analysis extraordinary is the following theorem, which your lecture states as a foundational fact:

Foundational Theorem

$f$ is complex differentiable on $D$ $\;\Longleftrightarrow\;$ $f$ equals a convergent power series near every point $z_0 \in D$:

$$f(z) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n (z - z_0)^n$$

In other words, in $\mathbb{C}$, having one derivative is the same as having infinitely many derivatives, which is the same as being a power series. These three conditions — which are genuinely different in real analysis — collapse to a single equivalence class in complex analysis.

To appreciate why this is shocking, compare it to the real case. The function $f(x) = e^{-1/x^2}$ (defined as $0$ at $x=0$) is infinitely differentiable at every real point, but every derivative at $0$ equals zero — so its Taylor series is identically zero, not $f$ itself. It is $C^\infty$ but not analytic. In $\mathbb{C}$, this pathology cannot occur. Once you impose complex differentiability, the function is forced to behave perfectly everywhere at once — it cannot "hide" information that its derivatives don't reveal.

The real vs. complex hierarchy

Real: differentiable $\subsetneq$ twice differentiable $\subsetneq \cdots \subsetneq C^\infty \supsetneq$ analytic. These are strictly nested, genuinely different classes.

Complex: once differentiable $=$ $C^\infty$ $=$ analytic. The complex structure is so rigid that a single derivative propagates all the way up.

This rigidity is what makes complex analysis so powerful — and so different in character from anything you've seen in real calculus. Later results like Cauchy's integral formula, the residue theorem, and the identity theorem all flow directly from this foundational collapse.

Meromorphic Functions and Poles
Extending the picture: what if analytic fails at isolated points?

Analytic functions are well-behaved everywhere on their domain. But many important functions — rational functions, $\tan z$, the gamma function $\Gamma(z)$ — are analytic almost everywhere, with a finite number of "bad" points sprinkled in. The question is: what does the failure look like, and how bad can it get?

It turns out the failures come in two very different flavors. The first and nicer flavor is a pole. Intuitively, a pole at $z_0$ means $f(z)$ blows up to infinity as $z \to z_0$, but it does so in a controlled, predictable way — like $1/z$ blowing up at $0$. The function is still "essentially" a power series near $z_0$, just with a finite number of negative-power terms prepended.

A function is called meromorphic on $D$ if it is analytic on all of $D$ except at a discrete set of isolated poles — points with no accumulation point in $D$. Meromorphic functions are the next best thing to analytic: they are analytic everywhere except at isolated, well-understood singularities.

Pole of order $n$ at $z_0$ — formal definition

$z_0$ is a pole of order $n \geq 1$ if $f$ can be written near $z_0$ as:

$$f(z) = \frac{g(z)}{(z-z_0)^n}$$

Reading each term: $z_0$ is the bad point — the location of the pole. $n$ is the order — how fast the blow-up happens ($n=1$ is the mildest). $g(z)$ is the "well-behaved part" — it's analytic near $z_0$ and nonzero there, so it's not causing any trouble. The entire singularity comes from dividing by $(z - z_0)^n$, which sends the denominator to zero as $z \to z_0$.

Equivalently, $(z-z_0)^n f(z)$ extends to an analytic function at $z_0$ — you can "cancel" the pole by multiplying through by exactly the right power. A pole of order 1 is called a simple pole; order 2 a double pole, and so on.

Worked examples — identifying poles

$f(z) = \dfrac{1}{z}$ — simple pole (order 1) at $z = 0$. Here $g(z) = 1$ (analytic, nonzero at $0$) and $n = 1$. As $z \to 0$, $|f(z)| \to \infty$.

$f(z) = \dfrac{1}{z^2}$ — double pole (order 2) at $z = 0$. Same structure, but now $n = 2$, so the blow-up is faster: $|f(z)| \sim 1/r^2$ as $r \to 0$, compared to $1/r$ for a simple pole.

$f(z) = \dfrac{1}{z^2 - 1} = \dfrac{1}{(z-1)(z+1)}$ — two simple poles, one at $z = 1$ and one at $z = -1$. Near $z = 1$: write $f(z) = \frac{1/(z+1)}{z-1}$; the numerator $1/(z+1)$ is analytic and nonzero at $z=1$, so it plays the role of $g(z)$. Same reasoning at $z = -1$.

$f(z) = \tan z = \dfrac{\sin z}{\cos z}$ — simple poles at every zero of $\cos z$, i.e., at $z = \tfrac{\pi}{2} + k\pi$ for $k \in \mathbb{Z}$. At each such point $\sin z \neq 0$, so $g(z) = \sin z$ is nonzero there, confirming simple poles.

Detecting poles: the blow-up test

$z_0$ is a pole of $f$ $\;\Longleftrightarrow\;$ $|f(z)| \to \infty$ as $z \to z_0$ from every direction uniformly. The "from every direction" part is what distinguishes a pole from an essential singularity — at a pole, the blow-up is direction-independent.

To find the order of a pole at $z_0$: find the smallest $n$ such that $\lim_{z \to z_0}(z - z_0)^n f(z)$ exists and is nonzero. That limit is $g(z_0)$.

The second flavor of failure is far worse: an essential singularity. Near an essential singularity the function doesn't blow up cleanly — it oscillates wildly and takes values arbitrarily close to every complex number simultaneously. The function $e^{1/z}$ near $z = 0$ is the canonical example. There is no way to "cancel" this singularity, and no sense in which the function has a finite limit or a clean blow-up rate.

Poles vs. essential singularities — with example

Pole ($1/z$ at $0$): Approach from any direction — along the real axis, the imaginary axis, a diagonal — and $|f(z)| \to \infty$ every time. The blow-up is predictable and uniform in direction. You can "remove" it by multiplying by $z$.

Essential singularity ($e^{1/z}$ at $0$): Approach along the positive real axis ($z = t, t \to 0^+$): $e^{1/t} \to \infty$. Approach along the negative real axis ($z = -t$): $e^{-1/t} \to 0$. Approach along the imaginary axis ($z = it$): $e^{-i/t}$, which has modulus $1$ but oscillates infinitely fast. The function doesn't "want" to be any particular value — by the Casorati-Weierstrass theorem, near $z = 0$ it gets arbitrarily close to every complex number. It is completely untameable.

The full hierarchy of complex functions

With these definitions in place, complex functions sort themselves into a clean classification based on where and how badly analyticity can fail:

Classification

Entire: analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$, no singularities anywhere. The "best" functions. Examples: polynomials, $e^z$, $\sin z$, $\cos z$.

Meromorphic: analytic except at isolated poles. Still very well-behaved — the singularities are understood and removable in a sense. Examples: rational functions, $\tan z$, $1/\sin z$, the gamma function $\Gamma(z)$.

Analytic on a proper domain: can only be defined on a subset of $\mathbb{C}$, often because a branch cut is needed. Examples: $\log z$, $\sqrt{z}$, $z^{1/3}$.

Not complex analytic: exist as functions $\mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ but lose all complex structure. Examples: $\bar{z}$, $|z|^2$, $|z|$, $\text{Re}(z)$.

The reason this hierarchy matters: most of the powerful theorems of complex analysis — Cauchy's theorem, the residue theorem, conformal mapping — apply to meromorphic functions or narrower. Knowing where a function sits in this hierarchy tells you immediately which tools you can use on it.

Problem 1 — Computing high powers of complex numbers
Background

This lives in §1 of the complex numbers chapter. The central idea: multiplying in polar means multiplying moduli and adding angles. That makes powers trivial.

Lecture example

$i^4 = (e^{i\pi/2})^4 = e^{i2\pi} = 1$. Every power of $i$ cycles with period 4.

Exam trap

Students try to expand $(1+i)^8$ by hand. That is eight nested multiplications. Never do it. The whole point of polar form is to avoid this. If you find yourself distributing more than once, you picked the wrong method.

Outlier: what if the angle doesn't divide evenly?

$(1+i)^7$: the angle is $7\pi/4$, so the answer is $(\sqrt{2})^7 e^{i7\pi/4} = 8\sqrt{2}(\cos(7\pi/4) + i\sin(7\pi/4)) = 8\sqrt{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}(1-i) = 8(1-i)$. You still get a clean answer — read $\cos$ and $\sin$ of the final angle off the unit circle.

How to approach any "compute $z^n$" problem
Step-by-step method

1. Find $r = |z|$ — just $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$.

2. Find $\theta = \arg z$ — use the quadrant to get the right angle. Common values: $\pi/6$ (30°), $\pi/4$ (45°), $\pi/3$ (60°), $\pi/2$ (90°).

3. Write $z = re^{i\theta}$.

4. Raise: $z^n = r^n e^{in\theta}$.

5. Reduce $n\theta \pmod{2\pi}$ if needed, then read off $\cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta)$ from the unit circle or leave in exponential form.

ProblemCompute $(1+i)^8$. Give your answer in the form $a + bi$.
Polar form of $1+i$

$r = |1+i| = \sqrt{1+1} = \sqrt{2}$. Angle: $1+i$ is in the first quadrant with equal real and imaginary parts, so $\theta = \pi/4$.

$$1+i = \sqrt{2}\,e^{i\pi/4}$$
Raise to the 8th power
$$(1+i)^8 = (\sqrt{2})^8\,e^{i \cdot 8 \cdot \pi/4} = 2^4 \cdot e^{i2\pi} = 16 \cdot 1$$
Answer

$(1+i)^8 = 16$. Real because $8 \cdot \pi/4 = 2\pi$ is a full rotation back to angle $0$.

Problem 2 — Bounding moduli with the triangle inequality
Background

The triangle inequality $|z+w| \leq |z| + |w|$ is the workhorse of complex estimates. It says: the "straight-line" distance is never longer than the two-leg path. Equally important: the reverse triangle inequality $|z-w| \geq \bigl||z|-|w|\bigr|$, used for lower bounds.

Exam trap: forgetting the equality condition

The problem always asks "when does equality hold?" The answer is never just "always." Equality $|a+b| = |a|+|b|$ requires $a = \lambda b$ for some $\lambda \geq 0$ — both vectors must point in the same direction. This uniquely pins down a single point on the circle.

Outlier: the reverse inequality

If you need a lower bound (e.g., show $|z - 3| \geq 1$ for $|z| = 2$), use $|z - 3| \geq |3| - |z| = 3 - 2 = 1$. This trips people up because they reflexively reach for the upper bound form.

Lecture note: modulus of $a+bi$

$|a + bi| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$. Spotting a Pythagorean triple saves time: $3,4,5$ and $6,8,10$ are common on exams. $|6+8i| = 10$ (not $\sqrt{100}$ — just $10$).

How to approach any modulus estimate problem
Step-by-step method

1. Decide: upper or lower bound? Upper → triangle inequality. Lower → reverse triangle inequality.

2. Split $|f(z)|$ into $|A + B| \leq |A| + |B|$ (or $\geq ||A|-|B||$).

3. Plug in the given constraint (e.g., $|z| = 2$) to evaluate $|A|$ and $|B|$ numerically.

4. For equality: $|a+b| = |a|+|b|$ iff $a/b \geq 0$ (same direction). Write $z = \lambda w$ with $\lambda > 0$, combine with $|z| = r$ to pin down $z$ exactly.

ProblemLet $|z| = 2$. Show that $|z + 6 + 8i| \leq 12$. Find all $z$ on this circle where equality holds.
Upper bound

By the triangle inequality: $|z + (6+8i)| \leq |z| + |6+8i|$.

$|6+8i| = \sqrt{36+64} = 10$. So $|z + 6+8i| \leq 2 + 10 = 12$. $\checkmark$

Equality condition

Need $z$ parallel to $6+8i$ with $z = \lambda(6+8i)$, $\lambda \geq 0$, $|z| = 2$.

Unit vector in the $(6,8)$ direction: $\dfrac{6+8i}{10}$. So $z = 2 \cdot \dfrac{6+8i}{10} = \dfrac{6}{5} + \dfrac{8}{5}i$.

Equality holds at exactly one point: $z = \tfrac{6}{5} + \tfrac{8}{5}i$.

Problem 3 — Sketching regions in $\mathbb{C}$
Background

Complex inequalities define regions in the plane $\mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{R}^2$. The skill is translating $z$-language into $(x,y)$-language, simplifying, and recognizing the shape. The three shapes that appear most are: lines, circles/disks, and angular wedges.

Lecture: computing $z^2$ in rectangular

$z^2 = (x+iy)^2 = x^2 - y^2 + 2xyi$, so $\text{Re}(z^2) = x^2 - y^2$ and $\text{Im}(z^2) = 2xy$. These come up constantly — memorize them.

Key algebraic identity for $|z-a|^2$

$|z-a|^2 = (z-a)\overline{(z-a)} = |z|^2 - a\bar z - \bar a z + |a|^2$. Or just expand rectangular: $|z-a|^2 = (x - \text{Re}\,a)^2 + (y - \text{Im}\,a)^2$. This always gives you something to complete the square in.

Exam trap: $|z-a|^2 + |z-b|^2$ — don't panic

This looks complicated but expands cleanly. The cross terms from $(x-a_1)^2 + (x-b_1)^2$ combine to give $2x^2 - 2(a_1+b_1)x + \ldots$ The $x^2$ coefficient is always $2$ (from both squares), which tells you the center is the midpoint of $a$ and $b$.

How to approach any region-sketching problem
Step-by-step method

1. Write $z = x+iy$ and expand the condition entirely into $x$ and $y$.

2. Simplify. Look for the pattern: $x^2+y^2$ = circle, $ax+by = c$ = line, $x^2 - y^2 = c$ = hyperbola/wedge.

3. If it's a circle, complete the square to find center and radius.

4. For wedge-type regions ($|x| > |y|$ or angle conditions), think in polar: $|\theta| < \alpha$ is a wedge.

5. Check the boundary (strict vs. non-strict inequality → open vs. closed region).

3aSketch $\{z \in \mathbb{C} : \text{Re}(z^2) > 0\}$.
Expand

$z = x+iy$, so $z^2 = x^2 - y^2 + 2xyi$. Thus $\text{Re}(z^2) = x^2 - y^2$.

Condition: $x^2 - y^2 > 0 \Leftrightarrow x^2 > y^2 \Leftrightarrow |x| > |y|$.

Region

The region where $|x| > |y|$: two open wedges centered on the real axis, bounded by the lines $y = x$ and $y = -x$. In polar: $|\theta| < \pi/4$ or $|\theta - \pi| < \pi/4$. The boundary lines are excluded (strict inequality).

3bSketch $\{z \in \mathbb{C} : |z-1|^2 + |z+1|^2 \leq 8\}$.
Expand
$$|z-1|^2 + |z+1|^2 = (x-1)^2+y^2 + (x+1)^2+y^2 = 2x^2+2y^2+2$$

Condition: $2x^2+2y^2+2 \leq 8 \Rightarrow x^2+y^2 \leq 3$.

Region

Closed disk centered at the origin, radius $\sqrt{3}$. The midpoint of the two base points $-1$ and $1$ is $0$ — consistent with the general principle that $|z-a|^2 + |z-b|^2 = C$ is always a circle centered at the midpoint of $a$ and $b$.

Problem 4 — Apollonius circles
Background

The Apollonius circle is the locus of points with a fixed ratio of distances to two fixed points. It generalizes the perpendicular bisector (ratio 1) to a family of circles.

General principle from the chapter

$|z - a| = \rho|z - b|$ describes:

  • A line when $\rho = 1$ (the perpendicular bisector of $ab$)
  • A circle when $\rho \neq 1$ (the Apollonius circle)

The circle is always located on the side of $b$ (the reference point) when $\rho > 1$, meaning $z$ is closer to $b$ than to $a$.

Outlier: what happens as $\rho \to \infty$?

As $\rho \to \infty$, the Apollonius circles converge to the single point $b$. As $\rho \to 0$, they converge to $a$. The family of Apollonius circles for all $\rho > 0$ partitions the plane (minus the two base points) into non-intersecting circles — this is the basis of Möbius transformations.

Exam trap: completing the square

After squaring both sides you get a quadratic in $x$ and $y$. You must complete the square to identify center and radius. Leaving it as $3x^2 - 8x + 3y^2 + 4 = 0$ is not a final answer.

How to approach Apollonius circle problems
Step-by-step method

1. Is $\rho = 1$? Then it's the perpendicular bisector of the two base points — done.

2. If $\rho \neq 1$: square both sides: $|z-a|^2 = \rho^2|z-b|^2$.

3. Expand using $z = x+iy$: get a quadratic in $x, y$.

4. Group: $(1-\rho^2)x^2 + (1-\rho^2)y^2 + \ldots = 0$. Divide by $(1-\rho^2)$.

5. Complete the square in both $x$ and $y$. Read off center and radius.

ProblemDescribe the set $\{z \in \mathbb{C} : |z| = \rho|z - 1|\}$ for $\rho = 1$ and $\rho = 2$. Identify the geometric object in each case.
$\rho = 1$: $|z| = |z-1|$

Equal distance from $0$ and $1$. This is the perpendicular bisector of the segment from $0$ to $1$, which passes through the midpoint $\tfrac{1}{2}$ perpendicularly to the real axis.

Answer: the vertical line $\text{Re}(z) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

$\rho = 2$: square both sides

$|z|^2 = 4|z-1|^2$: $\;x^2+y^2 = 4(x-1)^2+4y^2 = 4x^2-8x+4+4y^2$.

$0 = 3x^2 - 8x + 4 + 3y^2 \;\Rightarrow\; x^2 - \tfrac{8}{3}x + y^2 = -\tfrac{4}{3}$.

Complete the square: $\left(x - \tfrac{4}{3}\right)^2 + y^2 = \tfrac{16}{9} - \tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{4}{9}$.

Answer ($\rho = 2$)

A circle centered at $\tfrac{4}{3}$ on the real axis with radius $\tfrac{2}{3}$. Note the center is on the far side of $1$ from $0$ — consistent with $\rho > 1$ meaning $z$ is closer to the reference point $1$.

Problem 5 — de Moivre and multiple-angle formulas
Background

de Moivre's theorem $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta$ converts a hard trigonometry problem (what is $\cos 3\theta$?) into a routine algebra problem (expand a cube and match parts).

Why it works

In polar form, $\cos\theta + i\sin\theta = e^{i\theta}$, so $(e^{i\theta})^n = e^{in\theta} = \cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta$. de Moivre is just the law of exponents, dressed up in trig language.

Exam trap: tracking powers of $i$

When expanding $(a + ib)^3$, the term $(ib)^2 = -b^2$ (not $+b^2$) and $(ib)^3 = -ib^3$ (not $+ib^3$). Missing the sign from $i^2 = -1$ is the most common error. Write out $i^1 = i$, $i^2 = -1$, $i^3 = -i$ explicitly before collecting terms.

Outlier: extracting $\cos n\theta$ vs. $\sin n\theta$

$\cos n\theta$ = real part → has even powers of $\sin\theta$. $\sin n\theta$ = imaginary part → has odd powers of $\sin\theta$. If you're asked only for $\cos 3\theta$, just expand and take the real part — you don't need to finish the imaginary part.

After expanding: always simplify using $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$

$\cos 3\theta = \cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta\sin^2\theta$. Replace $\sin^2\theta = 1 - \cos^2\theta$: get $4\cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta$ — a polynomial in $\cos\theta$ alone. This form is what exams expect.

How to approach de Moivre problems
Step-by-step method

1. Write de Moivre: $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta$.

2. Expand the left side with the binomial theorem. Label $a = \cos\theta$, $b = i\sin\theta$ to keep track.

3. Compute each power of $i$ carefully: $i^2=-1$, $i^3=-i$, $i^4=1$.

4. Separate real and imaginary parts. Real part = $\cos n\theta$, imaginary part = $\sin n\theta$.

5. Simplify using $\sin^2+\cos^2 = 1$ to write in terms of one trig function if possible.

ProblemUse de Moivre's theorem to derive formulas for $\cos 3\theta$ and $\sin 3\theta$ in terms of $\sin\theta$ and $\cos\theta$. Then simplify each to a polynomial in a single trig function.
Expand $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^3$
