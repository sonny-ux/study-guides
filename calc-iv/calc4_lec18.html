The Big Picture
Why do we need the Cauchy-Riemann equations?

Recall from Lecture 17 that a function $f: D \to \mathbb{C}$ is complex differentiable at $z_0$ if the limit $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0+h) - f(z_0)}{h}$ exists, with $h$ approaching $0$ from any direction in $\mathbb{C}$. That limit condition is clean to state but hard to check directly — you'd have to verify it for infinitely many approach directions.

The Cauchy-Riemann equations translate that infinite-direction limit condition into just two partial derivative equations. Instead of checking a limit, you compute four partial derivatives and see whether two relationships hold. This is the main working criterion for analyticity throughout the rest of the course.

Breaking $f$ Into Real and Imaginary Parts
Setup — writing $f = u + iv$ as a function of $(x, y)$

Since any complex number $z = x + iy$ is really just a pair of real numbers, any complex function $f(z)$ can be split into its real and imaginary components:

$$f(z) = u(x,y) + i\,v(x,y)$$

Here $u$ and $v$ are ordinary real-valued functions of two real variables — the kind you know from multivariable calculus. The function $u(x,y)$ is the real part of $f$ at the point $z = x + iy$, and $v(x,y)$ is the imaginary part. For example, if $f(z) = z^2 = (x+iy)^2 = x^2 - y^2 + 2ixy$, then $u(x,y) = x^2 - y^2$ and $v(x,y) = 2xy$.

The key question is: what conditions on $u$ and $v$ make $f$ complex differentiable? The answer comes from approaching the derivative limit in two specific ways.

Deriving the CR Equations — Two Approaches
Horizontal approach ($h = \Delta x \in \mathbb{R}$)

Fix a point $z = x + iy$. To compute the complex derivative, let $h$ approach $0$ along the real axis, so $h = \Delta x$ is a real number. Plugging $f = u + iv$ into the difference quotient:

$$\frac{f(z + \Delta x) - f(z)}{\Delta x} = \frac{u(x+\Delta x,\, y) - u(x,y)}{\Delta x} + i\,\frac{v(x+\Delta x,\, y) - v(x,y)}{\Delta x}$$

As $\Delta x \to 0$, both fractions on the right are just the definition of partial derivatives in $x$. So the horizontal limit gives:

$$f'(z) = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(x,y) + i\,\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}(x,y)$$
Horizontal formula for $f'$
$$f'(z) = \partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v$$

Reading each term: $\partial_x u$ is how fast the real part of $f$ changes as you move right; $\partial_x v$ is how fast the imaginary part changes moving right. Together they assemble the complex derivative.

Vertical approach ($h = i\Delta y$, purely imaginary)

Now let $h$ approach $0$ along the imaginary axis, so $h = i\Delta y$ for real $\Delta y$. Plugging in:

$$\frac{f(z + i\Delta y) - f(z)}{i\Delta y} = \frac{u(x,\, y+\Delta y) - u(x,y)}{i\Delta y} + i\,\frac{v(x,\, y+\Delta y) - v(x,y)}{i\Delta y}$$

Dividing by $i$ is the same as multiplying by $-i$ (since $1/i = -i$). Doing this and taking $\Delta y \to 0$:

$$f'(z) = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}(x,y) - i\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}(x,y)$$
Vertical formula for $f'$
$$f'(z) = \partial_y v - i\,\partial_y u$$

Reading each term: $\partial_y v$ is how fast the imaginary part of $f$ changes as you move upward; $\partial_y u$ is how fast the real part changes moving upward. The $-i$ in front of $\partial_y u$ comes from dividing by $i$ when moving vertically.

Equating the two expressions — the CR equations emerge

If $f$ is complex differentiable at $z$, the limit must be the same no matter how $h \to 0$. In particular, the horizontal result and the vertical result must agree. This gives us two expressions for the same $f'(z)$:

$$\partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v = \partial_y v - i\,\partial_y u$$

Matching real parts and imaginary parts separately (since two complex numbers are equal iff their real and imaginary parts both match):

The Cauchy-Riemann Equations
$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}$$

Reading each equation: The first says the rate of change of the real part moving right must equal the rate of change of the imaginary part moving up. The second says the rate of change of the real part moving up must equal the negative rate of change of the imaginary part moving right. These two constraints tightly couple $u$ and $v$ — you can't change one without forcing specific changes in the other.

The CR equations are a necessary condition for complex differentiability. But the converse also holds (with a mild smoothness assumption): if $u$ and $v$ have continuous first-order partial derivatives that satisfy the CR equations, then $f = u + iv$ is complex differentiable. This gives us a clean if-and-only-if criterion.

Theorem (Gamelin II.3) — The Full Statement

Let $f = u + iv$ be defined on a domain $D \subset \mathbb{C}$. Then $f$ is analytic on $D$ if and only if $u(x,y)$ and $v(x,y)$ have continuous first-order partial derivatives that satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations throughout $D$.

When they hold, the derivative is given by either equivalent formula:

$$f'(z) = \partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v = \partial_y v - i\,\partial_y u$$
CR Equations — two approach directions
Worked Examples — Checking CR
$p(z) = z^2$ — satisfies CR everywhere

Recall $z^2 = (x+iy)^2 = x^2 - y^2 + 2ixy$. So the real and imaginary parts are $u(x,y) = x^2 - y^2$ and $v(x,y) = 2xy$. Compute all four partial derivatives:

Checking CR for $z^2$

$\partial_x u = 2x$, $\quad \partial_y v = 2x$ $\quad \Rightarrow \partial_x u = \partial_y v$ ✓

$\partial_y u = -2y$, $\quad -\partial_x v = -2y$ $\quad \Rightarrow \partial_y u = -\partial_x v$ ✓

Both equations hold for all $(x,y)$, so $z^2$ is analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$. The derivative is $f'(z) = \partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v = 2x + 2iy = 2(x+iy) = 2z$, as expected.

$e^z$ — satisfies CR everywhere

We extend the exponential to complex inputs as $e^z = e^{x+iy} = e^x(\cos y + i\sin y)$. So $u(x,y) = e^x \cos y$ and $v(x,y) = e^x \sin y$. This is one of the most important examples in the course, so it's worth doing carefully.

Checking CR for $e^z$

$\partial_x u = e^x \cos y$, $\quad \partial_y v = e^x \cos y$ $\quad \Rightarrow \partial_x u = \partial_y v$ ✓

$\partial_y u = -e^x \sin y$, $\quad -\partial_x v = -e^x \sin y$ $\quad \Rightarrow \partial_y u = -\partial_x v$ ✓

Both hold everywhere, so $e^z$ is entire (analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$). The derivative: $f'(z) = \partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v = e^x\cos y + ie^x\sin y = e^x(\cos y + i\sin y) = e^z$. So $(e^z)' = e^z$, exactly as in real calculus.

$f(z) = |z|^2$ — fails CR everywhere except the origin

Here $|z|^2 = x^2 + y^2$, so $u(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ and $v(x,y) = 0$ (the function is real-valued). Checking CR:

CR fails for $|z|^2$

$\partial_x u = 2x$, $\quad \partial_y v = 0$ $\quad \Rightarrow \partial_x u = \partial_y v$ requires $2x = 0$, i.e., $x = 0$.

$\partial_y u = 2y$, $\quad -\partial_x v = 0$ $\quad \Rightarrow \partial_y u = -\partial_x v$ requires $2y = 0$, i.e., $y = 0$.

Both equations hold only at $(x, y) = (0, 0)$, i.e., $z = 0$. So $|z|^2$ is complex differentiable at exactly one point and analytic nowhere.

Intuitively: $|z|^2$ is a purely real function (its imaginary part is identically $0$). Any real-valued non-constant function fails CR — a function with no imaginary part can't "rotate" information the way a complex derivative must.

Reading Off $f'(z)$ Once CR Holds
The derivative formula — use whichever is easier

Once you've confirmed the CR equations hold, you can compute $f'(z)$ from either the horizontal or vertical formula — they're equal. In practice, the horizontal formula is usually cleaner:

Derivative formula (horizontal)
$$f'(z) = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + i\,\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}$$

You already computed $\partial_x u$ and $\partial_x v$ while checking CR, so you get the derivative for free.

Exam trap — CR is necessary but you must state smoothness too

Strictly speaking, CR equations are necessary for complex differentiability, but not sufficient on their own. The full theorem requires that $u$ and $v$ also have continuous first-order partial derivatives. In practice, all the functions you'll encounter in this course have continuous partials, so CR is effectively equivalent to analyticity. But on a proof-based problem, state "the partial derivatives are continuous and satisfy CR" to be rigorous.

From Vector to Complex Line Integrals
Motivation — what we already know

Recall from earlier in Calc IV that for a vector field $\vec{F} = (P, Q)$ and a curve $C$, the line integral is $\int_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy$. Complex line integrals are the same idea, but now $f(z)$ is a complex-valued function and $dz = dx + i\,dy$ is a complex differential. The integral $\int_C f(z)\,dz$ is defined by expanding $f\,dz$ into real and imaginary parts and integrating each piece separately — so all the machinery you built for Green's theorem applies directly.

The power of this construction is that it lets us bring the full force of multivariable integral calculus — including Green's theorem — to bear on complex functions. The payoff is Cauchy's theorem, one of the most useful results in the course.

Definition and Computation
$f(z)\,dz$ as a standard line integral

Write $f = u + iv$ and interpret $dz = dx + i\,dy$. Expanding the product:

$$f(z)\,dz = (u + iv)(dx + i\,dy) = (u\,dx - v\,dy) + i(v\,dx + u\,dy)$$

So the complex line integral splits as:

$$\int_C f(z)\,dz = \underbrace{\int_C u\,dx - v\,dy}_{\text{real part}} + i\underbrace{\int_C v\,dx + u\,dy}_{\text{imaginary part}}$$

Comparing with $\int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy$, we see there are really two standard line integrals here: one with $P = u$, $Q = -v$ (real part) and one with $P = v$, $Q = u$ (imaginary part). Both are just real line integrals that you already know how to compute.

The $P, Q$ decomposition (Gamelin notation)

Writing $\int_C f(z)\,dz = \int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy$ where:

$$P = u + iv \qquad Q = -v + iu$$

Note that $P$ and $Q$ are themselves complex-valued — but the integral still makes sense because we evaluate the real and imaginary parts separately.

The parametric formula — how to actually compute

If $\gamma: [0,1] \to \mathbb{C}$ parametrizes the curve $C$ (so $\gamma(t) = \gamma_1(t) + i\gamma_2(t)$ traces out $C$ as $t$ goes from $0$ to $1$), then the complex line integral becomes an ordinary integral over $t$:

Parametric formula
$$\int_C f(z)\,dz = \int_0^1 f(\gamma(t))\cdot\gamma'(t)\,dt$$

Reading each term: $\gamma(t)$ is the point on the curve at parameter value $t$ — plug it into $f$ to evaluate $f$ there. $\gamma'(t) = \gamma_1'(t) + i\gamma_2'(t)$ is the complex velocity of the parametrization — it plays the role of $d\vec{r}/dt$. The product $f(\gamma(t))\cdot\gamma'(t)$ is complex multiplication, which you then integrate over $t$ from $0$ to $1$. The result is a complex number.

Worked example — integrating $f(z) = z$ around the unit circle

Let $C$ be the unit circle $|z| = 1$, parametrized by $\gamma(t) = e^{2\pi i t}$ for $t \in [0,1]$. Then $\gamma'(t) = 2\pi i\, e^{2\pi i t}$. So:

$$\int_C z\,dz = \int_0^1 e^{2\pi it}\cdot 2\pi i\, e^{2\pi it}\,dt = 2\pi i \int_0^1 e^{4\pi it}\,dt = 2\pi i \cdot \left[\frac{e^{4\pi it}}{4\pi i}\right]_0^1 = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{e^{4\pi i} - 1}{4\pi i} = 0$$

The answer is $0$ because $z$ is analytic (Cauchy's theorem, coming up).

The ML Inequality (Estimation)
Bounding a complex line integral by length × max value

In real calculus, you can bound an integral $|\int_a^b h(t)\,dt| \leq \int_a^b |h(t)|\,dt$ — the absolute value of an integral is at most the integral of the absolute value. This holds for complex-valued functions too, and it gives one of the most useful estimation tools in complex analysis.

For a complex line integral, the magnitude of the integrand is $|f(\gamma(t))\cdot\gamma'(t)| = |f(\gamma(t))|\cdot|\gamma'(t)|$. Recall that $|\gamma'(t)| = \sqrt{(\gamma_1')^2 + (\gamma_2')^2}$ is the speed of the parametrization, so $\int_0^1 |\gamma'(t)|\,dt$ is the arc length of $C$ — which we write as $L$ (length of $C$). Putting it all together:

ML Inequality
$$\left|\int_C f(z)\,dz\right| \leq \int_C |f(z)|\,|dz| \leq M \cdot L$$

Reading each term: $M = \max_{z \in C} |f(z)|$ is the maximum modulus of $f$ on the curve $C$ — the worst-case size of $f$ along $C$. $L$ is the arc length of $C$. The inequality says the complex line integral can't be larger in absolute value than $M$ times $L$. This is called the "ML bound."

The ML inequality doesn't compute the integral — it only bounds it. Its main use is in convergence arguments and error estimates, where you need to show that some integral goes to zero as a curve shrinks or a parameter changes.

Cauchy's Theorem — The Central Result
How Green's theorem and CR equations combine

Recall Green's theorem from earlier in Calc IV: if $D$ is a nice bounded domain with boundary $\partial D$, then $\int_{\partial D} P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)\,dA$. The integrand on the right is the "curl" of the vector field $(P, Q)$.

Now apply this to the complex line integral $\int_{\partial D} f(z)\,dz = \int_{\partial D} P\,dx + Q\,dy$ where $P = u + iv$ and $Q = -v + iu$. The curl integrand becomes $\partial_x Q - \partial_y P = \partial_x(-v + iu) - \partial_y(u + iv) = (-\partial_x v + i\,\partial_x u) - (\partial_y u + i\,\partial_y v)$. Regrouping real and imaginary parts: $(-\partial_x v - \partial_y u) + i(\partial_x u - \partial_y v)$. But this is exactly $0$ when the CR equations hold — $\partial_x u = \partial_y v$ kills the imaginary part, and $\partial_y u = -\partial_x v$ kills the real part. The entire double integral vanishes.

Cauchy's Theorem

If $f(z)$ is complex differentiable (analytic) on a domain $D \subset \mathbb{C}$, then for any simple closed curve $C$ bounding a region entirely inside $D$:

$$\oint_C f(z)\,dz = 0$$

What this means: integrating an analytic function around any closed loop gives zero. The loop can be any shape — the result is always the same. This is the complex analogue of "conservative vector fields have zero circulation."

Proof sketch (from lecture notes)

$\oint_{\partial D} f(z)\,dz = \oint_{\partial D} P\,dx + Q\,dy$ (write as real line integral)

$= \iint_D \left(\partial_x Q - \partial_y P\right)dA$ (Green's theorem)

$= 0$ (because CR equations say $\partial_x Q = \partial_y P$)

The CR equations are doing the critical work here — they are precisely the condition that makes the Green's theorem integrand vanish.

Key requirement: $f$ must be analytic on the entire region

Cauchy's theorem requires $f$ to be analytic on the interior of $C$, not just on $C$ itself. If $f$ has a singularity inside $C$ — for example, $f(z) = 1/z$ and $C$ is the unit circle — the theorem fails. In that case the integral is $2\pi i$, not $0$. The singularity inside the curve is exactly what the Cauchy integral formula (below) measures.

Cauchy's theorem — closed contour around analytic region
The Fundamental Theorem — Primitives
The complex antiderivative

In real calculus, if $F'(t) = f(t)$ then $\int_a^b f(t)\,dt = F(b) - F(a)$. The exact same theorem holds in complex analysis, and for exactly the same reason. The key concept is a primitive (also called an antiderivative): a function $F(z)$ is a primitive of $f(z)$ if $F'(z) = f(z)$ everywhere on the domain.

To see why this works: if $F = U + iV$ is a primitive of $f = u + iv$, then $F'(z) = \partial_x U + i\,\partial_x V$. So $\partial_x U = u$ and $\partial_x V = v$. The complex line integral $\int_C f(z)\,dz$ can then be written as $\int_C \nabla U \cdot d\vec{r} + i\int_C \nabla V \cdot d\vec{r}$, and each piece evaluates by the FTC for line integrals to give $U(b) - U(a)$ and $V(b) - V(a)$ respectively.

Fundamental Theorem for Complex Line Integrals

If $F(z)$ is a primitive of $f(z)$ (i.e., $F'(z) = f(z)$) and $C$ is any curve from $a$ to $b$, then:

$$\int_C f(z)\,dz = F(b) - F(a)$$

Reading each term: $F$ is the antiderivative — just like in single-variable calculus. $b$ and $a$ are the endpoints of the curve $C$. The crucial point: the answer depends only on the endpoints, not the path. Any two curves from $a$ to $b$ give the same integral, as long as $f$ is analytic on the region between them.

Worked example — $\int_C z^2\,dz$ along any path

$f(z) = z^2$ has primitive $F(z) = z^3/3$ (check: $F'(z) = z^2$). So for any curve $C$ from $z = 0$ to $z = 1+i$:

$$\int_C z^2\,dz = F(1+i) - F(0) = \frac{(1+i)^3}{3} - 0 = \frac{2i - 2}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{1} = \frac{-2+2i}{3}$$

We compute $(1+i)^3 = (1+i)^2(1+i) = (2i)(1+i) = 2i - 2$. The path shape doesn't matter — only the endpoints count.

Connection to Cauchy's theorem

If $f$ has a primitive $F$ on all of $D$, then for any closed loop $C$ in $D$, the endpoints are the same ($a = b$), so $\int_C f\,dz = F(a) - F(a) = 0$. This is consistent with Cauchy's theorem — but the FTC gives us an explicit formula for open paths too, not just the vanishing result for closed loops.

The Cauchy Integral Formula — What Comes Next
When there's a singularity inside the contour

Cauchy's theorem says the integral around a closed curve is zero when $f$ is analytic everywhere inside. But what if $f$ has an isolated singularity at a single point $z_0$ inside the curve? Then the theorem doesn't apply — and the integral is not zero. Instead, it measures the "strength" of the singularity, and this is the content of the Cauchy integral formula.

The formula arises naturally from Cauchy's theorem by a shrinking-disk argument: the integral around any contour containing $z_0$ equals the integral around a tiny circle centered at $z_0$, which can be computed directly by parametrizing that circle. The result is elegant and is the engine behind all residue calculations later in the course.

Cauchy Integral Formula

Let $f$ be analytic on and inside a simple closed contour $C$ (counterclockwise orientation). If $z_0$ is any point strictly inside $C$, then:

$$\oint_C \frac{f(z)}{z - z_0}\,dz = 2\pi i\, f(z_0)$$

Reading each term: $f(z)/(z - z_0)$ is the integrand — $f$ is the analytic function, $(z - z_0)$ is the denominator that creates the singularity at $z_0$. The left side is the contour integral. The right side is $2\pi i$ times the value of $f$ at the special interior point $z_0$. The formula says: a contour integral of this form doesn't depend on the shape of $C$ at all — it only depends on the value of $f$ at the one interior point $z_0$.

Critical check before applying: is $z_0$ inside $C$?

Before using the Cauchy integral formula, you must verify that $z_0$ is strictly inside the contour. If $z_0$ is outside $C$, then $f(z)/(z-z_0)$ is analytic everywhere inside $C$, and Cauchy's theorem gives $0$ instead. The two cases give completely different answers, so this check is not optional.

How to check: compute $|z_0 - \text{center}|$ and compare to the radius. For a circle $|z - a| = r$, the point $z_0$ is inside iff $|z_0 - a| < r$.

How to apply it — step by step

1. Identify the singularity: rewrite the denominator so it looks like $(\text{something})(z - z_0)$. Factor out constants so the coefficient of $z$ in the denominator is $1$.

2. Check $z_0$ is inside $C$: compute $|z_0 - \text{center of }C|$ and confirm it's less than the radius.

3. Identify $f(z)$: everything in the integrand except the factor $1/(z - z_0)$.

4. Apply the formula: result $= 2\pi i\, f(z_0)$.

Problem 1 — Checking Complex Differentiability via CR
1a$f(z) = 4x^2 + 5x - 4y^2 + 9 + i(8xy + 5y - 1)$

The strategy for all "check whether $f$ is complex differentiable" problems is identical: extract $u$ and $v$, compute all four partial derivatives, then check both CR equations. If both hold everywhere, $f$ is analytic; if they only hold on a subset, $f$ is differentiable only there; if they never hold, $f$ is not differentiable.

Approach

1. Read off $u = 4x^2 + 5x - 4y^2 + 9$ and $v = 8xy + 5y - 1$.

2. Compute: $\partial_x u$, $\partial_y v$, $\partial_y u$, $\partial_x v$.

3. Check CR1: $\partial_x u = \partial_y v$. Check CR2: $\partial_y u = -\partial_x v$.

4. State whether they hold everywhere, nowhere, or on a subset.

5. If analytic, write $f'(z) = \partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v$.

Solution

$u = 4x^2 + 5x - 4y^2 + 9$, $\quad v = 8xy + 5y - 1$

$\partial_x u = 8x + 5$, $\quad \partial_y v = 8x + 5$ $\quad \Rightarrow$ CR1: $\partial_x u = \partial_y v$ ✓ (holds everywhere)

$\partial_y u = -8y$, $\quad -\partial_x v = -(8y) = -8y$ $\quad \Rightarrow$ CR2: $\partial_y u = -\partial_x v$ ✓ (holds everywhere)

Both CR equations hold for all $(x,y)$, so $f$ is analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$ (entire).

Derivative: $f'(z) = \partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v = (8x+5) + i(8y+5) = 8(x+iy) + (5+5i) = 8z + 5(1+i)$.

1b$g(z) = x^3 + 3xy^2 - x + i(y^3 + 3x^2y - y)$

This one requires careful arithmetic. Don't mix up which partial goes with which CR equation. Remember CR1 is $\partial_x u = \partial_y v$ and CR2 is $\partial_y u = -\partial_x v$ — the minus sign in CR2 is where errors happen.

Approach

Same checklist as 1a. The function looks symmetric — check if that symmetry saves CR or breaks it.

Solution

$u = x^3 + 3xy^2 - x$, $\quad v = y^3 + 3x^2y - y$

$\partial_x u = 3x^2 + 3y^2 - 1$, $\quad \partial_y v = 3y^2 + 3x^2 - 1$ $\quad \Rightarrow$ CR1: $\partial_x u = \partial_y v$ ✓ (always equal)

$\partial_y u = 6xy$, $\quad -\partial_x v = -(6xy) = -6xy$ $\quad \Rightarrow$ CR2 requires $6xy = -6xy$, i.e. $12xy = 0$.

CR2 holds only when $x = 0$ or $y = 0$ — that is, on the coordinate axes. So $g$ is complex differentiable only at points on the axes, and is not analytic anywhere (analytic requires differentiability on an open set, but the axes form a set with no interior).

Note: This function is not $z^3$. Recall $z^3 = (x^3 - 3xy^2) + i(3x^2y - y^3)$. What's in problem 1b has the opposite signs on the cubic cross-terms — it is not a holomorphic function of $z$.

Problem 2 — Finding Constants for Analyticity
2Find real constants $a$ and $b$ such that $f(z) = 3x - y + 5 + i(ax + by - 3)$ is complex differentiable.

When asked to find constants that make a function analytic, you apply the CR equations as equations in the constants rather than as checks. Set $\partial_x u = \partial_y v$ and $\partial_y u = -\partial_x v$, solve the resulting system. Each CR equation gives one constraint, so two unknowns ($a$ and $b$) are determined exactly.

Approach

1. Identify $u = 3x - y + 5$ and $v = ax + by - 3$.

2. Compute partial derivatives in terms of $a$ and $b$.

3. Set up CR1 and CR2 as equations — solve for $a$ and $b$.

4. Verify by writing $f$ as an explicit complex function of $z$.

Solution

$u = 3x - y + 5$, $\quad v = ax + by - 3$

$\partial_x u = 3$, $\quad \partial_y v = b$ $\quad \Rightarrow$ CR1: $3 = b$, so $\boxed{b = 3}$.

$\partial_y u = -1$, $\quad -\partial_x v = -a$ $\quad \Rightarrow$ CR2: $-1 = -a$, so $\boxed{a = 1}$.

With $a = 1$, $b = 3$: $f(z) = 3x - y + 5 + i(x + 3y - 3)$.

Sanity check — write as a function of $z$: Notice $3x + ix = x(3+i)$ and $-y + 3iy = y(-1+3i) = iy \cdot (3+i)$. So $(3+i)(x+iy) = (3x - y) + i(x + 3y)$. Therefore:

$$f(z) = (3+i)z + 5 - 3i$$

This is a linear function — entirely consistent with being analytic everywhere.

Problem 3 — Both $f$ and $\overline{f(z)}$ Analytic Implies Constant
3Show that if $f(z)$ and $\overline{f(z)}$ are both complex differentiable, then $f'(z) = 0$.

The notation $\overline{f(z)}$ means the complex conjugate of the output $f(z)$ — if $f = u + iv$, then $\overline{f(z)} = u - iv$. The key idea: applying the CR equations to both $f$ and $\overline{f}$ simultaneously forces all four partial derivatives to be zero, which makes $f'(z) = 0$. This is a proof-style problem, so write it in full logical steps.

Approach

1. Write the CR equations for $f = u + iv$.

2. Identify the real and imaginary parts of $\overline{f} = u + i(-v)$ and write its CR equations.

3. The two sets of equations will be contradictory unless all partials are zero.

4. Conclude $f'(z) = \partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v = 0 + 0 = 0$.

Solution

Write $f = u + iv$.

CR for $f$: $\;\partial_x u = \partial_y v\;$ and $\;\partial_y u = -\partial_x v\;$ ... (I)

The function $\overline{f(z)} = u(x,y) - iv(x,y)$ has real part $U = u$ and imaginary part $V = -v$.

CR for $\overline{f}$: $\;\partial_x U = \partial_y V\;$ and $\;\partial_y U = -\partial_x V$, which becomes:

$\partial_x u = \partial_y(-v) = -\partial_y v\;$ and $\;\partial_y u = -\partial_x(-v) = \partial_x v$ ... (II)

Now combine (I) and (II):

From (I): $\partial_x u = +\partial_y v$. From (II): $\partial_x u = -\partial_y v$. Adding: $2\partial_x u = 0 \Rightarrow \partial_x u = 0$, hence $\partial_y v = 0$.

From (I): $\partial_y u = -\partial_x v$. From (II): $\partial_y u = +\partial_x v$. Adding: $2\partial_y u = 0 \Rightarrow \partial_y u = 0$, hence $\partial_x v = 0$.

All four partial derivatives vanish: $\partial_x u = \partial_y u = \partial_x v = \partial_y v = 0$. Therefore $f'(z) = \partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v = 0$. Since the domain is connected and $f' = 0$ throughout, $f$ is constant. $\blacksquare$

Problem 4 — Cauchy Integral Formula
4$\displaystyle\int_C \frac{\cos z}{3z - \pi}\,dz$, where $C = \{|z| = 2\}$

The Cauchy integral formula states: if $f$ is analytic inside and on $C$ and $z_0$ is inside $C$, then $\oint_C \frac{f(z)}{z - z_0}\,dz = 2\pi i\, f(z_0)$. To apply it, you first need to massage the denominator into the form $(z - z_0)$ with a leading coefficient of $1$, by factoring out constants. Then identify $f(z)$ (everything except the $1/(z-z_0)$ factor) and check that $z_0$ is inside $C$.

Approach

1. Factor out the coefficient of $z$ from the denominator: $3z - \pi = 3(z - \pi/3)$.

2. Check: is $z_0 = \pi/3$ inside $|z| = 2$? Compute $|\pi/3| \approx 1.05 < 2$. Yes.

3. Identify $f(z) = \cos z$ (the rest of the integrand after removing $1/(z - z_0)$).

4. Apply the formula, accounting for the factor of $1/3$ from the denominator.

Solution

Factor: $3z - \pi = 3\!\left(z - \frac{\pi}{3}\right)$. So:

$$\int_C \frac{\cos z}{3z - \pi}\,dz = \frac{1}{3}\int_C \frac{\cos z}{z - \pi/3}\,dz$$

Check: $z_0 = \pi/3$. $|z_0| = \pi/3 \approx 1.047 < 2$, so $z_0$ is inside $C = \{|z| = 2\}$. ✓

$f(z) = \cos z$ is entire, so it's analytic inside $C$. Apply the Cauchy integral formula with $z_0 = \pi/3$:

$$\frac{1}{3} \cdot 2\pi i \cdot \cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{2\pi i}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \boxed{\frac{\pi i}{3}}$$
Problem 5 — Cauchy Integral Formula with Partial Fractions
5$\displaystyle\int_C \frac{\sin z}{z^2 + \pi^2}\,dz$, where $C = \{|z - 2i| = 2\}$

The denominator $z^2 + \pi^2$ factors over $\mathbb{C}$ as $(z - \pi i)(z + \pi i)$, giving two singularities. The contour $C$ is a circle centered at $2i$ with radius $2$, so you need to check which singularities lie inside $C$. Only the ones inside contribute — the others can be ignored. Once you've identified which singularity is inside, the integral reduces directly to the Cauchy integral formula with $f(z)$ being the remaining analytic factor.

Approach

1. Factor: $z^2 + \pi^2 = (z - \pi i)(z + \pi i)$. Singularities at $z = \pi i$ and $z = -\pi i$.

2. Check which singularities are inside $C = \{|z - 2i| = 2\}$ (center $2i$, radius $2$):

 $|z_0 - 2i|$ for each: $|\pi i - 2i| = |(\pi-2)i| = \pi - 2 \approx 1.14 < 2$ ✓ (inside)

 $|-\pi i - 2i| = |(-\pi-2)i| = \pi + 2 \approx 5.14 > 2$ ✗ (outside)

3. Only $z = \pi i$ is inside. Write the integrand as $\frac{f(z)}{z - \pi i}$ where $f(z) = \frac{\sin z}{z + \pi i}$.

4. Verify $f(z) = \sin z/(z + \pi i)$ is analytic inside $C$ (the only singularity $-\pi i$ is outside). Apply the formula.

5. Evaluate $\sin(\pi i)$: recall $\sin(w) = (e^{iw} - e^{-iw})/(2i)$, so $\sin(\pi i) = (e^{-\pi} - e^{\pi})/(2i) = i\sinh(\pi)$.

Solution

Factor: $z^2 + \pi^2 = (z - \pi i)(z + \pi i)$.

Only $z_0 = \pi i$ is inside $C$ (distance to center $2i$: $|\pi i - 2i| = \pi - 2 \approx 1.14 < 2$).

Write: $\displaystyle\frac{\sin z}{(z - \pi i)(z + \pi i)} = \frac{f(z)}{z - \pi i}$ where $f(z) = \dfrac{\sin z}{z + \pi i}$.

$f(z)$ is analytic inside $C$ (its only singularity $z = -\pi i$ is outside). Apply Cauchy integral formula:

$$\int_C \frac{\sin z}{z^2 + \pi^2}\,dz = 2\pi i \cdot f(\pi i) = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{\sin(\pi i)}{\pi i + \pi i} = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{\sin(\pi i)}{2\pi i} = \sin(\pi i)$$

Now evaluate $\sin(\pi i)$ using the complex sine formula:

$$\sin(\pi i) = \frac{e^{i(\pi i)} - e^{-i(\pi i)}}{2i} = \frac{e^{-\pi} - e^{\pi}}{2i} = \frac{-(e^\pi - e^{-\pi})}{2i} = \frac{-(e^\pi - e^{-\pi})}{2i} \cdot \frac{i}{i} = \frac{i(e^\pi - e^{-\pi})}{2}$$

Since $\sinh(\pi) = (e^\pi - e^{-\pi})/2$ by definition:

$$\boxed{\int_C \frac{\sin z}{z^2 + \pi^2}\,dz = i\sinh(\pi)}$$

Exam Checklist — CR Problems
  • Write down $u$ and $v$ explicitly before computing anything.
  • Compute all four: $\partial_x u$, $\partial_y u$, $\partial_x v$, $\partial_y v$.
  • Check CR1 ($\partial_x u = \partial_y v$) and CR2 ($\partial_y u = -\partial_x v$) — both must hold.
  • If both hold everywhere on an open set → analytic. If only on a discrete set → differentiable at those points but not analytic.
  • State the derivative using $f'(z) = \partial_x u + i\,\partial_x v$ if asked.
Exam Checklist — Cauchy Integral Formula Problems
  • Factor the denominator completely over $\mathbb{C}$ to find all singularities.
  • For each singularity $z_0$: compute $|z_0 - \text{center}|$ and compare to radius.
  • Only singularities strictly inside $C$ contribute.
  • For each interior singularity: pull out the factor $(z - z_0)$ and identify the analytic $f(z)$.
  • Apply $2\pi i\,f(z_0)$ for each interior singularity and sum the results.
  • For $\sin(ai)$ or $\cos(ai)$: use $\sin(ai) = i\sinh(a)$ and $\cos(ai) = \cosh(a)$.
