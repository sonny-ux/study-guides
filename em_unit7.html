Unit 7 · Section 1
Magnetic Force on Charges
The Lorentz force law and the right-hand rule

A magnetic field exerts a force on any charged particle that is moving through it — this is the Lorentz force, and it is the fundamental reason magnets attract, motors spin, and cosmic rays curve in Earth's field. Unlike gravity or the electric force, it acts sideways: think of a moving charge as a boat on a river, where the magnetic field is the current that sweeps the boat perpendicular to its heading, never speeding it up or slowing it down, only bending its path. This kicks off Unit 7 from scratch — there is no prior magnetic material to build on, though the cross-product geometry will reappear in every section that follows.

⚡ The Magnetic Force

A charge $q$ moving with velocity $\vec{v}$ in a magnetic field $\vec{B}$ experiences:

$$\vec{F}_B = q\vec{v}\times\vec{B}$$

$q$ is the charge of the particle experiencing the force (signed: positive for protons, negative for electrons); $\vec{v}$ is that particle's velocity vector; $\vec{B}$ is the external magnetic field at its location. The cross product $\vec{v}\times\vec{B}$ encodes the geometry: the result points perpendicular to the plane containing $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$, so the force is always sideways to the motion — it can steer the particle but never speed it up or slow it down. For a negative charge, the sign of $q$ flips the direction of $\vec{F}_B$, so electrons curve the opposite way from protons in the same field.

Magnitude: $F_B = |q|vB\sin\phi$, where $\phi$ is the angle between $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$.

The $\sin\phi$ factor is the scalar shadow of the cross product: only the component of velocity perpendicular to $\vec{B}$ contributes. When $\phi = 0°$ (particle moving along the field), the force vanishes entirely — the field has nothing to push against. When $\phi = 90°$ (particle moving across the field), $\sin\phi = 1$ and the force is at its maximum $|q|vB$.

Key facts:

  • $\vec{F}_B$ is always perpendicular to both $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$ — it does no work
  • $F_B$ is zero when $\vec{v} \parallel \vec{B}$ (i.e. $\phi = 0°$)
  • $F_B$ is maximum when $\vec{v} \perp \vec{B}$ (i.e. $\phi = 90°$)
  • Direction reverses for negative charges
🖐 Right-Hand Rule
1

Point fingers along $\vec{v}$

2

Curl toward $\vec{B}$

3

Thumb points along $\vec{F}$ (for $+q$)

4

Flip for $-q$

This is just a physical mnemonic for the cross product $\vec{v}\times\vec{B}$. The key insight: your thumb gives the direction for a positive charge. For a negative charge the force is in exactly the opposite direction — so an electron in the same situation curves the other way. When $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$ are parallel (steps 1 and 2 point the same way), there is no unique plane to pick and the force is zero, consistent with $\sin 0° = 0$.

📐 Units & Field Lines

Tesla (SI unit)

$$1\,\text{T} = 1\,\frac{\text{N}}{\text{A}\cdot\text{m}} = \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{A}\cdot\text{s}^2}$$

This is derived directly from $F = qvB$: rearranging, $B = F/(qv)$. In SI, $F$ is in N, $q$ in C = A·s, and $v$ in m/s, giving N/(A·s·m/s) = N/(A·m). The kg form comes from substituting N = kg·m/s². In practice, 1 T is an enormous field — a strong MRI magnet is 1.5–3 T; Earth's surface field is only about 50 μT.

Gauss (CGS)

$$1\,\text{T} = 10^4\,\text{G}$$

The Gauss is the older CGS unit. The factor of $10^4$ is purely a unit conversion — there is no new physics. Earth's field (~0.5 G) sounds more natural in Gauss, while MRI magnets are described in Tesla. You will see Gauss in older texts and in geophysics; always convert to Tesla before plugging into $F = qvB$.

Earth's field ≈ 0.5 G = 50 μT

Gauss's Law for Magnetism

$$\oint \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{A} = 0$$

The integral sums the outward magnetic flux through any closed surface. The result is always zero because magnetic monopoles do not exist — every field line that enters a closed surface must also exit it. Compare with Gauss's law for electricity ($\oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{A} = Q_\text{enc}/\varepsilon_0$), where a net charge inside gives nonzero flux. This is one of Maxwell's four equations and tells you that magnetic field lines form closed loops with no beginning or end.

No magnetic monopoles — field lines are always closed loops.

Crossed Fields & Hall Effect
Velocity selectors, e/m ratio, and the Hall voltage

When you place both an electric field and a magnetic field in the same region, their forces on a moving charge can cancel — this competition is the basis of the velocity selector and the Hall effect. Picture a charged marble rolling between two fans blowing in opposite directions: only at one exact speed do the gusts balance and the marble travel straight. Building directly on the Lorentz force from Section 1, crossed fields let us isolate particles by speed and even reveal what sign of charge carrier is flowing in a conductor.

🎯 Velocity Selector

When $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ are perpendicular, a particle moves in a straight line when electric and magnetic forces balance:

$$qE = qvB \implies v = \frac{E}{B}$$

The left side is the electric force on the particle (magnitude $qE$, directed along $\vec{E}$); the right side is the magnetic force (magnitude $qvB$, directed opposite to $\vec{E}$ by design of the field orientation). Setting them equal and cancelling $q$ gives the selected speed $v = E/B$. Crucially, $q$ and $m$ both cancel — every particle with this speed passes straight through regardless of its charge or mass. This is what makes the velocity selector a useful filter: it sorts by speed, not by particle type.

Only particles with speed $v = E/B$ pass through undeflected — independent of $q$ and $m$.

⚛️ Measuring $e/m$ (J.J. Thomson)

Electrons are accelerated and then deflected by $\vec{B}$. Using circular motion in the field:

$$\frac{e}{m} = \frac{v}{rB} = \frac{E}{rB^2}$$

$r$ is the measured radius of the electron's circular arc in the magnetic field; $v$ is the electron's speed (determined from the velocity selector condition $v = E/B$); $B$ is the applied magnetic field strength. The first form $e/m = v/(rB)$ comes directly from rearranging the circular orbit condition $evB = mv^2/r$. The second form $e/m = E/(rB^2)$ substitutes $v = E/B$, so you only need to measure $r$, $E$, and $B$ — no direct speed measurement required. Thomson's 1897 result, $e/m \approx 1.76\times10^{11}$ C/kg, was the first evidence that the electron is a particle far lighter than any atom.

Thomson's 1897 measurement: $e/m \approx 1.76\times10^{11}$ C/kg.

🔬 Hall Effect

A current-carrying conductor in $\vec{B}$ develops a transverse voltage — the Hall voltage.

Charge carriers pushed sideways by $\vec{F}_B$ accumulate until equilibrium $qE_H = qv_d B$:

$$V_H = v_d B d$$

$v_d$ is the drift velocity of the charge carriers in the conductor (typically $\sim$mm/s for ordinary currents); $B$ is the applied magnetic field perpendicular to the current flow; $d$ is the width of the conductor measured in the direction that the carriers are pushed (i.e., transverse to both current and $\vec{B}$). What this equation says physically: the sideways magnetic force piles carriers onto one face of the conductor, building up an opposing electric field $E_H = V_H/d$ until that electric force exactly cancels the magnetic push. The sign of $V_H$ (which face goes positive) tells you whether the carriers are positive or negative — this is how the Hall effect proved that metals conduct via negative electrons, not positive charges.

where $d$ is the width of the conductor. This can determine the sign of the charge carriers.

  • Hall effect proves electrons (not holes) carry current in metals
  • Hall probes measure $\vec{B}$ field magnitude directly
  • Used in modern sensors (brushless motors, position detectors)

Circular Motion in $\vec{B}$
Cyclotrons, cyclotron frequency, and helical paths

Because the magnetic force is always perpendicular to velocity, it never changes a particle's speed — it can only steer it. The result in a uniform field is a perfect circle, like a ball on a frictionless string whose tension is supplied by the magnetic force. If the particle also has a velocity component along the field direction, that component is untouched and it corkscrew-drills forward in a helix. This section follows directly from the Lorentz force: we just apply Newton's second law with $\vec{F}_B$ as the centripetal force.

🔄 Circular Orbit

Since $\vec{F}_B \perp \vec{v}$, it acts as centripetal force — the particle moves in a circle:

$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r} \implies r = \frac{mv}{qB}$$

The left side is the magnetic force magnitude $|q|vB$ (with $v \perp B$); the right side is the centripetal acceleration requirement $mv^2/r$ from Newton's second law. Setting them equal and solving for $r$ gives the Larmor radius (also called the cyclotron radius). Larger momentum $mv$ means a wider arc — a fast or heavy particle is harder to bend. Stronger $B$ bends the path more tightly. Crucially, speed $v$ appears in both numerator and denominator of $r = mv/(qB)$ when you think about it dynamically: if you speed the particle up, the centripetal force demand also grows, and the orbit radius increases proportionally.

The radius (Larmor radius) is larger for faster or heavier particles, smaller for stronger $B$.

⏱ Period & Cyclotron Frequency

Period $T = 2\pi r/v = 2\pi m/(qB)$ — independent of speed!

$$T = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}$$

$T$ is the time for one complete orbit. Substituting $r = mv/(qB)$ into the circumference formula $T = 2\pi r/v$ causes $v$ to cancel completely. This means that regardless of how fast the particle is moving, it always completes one revolution in the same time — faster particles trace larger circles but at proportionally higher speed. This speed-independence is the operating principle of the cyclotron accelerator: you can use a fixed-frequency alternating voltage to accelerate particles repeatedly without having to retune the frequency as they gain energy.

Period is the same for all speeds

$$\omega_c = \frac{qB}{m}$$

$\omega_c$ is the cyclotron angular frequency, equal to $2\pi/T$. It depends only on the charge-to-mass ratio $q/m$ and the field strength $B$ — not on the particle's speed or position. This is the resonant frequency at which a cyclotron's "dee" voltage must alternate to keep accelerating the particle in sync with its orbital period. For electrons, $\omega_c$ is in the GHz range for typical lab fields; this is the basis of electron cyclotron resonance plasma heating and certain radar tubes (magnetrons).

Cyclotron angular frequency

This is the principle behind the cyclotron particle accelerator.

🌀 Helical Paths

If $\vec{v}$ has a component parallel to $\vec{B}$ (angle $\phi$), the particle undergoes helical motion:

  • $v_\perp = v\sin\phi$ → circular motion (radius $r = mv\sin\phi/qB$)
  • $v_\parallel = v\cos\phi$ → constant drift along $\vec{B}$
  • Pitch: $p = v_\parallel \cdot T = v\cos\phi\cdot\dfrac{2\pi m}{qB}$

The pitch $p$ is the axial distance the particle advances per orbit. Since $v_\parallel$ is untouched by the magnetic force (it's parallel to $\vec{B}$, so $\vec{v}_\parallel \times \vec{B} = 0$), the particle drifts forward at constant speed while simultaneously circling. Think of it as a corkscrew: tighter spiral for larger $\phi$ (more $v_\perp$), longer pitch for smaller $\phi$ (more $v_\parallel$). This helical motion explains how charged particles are trapped along magnetic field lines in Earth's magnetosphere.

Force on a Current-Carrying Wire
A wire in a magnetic field experiences a net force

A wire carrying current is just a river of moving charges, so the same Lorentz force that acts on a single particle acts — summed over all the carriers — on the wire as a whole. Think of it like a current of water pushing against a paddle: the individual water molecules are the moving charges, and the paddle is the wire that feels the net sideways kick. This section is the direct macroscopic consequence of Section 1, and the result ($\vec{F} = i\vec{L} \times \vec{B}$) is exactly the Lorentz force scaled up from a single charge to a full current.

🔌 Force on Straight Wire

A wire of length $L$ carrying current $i$ in field $\vec{B}$:

$$\vec{F} = i\vec{L}\times\vec{B}$$

$i$ is the current in the wire (the net charge per second flowing through it); $\vec{L}$ is a vector of magnitude equal to the wire's length, pointing in the direction of conventional current flow (positive charge drift direction); $\vec{B}$ is the external field at the wire's location. This is the Lorentz force $q\vec{v}\times\vec{B}$ summed over all the mobile carriers in the segment: each carrier contributes $q\vec{v}_d\times\vec{B}$, and multiplying charge density × drift velocity × cross-sectional area = current $i$, so the total force is $i\vec{L}\times\vec{B}$. The direction of $\vec{F}$ is always perpendicular to the wire — it pushes or pulls the wire sideways, which is how electric motors generate torque.

Magnitude: $F = iLB\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the wire direction and $\vec{B}$.

  • The force is zero when current is parallel to $\vec{B}$
  • Maximum force when wire is perpendicular to $\vec{B}$
  • Direction: RHR on current direction × $\vec{B}$
⌒ Curved Wire in Uniform $\vec{B}$

For any curved wire from point $a$ to $b$ in a uniform field $\vec{B}$:

$$\vec{F} = i\vec{L}_{ab}\times\vec{B}$$

$\vec{L}_{ab}$ is the straight-line displacement vector from endpoint $a$ to endpoint $b$ — it does not care about the shape of the wire between them. This remarkable result follows from integrating $i\,d\vec{l}\times\vec{B}$ along any path: since $\vec{B}$ is uniform, it factors out of the integral, and $\int_a^b d\vec{l} = \vec{L}_{ab}$ regardless of the path. The physical insight is that in a uniform field, every little segment of a curved wire feels the same field, so the cross-path forces cancel sideways and only the net chord displacement matters. For a closed loop, $\vec{L}_{aa} = \vec{0}$, so the net force is exactly zero — the loop may be torqued but not translated.

where $\vec{L}_{ab}$ is the vector from $a$ to $b$ (the chord). Only the displacement between endpoints matters!

Consequence: a closed loop in a uniform $\vec{B}$ has zero net force.

For a semicircular arc of radius $R$ in the $xy$-plane with $\vec{B}=B\hat{z}$: the chord is $2R\hat{x}$, so $F_x = i(2R)B$.

🛤 Wire on Rails (HW6 P4)

A rod of mass $m$, length $d$, carrying current $i$ on frictionless rails in field $\vec{B}$:

$$ma = idB \implies v(t) = \frac{idB}{m}\,t$$

$m$ is the mass of the rod; $a$ is its linear acceleration along the rails; $d$ is the length of the rod (the "wire length" in $F = iLB$); $B$ is the perpendicular field. The force $F = idB$ is constant because $i$, $d$, and $B$ don't change as the rod slides. Constant force on mass $m$ gives constant acceleration $a = idB/m$, and integrating from rest gives velocity growing linearly in time: $v = at$. This is the simplest electromagnetic actuator — a magnetic rail gun in its most basic form.

Constant force → constant acceleration → velocity grows linearly in time.

Torque & Magnetic Dipole Moment
Current loops as magnetic dipoles

A current loop in a magnetic field does not just get pushed in one direction — the two sides of the loop feel forces in opposite directions, which together produce a twisting torque that tries to align the loop with the field, just like a compass needle snapping toward magnetic north. The loop's tendency to align is captured by a single vector called the magnetic dipole moment $\vec{\mu}$, which points along the loop's axis and encodes both how much current flows and how large the area is. This is the rotational counterpart to the translational force of Section 3, and the dipole framework will carry forward directly into potential energy in Section 6.

🌀 Torque on a Current Loop

A rectangular loop of area $A$ carrying current $i$ at angle $\theta$ to $\vec{B}$:

$$\tau = iAB\sin\theta$$

$i$ is the current in the loop; $A$ is the area enclosed by the loop; $B$ is the external field magnitude; $\theta$ is the angle between the loop's magnetic moment vector $\vec{\mu}$ (perpendicular to the loop plane, by the right-hand rule with the current) and $\vec{B}$. The torque is maximum when $\theta = 90°$ — that is, when the loop's face is parallel to the field, so the two long sides of the loop are both fully perpendicular to $\vec{B}$ and each feel the maximum sideways force. The torque is zero when $\theta = 0°$ — face perpendicular to field, the loop is already aligned. This formula applies to any flat loop shape since only the enclosed area $A$ matters, not the geometry.

For $N$ turns: $\tau = NiAB\sin\theta$. Torque is maximum at $\theta=90°$ (loop plane parallel to $\vec{B}$) and zero at $\theta=0°$ (loop plane perpendicular to $\vec{B}$).

🧲 Magnetic Dipole Moment

Define the magnetic dipole moment:

$$\vec{\mu} = NiA\,\hat{n}$$

$N$ is the number of turns of wire wound in the coil (each turn contributes the same torque, so they add); $i$ is the current in each turn; $A$ is the area of the loop; $\hat{n}$ is the unit normal to the loop plane, determined by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction of current flow and your thumb points along $\hat{n}$ (and thus along $\vec{\mu}$). The magnitude $\mu = NiA$ has units of A·m², which is the standard SI unit for magnetic moment. A larger loop, more turns, or higher current all increase $\mu$ and thus the coupling to the field.

Direction $\hat{n}$: right-hand rule with current direction (curl fingers → thumb points along $\hat{n}$).

The torque becomes:

$$\vec{\tau} = \vec{\mu}\times\vec{B}$$

This compact vector form replaces the scalar $\tau = NiAB\sin\theta$ and automatically gives both magnitude and direction. The magnitude is $|\vec{\mu}|B\sin\theta$, which matches the scalar formula with $\mu = NiA$. The direction of $\vec{\tau}$ (given by the right-hand rule on $\vec{\mu}\times\vec{B}$) points along the axis of rotation — the torque acts to rotate $\vec{\mu}$ toward alignment with $\vec{B}$, just as an electric dipole torque $\vec{\tau} = \vec{p}\times\vec{E}$ tries to align $\vec{p}$ with $\vec{E}$. This is why a compass needle points north: its magnetic dipole moment aligns with Earth's field.

This drives the dipole to align with $\vec{B}$ — exactly like an electric dipole $\vec{p}$ in $\vec{E}$.

Worked Example

$N=100$ turns, $2\,\text{m}\times1\,\text{m}$ loop, $B=2\,\text{T}$, perpendicular:

$\mu = 100\cdot i\cdot2\cdot1 = 200\,i$ → at $\theta=90°$: $\tau = \mu B = 200\cdot2 = 400$ N·m per amp of $i$.

Potential Energy of Magnetic Dipole
Orientation energy and equilibrium states

Just as lifting a mass against gravity stores gravitational potential energy, rotating a magnetic dipole away from its preferred alignment with $\vec{B}$ stores energy in the orientation of the system. The lowest-energy state is when $\vec{\mu}$ points along $\vec{B}$ (aligned), and the highest is when it points opposite — exactly like an electric dipole in an electric field. This section closes the loop on the dipole story begun in Section 5: torque describes how the field tries to rotate the dipole, and potential energy $U = -\vec{\mu} \cdot \vec{B}$ quantifies how much rotational work has been stored.

⚡ Dipole Potential Energy

Work done against the torque to rotate the dipole defines the potential energy:

$$U = -\vec{\mu}\cdot\vec{B} = -\mu B\cos\theta$$

$\vec{\mu}$ is the magnetic dipole moment of the loop; $\vec{B}$ is the external field; $\theta$ is the angle between them. The dot product $\vec{\mu}\cdot\vec{B} = \mu B\cos\theta$ is maximized when $\vec{\mu}$ and $\vec{B}$ are parallel ($\theta=0$), making $U = -\mu B$ (most negative, lowest energy — stable equilibrium). When $\theta=180°$, $\cos\theta = -1$, so $U = +\mu B$ (highest energy — unstable equilibrium). The negative sign is not arbitrary: it comes from integrating the torque $\tau = -\mu B\sin\theta$ against rotation, with the choice that $U = 0$ at $\theta = 90°$. The same formula $U = -\vec{p}\cdot\vec{E}$ applies to electric dipoles — the structural analogy between electric and magnetic dipoles is exact.

  • $\theta = 0°$: $U = -\mu B$ (minimum, stable equilibrium — $\vec{\mu}$ aligned with $\vec{B}$)
  • $\theta = 90°$: $U = 0$ (reference state)
  • $\theta = 180°$: $U = +\mu B$ (maximum, unstable equilibrium)

The only physical difference is the origin: electric dipoles arise from separated charges ($\vec{p} = q\vec{d}$), while magnetic dipoles arise from circulating currents ($\vec{\mu} = NiA\hat{n}$). Memorizing one set of formulas gives you the other for free by substitution $\vec{p}\to\vec{\mu}$, $\vec{E}\to\vec{B}$.

📐 HW6 P5 — Potential Energy

With $\vec{\mu} = \mu_0\!\left[\tfrac{3}{5}\hat{x} - \tfrac{4}{5}\hat{y}\right]$ and $\vec{B} = B_0\!\left[0.25\hat{x}+0.3\hat{z}\right]$:

$$U = -\vec{\mu}\cdot\vec{B} = -\mu_0 B_0\!\left[\frac{3}{5}(0.25) + \left(-\frac{4}{5}\right)(0) + (0)(0.3)\right]$$

The dot product is computed component-by-component: multiply matching components and sum. The $\hat{y}$ component of $\vec{B}$ is zero (there is no $\hat{y}$ term in $\vec{B}$), so the $-4/5$ component of $\vec{\mu}$ contributes nothing. The $\hat{z}$ component of $\vec{\mu}$ is also zero. Only the $\hat{x}$ overlap survives: $(3/5)(0.25) = 0.15$. The negative sign out front then makes $U = -0.15\,\mu_0 B_0$ (a negative energy, meaning the dipole is partially aligned with the field — a lower energy state than fully perpendicular).

$$U = -\mu_0 B_0(0.15) = -0.15\,\mu_0 B_0$$

DC Motor & Final Quiz
Continuous rotation via current reversal

A DC motor is the punchline of the entire unit: by cleverly reversing the current through a rotating loop at exactly the right moment, the magnetic torque is made to always push in the same rotational direction, converting a steady electrical current into continuous mechanical rotation. Without the commutator, the loop would just oscillate back and forth around equilibrium — with it, the loop spins indefinitely. This section connects torque ($\vec{\tau} = \vec{\mu} \times \vec{B}$) from Section 5 to a real engineering device, and sets up the generator concept that Faraday's law (Unit 8) will explain from the other direction.

⚙️ DC Motor Principle

A current loop in $\vec{B}$ will rotate toward $\theta=0$ (stable equilibrium) and stop — unless we intervene.

A commutator automatically reverses the current every half-turn, so the torque always pushes the loop in the same rotational direction:

  • At alignment ($\theta=0$): commutator switches current direction
  • Loop overshoots, torque immediately acts to rotate further in the same sense
  • Result: continuous rotation, converting electrical energy to mechanical energy

Key insight:

Without the commutator, the coil oscillates about equilibrium. With it, the coil rotates indefinitely. The commutator is timed so current reversal happens at the exact moment the loop would stall.

Think about what $\vec{\tau} = \vec{\mu}\times\vec{B}$ says as the loop rotates: torque is proportional to $\sin\theta$, which goes to zero at $\theta=0°$ (aligned) and $\theta=180°$ (anti-aligned). Without the commutator, the loop coasts through alignment at $\theta=0$ and decelerates toward a stop. The commutator flips the current at the exact moment $\theta$ passes through 0, reversing $\vec{\mu}$, which means the new "anti-aligned" orientation is now the starting condition again — so $\sin\theta$ is reset to a large value and the torque kicks hard in the same direction. The motor always sees the torque as pushing forward, never as pulling back.

$\vec{\tau} = \vec{\mu}\times\vec{B}$ in the motor row encodes the entire Unit 7 chain: current loop → dipole moment $\vec{\mu}$ → cross product with $\vec{B}$ → torque → mechanical rotation. In the generator (Unit 8), the causal arrow reverses: mechanical rotation changes the flux through the loop over time, inducing an EMF by Faraday's law. The two devices are physically the same machine run in opposite directions — a testament to the time-reversal symmetry of Maxwell's equations.
