01 What Chapter 4 Is About

Main Idea
Chapters 1â€“3 built the infrastructure: metric spaces, sequences, compactness, completeness, series. Chapter 4 is where you first study functions rather than just sets and sequences. The central question is what it means for a function to be "well-behaved" near a point. The answer is continuity â€” and the chapter systematically explores what continuity implies, what breaks it, and how it interacts with compactness and connectedness.

The deepest results here â€” the Extreme Value Theorem, the Intermediate Value Theorem, and uniform continuity on compact sets â€” are not just facts to memorize. They are the payoff of everything built in Chapters 1â€“3. Understanding why they require compactness or connectedness is the real content of the chapter.

02 Limits of Functions

Main Idea
The limit $\lim_{x\to p} f(x) = q$ describes the behavior of $f$ as $x$ approaches $p$ â€” never at $p$ itself. The function need not even be defined at $p$. Why must $p$ be a limit point? Because if $p$ is isolated, the limit is vacuously true for any $q$ â€” meaningless.
Definition â€” Limit at a Point
Let $(X,d_X)$ and $(Y,d_Y)$ be metric spaces, $E\subseteq X$, $p$ a limit point of $E$, $f:E\to Y$. Then $\lim_{x\to p}f(x)=q$ if:

$\forall\,\varepsilon>0\;\exists\,\delta>0$ such that $d_X(x,p)<\delta$ and $x\in E\setminus\{p\}$ $\Rightarrow$ $d_Y(f(x),q)<\varepsilon$
Uniqueness of Limits
If $\lim_{x\to p}f(x)=q$ and $\lim_{x\to p}f(x)=q'$, then $q=q'$. Proof: for any $\varepsilon>0$, get $\delta$ so $f(x)$ is within $\varepsilon$ of both. Then $d(q,q')\leq d(q,f(x))+d(f(x),q')<2\varepsilon$. Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary, $d(q,q')=0$.
Sequential Criterion (Rudin Â§4.2)
$\lim_{x\to p}f(x)=q$ $\;\Longleftrightarrow\;$ for every sequence $\{p_n\}$ in $E\setminus\{p\}$ with $p_n\to p$, we have $f(p_n)\to q$.

To disprove a limit: find two sequences approaching $p$ along which $f$ approaches different values. Classic: $f(x)=\sin(1/x)$ as $x\to 0$. Along $x_n=1/(2\pi n)$: $f\to 0$. Along $x_n=1/(\pi/2+2\pi n)$: $f\to 1$. No limit exists.
Arithmetic of Limits
If $\lim_{x\to p}f(x)=A$ and $\lim_{x\to p}g(x)=B$, then $\lim(f+g)=A+B$, $\lim(fg)=AB$, $\lim(f/g)=A/B$ (if $B\neq 0$). These follow immediately from the sequential criterion and the corresponding theorems for sequences.

03 Continuity

Main Idea
Continuity at $p$ is the statement that the limit and the function value agree: $\lim_{x\to p}f(x)=f(p)$. If $p$ is an isolated point, $f$ is automatically continuous there. Continuity is only a real constraint at limit points.
Definition â€” Continuity at $p$
$f:E\to Y$ is continuous at $p\in E$ if $\forall\,\varepsilon>0\;\exists\,\delta>0$ such that $d_X(x,p)<\delta$ and $x\in E$ $\Rightarrow$ $d_Y(f(x),f(p))<\varepsilon$.

Unlike the limit definition, $x=p$ is allowed â€” it's automatically fine since $d_Y(f(p),f(p))=0<\varepsilon$.
Scratch Work â€” How to Read and Write a Continuity Proof
The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition looks like it requires you to magically produce $\delta$. In practice you always find $\delta$ by working backwards first, then writing the proof forwards.

Template: (1) Write what you want to show: $|f(x)-f(p)|<\varepsilon$. (2) Expand using the formula for $f$. (3) Simplify until you see something like $C\cdot|x-p|$ on the right. (4) Set $\delta=\varepsilon/C$ (or smaller if needed). (5) Re-read the proof forward with this $\delta$.

Example: $f(x)=x^2$ is continuous at $p$.} Scratch work: $|x^2-p^2|=|x+p|\cdot|x-p|$. I need $|x+p|$ bounded. Restrict: if $|x-p|<1$, then $|x|<|p|+1$, so $|x+p|\leq|x|+|p|<2|p|+1$. Then $|x^2-p^2|<(2|p|+1)|x-p|$. Set $\delta=\min(1,\varepsilon/(2|p|+1))$. Now write the proof forward using this $\delta$. Notice $\delta$ depends on both $\varepsilon$ and $p$ â€” this is ordinary continuity, not uniform.

Why we "restrict $\delta\leq 1$" first: Bounding $|x+p|$ requires knowing roughly where $x$ is. By first restricting $|x-p|<1$, we localize $x$ near $p$ and get a useful bound on $|x+p|$. This "two-part $\delta$" technique is standard and should become automatic.
Composition (Rudin Â§4.7)
If $f:E\to Y$ is continuous at $p$, and $g:f(E)\to Z$ is continuous at $f(p)$, then $g\circ f:E\to Z$ is continuous at $p$.

Example: $h(x)=\sin(x^2)$ is continuous everywhere, since $x^2$ is continuous and $\sin$ is continuous.
Arithmetic (Rudin Â§4.9)
If $f,g:E\to\mathbb{R}$ are continuous at $p$, then so are $f+g$, $f-g$, $fg$, and $f/g$ (provided $g(p)\neq 0$). All polynomials are continuous everywhere. All rational functions are continuous wherever the denominator is nonzero.
Topological Characterization (Rudin Â§4.8)
$f:X\to Y$ is continuous on all of $X$ $\;\Longleftrightarrow\;$ for every open set $V\subseteq Y$, the preimage $f^{-1}(V)$ is open in $X$.

This reformulation is purely in terms of open sets â€” no $\varepsilon$, no $\delta$. It is the definition used in topology. It reveals the deepest structure: continuity is about how open sets pull back.

04 Uniform Continuity

Main Idea
Ordinary continuity lets $\delta$ depend on both $\varepsilon$ and the point $p$. Uniform continuity demands a single $\delta$ that works everywhere simultaneously. This is a much stronger condition, and it is the one that plays well with limits, integrals, and approximations.
Definition â€” Uniform Continuity
$f:E\to Y$ is uniformly continuous on $E$ if:

$\forall\,\varepsilon>0\;\exists\,\delta>0$ such that $d_X(x,y)<\delta$ $\Rightarrow$ $d_Y(f(x),f(y))<\varepsilon$ for all $x,y\in E$.

Key difference: $\delta$ depends only on $\varepsilon$, not on the specific points $x$ and $y$.
Scratch Work â€” How to Find $\delta$ from $\varepsilon$
You are never "given" the right $\delta$ â€” you have to derive it by working backwards. The template: start with the goal $|f(x)-f(y)|<\varepsilon$ and manipulate until you isolate $|x-y|$. Whatever bound on $|x-y|$ makes the inequality work becomes your $\delta$.

Example: $f(x)=3x+1$ on $\mathbb{R}$. Goal: $|f(x)-f(y)|=|3x+1-(3y+1)|=3|x-y|<\varepsilon$. This requires $|x-y|<\varepsilon/3$. So set $\delta=\varepsilon/3$. Done â€” no dependence on $x$ or $y$, hence uniformly continuous.

Example: $g(x)=\sqrt{x}$ on $[0,\infty)$. Goal: $|\sqrt{x}-\sqrt{y}|<\varepsilon$. Rationalize: $|\sqrt{x}-\sqrt{y}|=\frac{|x-y|}{\sqrt{x}+\sqrt{y}}\leq\sqrt{|x-y|}$ (since $\sqrt{x}+\sqrt{y}\geq\sqrt{|x-y|}$ by AM-type reasoning, or just use $(\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b})^2\leq|a-b|$). So $\sqrt{|x-y|}<\varepsilon$ requires $|x-y|<\varepsilon^2$. Set $\delta=\varepsilon^2$. The key step was rationalizing; the key insight was that the $\sqrt{x}+\sqrt{y}$ denominator helps when $x,y$ are large but the bound $\leq\sqrt{|x-y|}$ is the clean uniform estimate.

When $\delta$ ends up depending on the point, you do not have uniform continuity. For $f(x)=x^2$: $|x^2-y^2|=|x+y|\cdot|x-y|$. To make this $<\varepsilon$, you need $|x-y|<\varepsilon/|x+y|$. But $|x+y|$ grows without bound, so no single $\delta$ works everywhere.
$f(x)=x^2$ is NOT uniformly continuous on $\mathbb{R}$
Take $\varepsilon=1$. For any $\delta>0$, pick $x=n$ and $y=n+\delta/2$ with $n$ large. Then $|x-y|=\delta/2<\delta$, but $|x^2-y^2|=n\delta+\delta^2/4>n\delta$, which exceeds $1$ once $n>1/\delta$. No single $\delta$ works for all $x$. The problem: as $x\to\infty$, small changes in $x$ produce large changes in $x^2$.
$g(x)=\sqrt{x}$ IS uniformly continuous on $[0,\infty)$
Set $\delta=\varepsilon^2$. For any $x,y\geq 0$ with $|x-y|<\delta$: $$|\sqrt{x}-\sqrt{y}|=\frac{|x-y|}{\sqrt{x}+\sqrt{y}}\leq\sqrt{|x-y|}<\sqrt{\delta}=\varepsilon$$ One $\delta$ works for all $x,y\geq 0$. But $g$ is not Lipschitz at $0$: $|\sqrt{x}-0|/|x-0|=1/\sqrt{x}\to\infty$.

Three conditions from strongest to weakest:

Lipschitz$|f(x)-f(y)|\leq L|x-y|$ for some fixed $L$ and all $x,y\in E$. Implies uniform continuity.
Unif. continuousOne $\delta$ works for all points. Implies ordinary continuity.
Continuous$\delta$ may depend on the point. The weakest condition.

$\sqrt{x}$ is uniformly continuous but not Lipschitz; $x^2$ is continuous but not uniformly continuous on $\mathbb{R}$.

Compactness & Connectedness

The payoff of Chapters 2â€“3: continuous functions preserve topological structure, yielding EVT and IVT.

01 Continuous Images of Compact Sets

Main Idea
Continuity alone doesn't tell you much globally. The additional structure of compactness and connectedness constrains continuous functions to behave well everywhere. The central principle: continuous functions preserve topological structure â€” the continuous image of a compact set is compact, and the continuous image of a connected set is connected.
Theorem (Rudin Â§4.14)
Let $f:X\to Y$ be continuous. If $K\subseteq X$ is compact, then $f(K)$ is compact.

Proof idea: Let $\{V_\alpha\}$ be an open cover of $f(K)$. Then $\{f^{-1}(V_\alpha)\}$ is an open cover of $K$ (each preimage is open by continuity). Since $K$ is compact, finitely many preimages cover $K$. The corresponding $V_\alpha$'s cover $f(K)$.
Corollary: Extreme Value Theorem (Rudin Â§4.16)
Let $f:K\to\mathbb{R}$ be continuous with $K$ compact. Then $f$ attains its maximum and minimum: there exist $p,q\in K$ such that $f(q)\leq f(x)\leq f(p)$ for all $x\in K$.

Why: $f(K)$ is compact in $\mathbb{R}$, hence closed and bounded. A closed bounded subset of $\mathbb{R}$ has a supremum and an infimum, and since it is closed, these are attained.

Example: $f(x)=x^2$ on $[-3,2]$ attains max $9$ at $x=-3$ and min $0$ at $x=0$. On the open interval $(-3,2)$, neither extremum need be attained.
Why Compactness Is Necessary
$f(x)=1/x$ on $(0,1)$: continuous, but $f(0,1)=(1,\infty)$ is not compact. No maximum.
$f(x)=x$ on $\mathbb{R}$: continuous, but $f(\mathbb{R})=\mathbb{R}$ is not bounded. No extrema at all.
Theorem (Rudin Â§4.19) â€” Uniform Continuity on Compact Sets
If $f:K\to Y$ is continuous and $K$ is compact, then $f$ is uniformly continuous on $K$.

Proof idea: Fix $\varepsilon>0$. For each $p\in K$, get $\delta(p)>0$ from continuity. Cover $K$ by balls $B(p,\delta(p)/2)$; extract a finite subcover. Set $\delta=\min(\delta_i/2)$. Any two points within $\delta$ lie in a common ball $B(p_i,\delta_i)$, giving $d(f(x),f(y))<\varepsilon$.
Proof Walk â€” Compact Image is Compact
1
Let $\{V_\alpha\}$ be an arbitrary open cover of $f(K)$.
Strategy. Every compactness proof opens by fixing an arbitrary cover.
2
By continuity of $f$, each preimage $f^{-1}(V_\alpha)$ is open in $X$.
Key step. Uses topological characterization of continuity (preimages of opens are open).
3
$\{f^{-1}(V_\alpha)\}$ covers $K$, so by compactness finitely many suffice: $f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_1}),\ldots,f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_n})$.
Apply compactness of $K$.
4
Then $V_{\alpha_1},\ldots,V_{\alpha_n}$ cover $f(K)$. Every open cover has a finite subcover. $\blacksquare$
Push forward. The corresponding original sets cover the image.

02 Continuity and Connectedness

Main Idea
A metric space $E$ is connected if it cannot be split into two nonempty disjoint open sets. The connected subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ are exactly the intervals. Since continuous functions preserve connectedness, a continuous function on an interval produces an interval â€” which is the Intermediate Value Theorem.
Connected Subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ (Rudin Â§2.47)
$E\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ is connected if and only if $E$ is an interval (any convex subset: $[a,b]$, $(a,b)$, $[a,\infty)$, $(-\infty,b)$, or $\mathbb{R}$ itself).
Theorem (Rudin Â§4.22)
If $f:X\to Y$ is continuous and $E\subseteq X$ is connected, then $f(E)$ is connected.

Proof: Suppose $f(E)=A\cup B$ with $A,B$ nonempty, open, and disjoint. Then $E=f^{-1}(A)\cup f^{-1}(B)$. Each preimage is open (by continuity), they're disjoint and nonempty, and they cover $E$ â€” contradicting connectedness of $E$.
Corollary: Intermediate Value Theorem (Rudin Â§4.23)
Let $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ be continuous with $f(a)\neq f(b)$. Then for every $c$ between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, there exists $x\in(a,b)$ such that $f(x)=c$.

Why: $[a,b]$ is connected, so $f([a,b])$ is connected, so it is an interval. Any interval containing $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ contains everything between them.

Example: $f(x)=x^3-x-1$ on $[1,2]$. $f(1)=-1<0$, $f(2)=5>0$. By IVT, $\exists\, x\in(1,2)$ with $f(x)=0$.
IVT Does NOT Guarantee Uniqueness
$f(x)=\sin x$ on $[0,3\pi]$ satisfies $f(0)=f(3\pi)=0$. The intermediate value $c=0$ is achieved at $x=0,\pi,2\pi,3\pi$. IVT gives existence, not uniqueness.

03 Path-Connectedness

Main Idea
Path-connectedness is stronger than connectedness: for any two points $p,q\in E$ there is a continuous path $\gamma:[0,1]\to E$ with $\gamma(0)=p$ and $\gamma(1)=q$. Path-connected always implies connected, but the converse fails â€” the Topologist's Sine Curve is the canonical counterexample.
The Topologist's Sine Curve
Let $T=\{(x,\sin(1/x)):x\in(0,1]\}\cup(\{0\}\times[-1,1])$.

Connected: The graph $S=\{(x,\sin(1/x))\}$ is the continuous image of the connected set $(0,1]$, hence connected. The segment $\{0\}\times[-1,1]\subseteq\overline{S}$ (sequences $x_n=1/(2\pi n)$ approach $(0,0)$). The closure of a connected set is connected, so $T=\overline{S}$ is connected.

NOT path-connected: Any path from $(0,0)$ to a point on $S$ forces $1/\gamma_1(t)\to\infty$ near the starting time, making $\gamma_2(t)=\sin(1/\gamma_1(t))$ oscillate â€” no limit exists, contradicting continuity of $\gamma_2$.

Discontinuities & Monotone Functions

Classifying how continuity fails, and why monotone functions are uniquely well-controlled.

01 Types of Discontinuities

Main Idea
Not all discontinuities are created equal. Rudin classifies them using one-sided limits. For $f:(a,b)\to\mathbb{R}$ and $p\in(a,b)$: $f(p^+)=\lim_{x\to p^+}f(x)$ and $f(p^-)=\lim_{x\to p^-}f(x)$ â€” if these exist. Understanding the taxonomy matters for problem sets.
The Three Types
Removable discontinuity: Both one-sided limits exist and are equal, $f(p^+)=f(p^-)$, but this common value $\neq f(p)$. "Removable" because redefining $f(p)$ fixes it.

Jump discontinuity (first kind): Both one-sided limits exist but $f(p^+)\neq f(p^-)$. There is a "jump."

Discontinuity of the second kind (oscillation): At least one one-sided limit fails to exist. The most severe â€” the function doesn't settle from at least one side.
Examples of Each Type
Removable: $f(x)=\sin(x)/x$ at $x=0$ (limit is $1$, but $f(0)$ undefined or wrong).

Jump: $h(x)=|x|/x=\text{sgn}(x)$ at $x=0$. $h(0^+)=1$, $h(0^-)=-1$. Jump of size $2$.

Oscillation: $f(x)=\sin(1/x)$ at $x=0$. Neither $f(0^+)$ nor $f(0^-)$ exists.
The Popcorn Function â€” a Subtle Example (PS6)
Define $p:[0,1]\to[0,1]$ by $p(x)=1/q$ if $x=m/q$ in lowest terms, $p(x)=0$ if $x$ is irrational. Continuous at every irrational, discontinuous at every rational. The discontinuities at rationals are all removable (the limit is $0$). Striking: continuous on a dense set but discontinuous on another dense set.

02 Monotone Functions

Main Idea
Monotone functions are extraordinarily well-controlled: their discontinuities can only be of jump type, and there cannot be too many of them. The key tool: each jump at $x$ creates a nonempty open interval $(f(x^-),f(x^+))$ in the range. Different jumps give disjoint intervals â€” which forces countability.
One-Sided Limits Always Exist (Rudin Â§4.29)
Let $f$ be monotonically increasing on $(a,b)$. Then for every $x\in(a,b)$: $$f(x^-)=\sup_{t<x}f(t)\leq f(x)\leq\inf_{t>x}f(t)=f(x^+)$$ The sandwich $f(x^-)\leq f(x)\leq f(x^+)$ shows monotone functions can only have jump discontinuities â€” no oscillation possible.
Theorem (Rudin Â§4.30) â€” At Most Countably Many Discontinuities
Let $f$ be monotonically increasing on $(a,b)$. The set of discontinuities of $f$ is at most countable.

Proof: At each discontinuity $x$, define $I_x=(f(x^-),f(x^+))$. If $x<y$ are two discontinuities, $f(x^+)\leq f(y^-)$ (since $f$ is increasing), so $I_x$ and $I_y$ are disjoint. Each $I_x$ contains a rational $q_x\in\mathbb{Q}$ (by density). This gives an injection from discontinuities into $\mathbb{Q}$. Since $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable, the set of discontinuities is countable.
Why This Is Surprising
Monotone functions can be discontinuous at every rational and continuous at every irrational â€” or discontinuous at any countable set you choose. But you can never have uncountably many: the jumps force disjoint intervals in the range, and $\mathbb{R}$ can only contain countably many disjoint intervals with positive length.

01 Quick Drills â€” True / False

T/FIf $\lim_{x\to p}f(x)=L$, then $f(p)=L$.
False

T/FIf $f$ and $g$ are each discontinuous at $p$, then $f+g$ is discontinuous at $p$.
False â€” discontinuities can cancel
Let $f=\mathbf{1}_\mathbb{Q}$ and $g=1-\mathbf{1}_\mathbb{Q}$. Both are discontinuous everywhere, but $f+g\equiv 1$ is continuous everywhere.
T/FEvery uniformly continuous function on $(0,1)$ is bounded.
True
Apply uniform continuity with $\varepsilon=1$ to get $\delta>0$. Cover $(0,1)$ by finitely many intervals of width $\delta/2$. On each, $|f|$ is bounded by $|f(\text{ref point})|+1$. The max over finitely many reference points plus $1$ gives a global bound.
T/FIf $f:[a,b]\to[a,b]$ is continuous, then $f$ has a fixed point.
True â€” apply IVT to $g(x)=f(x)-x$
$g(a)=f(a)-a\geq 0$ (since $f(a)\in[a,b]$) and $g(b)=f(b)-b\leq 0$. By IVT, $\exists\,c$ with $g(c)=0$, i.e., $f(c)=c$.

ProblemAnalyze where each function is continuous: $f(x)=|x|$; $g(x)=0$ on $\mathbb{Q}$, $g(x)=x$ on $\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{Q}$; $h(x)=|x|/x$; the metric $d:X\times X\to\mathbb{R}$; the Cantor function; the popcorn function. PS6 Ex.8
1. $|x|$ â€” continuous everywhere
$||x|-|p||\leq|x-p|$, so $\delta=\varepsilon$ works. Lipschitz with constant $1$.
2. $g$ â€” continuous only at $x=0$
At $0$: $|g(x)|\leq|x|<\varepsilon$ whenever $|x|<\varepsilon$. At $p\neq 0$: approach along rationals ($g\to 0$) and irrationals ($g\to p\neq 0$). Sequential criterion fails.
3. $h=\text{sgn}(x)$ â€” continuous on $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$, jump at $0$
Constant ($\pm 1$) on each half-line. At $0$: $h(0^+)=1\neq-1=h(0^-)$. Jump discontinuity of size $2$.
4. Metric $d$ â€” continuous everywhere on $X\times X$
$|d(x,y)-d(p,q)|\leq d(x,p)+d(y,q)$ by the triangle inequality. Take $\delta=\varepsilon/2$ in each coordinate.
5. Cantor function â€” continuous everywhere on $[0,1]$
Monotone increasing, constant on each middle-thirds interval. One-sided limits exist everywhere (monotone). One shows $c(x^+)=c(x^-)$ at every point, so no discontinuities. Uniformly continuous on $[0,1]$ yet $c'=0$ a.e.
6. Popcorn function â€” continuous at irrationals, discontinuous at rationals
At irrational $t$: for given $\varepsilon$, only finitely many rationals $m/q$ have $1/q\geq\varepsilon$. Choose $\delta$ avoiding them. At rational $r$: approach along irrationals $x_n\to r$ â€” $p(x_n)=0\not\to p(r)=1/q>0$.
ProblemShow $f(x)=x^2$ is continuous but not uniformly continuous on $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$. Show $g(x)=\sqrt{x}$ is uniformly continuous but not Lipschitz on $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$. PS7 Ex.1
$f=x^2$: continuous
$|x^2-p^2|=|x-p||x+p|$. Near $p$: if $|x-p|<1$ then $|x+p|<2p+1$. Take $\delta=\min(1,\varepsilon/(2p+1))$. This $\delta$ depends on $p$.
$f=x^2$: NOT uniformly continuous
Take $x_n=n$, $y_n=n+1/(2n)$. Then $|x_n-y_n|=1/(2n)\to 0$. But $|x_n^2-y_n^2|=|x_n-y_n||x_n+y_n|=\frac{1}{2n}(2n+\frac{1}{2n})\to 1\neq 0$. No single $\delta$ handles all $n$.
$g=\sqrt{x}$: uniformly continuous
For $x,y\geq 0$: $|\sqrt{x}-\sqrt{y}|\leq\sqrt{|x-y|}$. Set $\delta=\varepsilon^2$. Then $|x-y|<\delta\Rightarrow|\sqrt{x}-\sqrt{y}|\leq\sqrt{|x-y|}<\varepsilon$. One $\delta$ works everywhere.
$g=\sqrt{x}$: NOT Lipschitz at $0$
If $|\sqrt{x}-0|\leq L|x-0|$ for all $x>0$, then $1/\sqrt{x}\leq L$ for all $x>0$. But $1/\sqrt{x}\to\infty$ as $x\to 0^+$. No finite $L$ works. $\blacksquare$
ProblemLet $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ be monotonically increasing. Show that the set of discontinuities is at most countable. PS7 Ex.2
Proof
Since $f$ is increasing, $f(x^-)=\sup_{t<x}f(t)$ and $f(x^+)=\inf_{t>x}f(t)$ exist for every $x$, with $f(x^-)\leq f(x)\leq f(x^+)$. The function is discontinuous at $x$ iff $J(x):=f(x^+)-f(x^-)>0$.

For each discontinuity $x$, define $I_x=(f(x^-),f(x^+))$. Claim: if $x<y$ are both discontinuities, then $I_x\cap I_y=\emptyset$. Indeed, since $f$ is increasing and $x<y$, we have $f(x^+)\leq f(y^-)$, so $I_x$ lies entirely to the left of $I_y$.

Each $I_x$ is a nonempty open interval, hence contains a rational $q_x\in\mathbb{Q}$. Since the $I_x$ are pairwise disjoint, $x\mapsto q_x$ is an injection into $\mathbb{Q}$. Since $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable, the set of discontinuities is countable. $\blacksquare$
ProblemLet $(X,d)$ be a metric space and $A\subseteq X$ path-connected. Show $A$ is connected. PS7 Ex.5
Proof
Suppose for contradiction that $A$ is not connected. Then there exist open sets $U,V$ in $X$ such that $A\subseteq U\cup V$, $A\cap U\neq\emptyset$, $A\cap V\neq\emptyset$, and $U\cap V=\emptyset$.

Pick $p\in A\cap U$ and $q\in A\cap V$. Since $A$ is path-connected, there exists a continuous $\gamma:[0,1]\to A$ with $\gamma(0)=p$ and $\gamma(1)=q$.

Let $S=\gamma^{-1}(U\cap A)$ and $T=\gamma^{-1}(V\cap A)$. Since $U$ and $V$ are open, $S$ and $T$ are open in $[0,1]$ by continuity of $\gamma$. They are disjoint, their union is $[0,1]$, $0\in S$ and $1\in T$ so both are nonempty. This contradicts the connectedness of $[0,1]$. $\blacksquare$
ProblemShow $T=\{(x,\sin(1/x)):x\in(0,1]\}\cup(\{0\}\times[-1,1])$ is connected but not path-connected. PS7 Ex.6
Part 1: $T$ is connected
Let $S=\{(x,\sin(1/x)):x\in(0,1]\}$. The map $\phi(x)=(x,\sin(1/x))$ is continuous, and $(0,1]$ is connected, so $S$ is connected.

Claim: $\{0\}\times[-1,1]\subseteq\overline{S}$. For any $c\in[-1,1]$, write $c=\sin\theta_0$ and set $x_n=1/(\theta_0+2\pi n)$. Then $(x_n,\sin(1/x_n))=(x_n,c)\to(0,c)$. So every point of $\{0\}\times[-1,1]$ is a limit of points of $S$.

Thus $T\subseteq\overline{S}$ and $S\subseteq T$, so $T=\overline{S}$. The closure of a connected set is connected. $\blacksquare$
Part 2: $T$ is not path-connected
Suppose $\gamma=(\gamma_1,\gamma_2):[0,1]\to T$ is continuous with $\gamma(0)=(0,0)$ and $\gamma(1)\in S$ (so $\gamma_1(1)>0$). Define $t_0=\sup\{t\in[0,1]:\gamma_1(t)=0\}$. By continuity, $\gamma_1(t_0)=0$. For all $t>t_0$, $\gamma_1(t)>0$, so $\gamma_2(t)=\sin(1/\gamma_1(t))$.

As $t\to t_0^+$, $\gamma_1(t)\to 0^+$, so $1/\gamma_1(t)\to+\infty$. The function $\sin(1/\gamma_1(t))$ oscillates between $-1$ and $+1$ infinitely â€” so $\lim_{t\to t_0^+}\gamma_2(t)$ does not exist, contradicting continuity of $\gamma_2$ at $t_0$. $\blacksquare$
R1Let $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ be continuous with $f(x)=0$ for all $x\in\mathbb{Q}$. Show that $f\equiv 0$. RUDIN
Proof
Let $x\in\mathbb{R}$. Since $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$, there exists a sequence of rationals $q_n\to x$. By hypothesis $f(q_n)=0$ for all $n$. By continuity of $f$ at $x$: $f(x)=\lim_{n\to\infty}f(q_n)=0$. Since $x$ was arbitrary, $f\equiv 0$. $\blacksquare$

Remark: A continuous function is completely determined by its values on any dense subset.
R2Let $f:(a,b)\to\mathbb{R}$ be uniformly continuous. Show that $f$ is bounded. RUDIN
Proof
By uniform continuity with $\varepsilon=1$, there exists $\delta>0$ such that $|x-y|<\delta\Rightarrow|f(x)-f(y)|<1$.

Cover $(a,b)$ by finitely many subintervals of length $\delta/2$. On each subinterval $(x_k,x_{k+1})$, any two points are within $\delta$ of each other, so $|f(x)-f(x_k)|<1$, giving $|f(x)|\leq|f(x_k)|+1$.

Let $M=\max_k|f(x_k)|+1$. Then $|f(x)|\leq M$ for all $x\in(a,b)$, so $f$ is bounded. $\blacksquare$
