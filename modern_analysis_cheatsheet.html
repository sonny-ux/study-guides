Chapter 1

The Real & Complex Number Systems

Ordered fields · LUB property · Archimedean · Density · Cauchy-Schwarz

Ordered Sets & Fields
Ordered Set

A set $S$ with relation $\lt$ satisfying: (i) trichotomy — exactly one of $x\lt y$, $x=y$, $y\lt x$ holds; (ii) transitivity — $x\lt y$ and $y\lt z\Rightarrow x\lt z$.

LUB Property

Every nonempty $E\subseteq S$ that is bounded above has a least upper bound (supremum) in $S$.

$\mathbb{R}$ has the LUB property. $\mathbb{Q}$ does not: $\{q\in\mathbb{Q}:q^2\lt 2\}$ is bounded above in $\mathbb{Q}$ but its sup $(\sqrt{2})$ is not in $\mathbb{Q}$.

Trap

The LUB property is an axiom of $\mathbb{R}$, not something you prove. It's what distinguishes $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$ and is the engine behind every completeness argument.

Field

Set with $+$ and $\cdot$ satisfying commutativity, associativity, distributivity, identities ($0,1$), and inverses. $\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{Q}$ are ordered fields. $\mathbb{C}$ is a field but cannot be ordered compatibly with arithmetic.

Trap — No ordering on ℂ

If $i\gt 0$, then $i\cdot i\gt 0$, i.e. $-1\gt 0$, contradiction. If $i\lt 0$, then $-i\gt 0$, so $(-i)(-i)=-1\gt 0$, same contradiction.

Sup & Inf — The Machinery

$\alpha=\sup E$ iff: (i) $\alpha\geq e$ for all $e\in E$; (ii) if $\beta\lt\alpha$ then $\exists e\in E$ with $e\gt\beta$.

Equivalently: $\alpha=\sup E$ iff $\alpha$ is an upper bound AND $\forall\varepsilon>0,\;\exists e\in E: e>\alpha-\varepsilon$.

$\inf(-E)=-\sup E$. If $E\subseteq F$ and both have sups, then $\sup E\leq\sup F$.

The Move — Proving α = sup E

Step 1: Show $\alpha$ is an upper bound ($\alpha\geq e$ for all $e\in E$).

Step 2: For any $\varepsilon>0$, exhibit an element of $E$ greater than $\alpha-\varepsilon$ (usually by direct construction or Archimedean).

Trap — Ambient Set Matters

$\sup E$ depends on which ordered set $E$ lives in. Always specify. $\sup\{q\in\mathbb{Q}:q^2\lt 2\}$ doesn't exist in $\mathbb{Q}$ but equals $\sqrt{2}$ in $\mathbb{R}$.

Archimedean & Density
Archimedean:$\forall x\in\mathbb{R},\;\exists n\in\mathbb{N}$ with $n>x$. Equivalently, $\inf\{1/n:n\in\mathbb{N}\}=0$.
Density of $\mathbb{Q}$:Between any $x\lt y$ in $\mathbb{R}$, $\exists p\in\mathbb{Q}$ with $x\lt p\lt y$. Also $\exists$ irrationals in $(x,y)$.
Density Proof

Find $n\in\mathbb{N}$ with $n(y-x)\gt 1$ (Archimedean). Then $\exists m\in\mathbb{Z}$ with $nx\lt m\leq nx+1\leq ny$. Set $p=m/n\in(x,y)$.

Complex Numbers & Cauchy-Schwarz
Complex Arithmetic
Form:$z=x+iy$, $\bar z=x-iy$, $|z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$
Key id:$z\bar z=|z|^2$; $|zw|=|z||w|$
Divide:$z/w = z\bar w/|w|^2$ — multiply top & bottom by $\bar w$
Triangle:$|z+w|\leq|z|+|w|$; reverse: $\bigl||z|-|w|\bigr|\leq|z-w|$
Extended $\mathbb{R}$:$\infty-\infty$, $0\cdot\infty$ undefined. $\sup\emptyset=-\infty$, $\inf\emptyset=+\infty$.
Cauchy-Schwarz (Thm 1.35)
$$\left|\sum_{j=1}^n a_j\overline{b_j}\right|^2\leq\left(\sum_{j=1}^n|a_j|^2\right)\!\left(\sum_{j=1}^n|b_j|^2\right)$$

Proof: set $B=\sum|b_j|^2$. If $B=0$, all $b_j=0$ and the inequality is trivial. Otherwise let $\lambda=\sum a_j\overline{b_j}$ and expand $0\leq\sum|Ba_j-\lambda b_j|^2=B^2\sum|a_j|^2-B|\lambda|^2$. Divide by $B$.

Proof Strategies — Ch 1
  • Show $\sqrt{2}\notin\mathbb{Q}$: suppose $p/q$ in lowest terms, get $p^2=2q^2$, so $p$ even, write $p=2m$, then $q^2=2m^2$, so $q$ even — contradicts $\gcd(p,q)=1$.
  • $\inf\{1/n\}=0$: for any $\varepsilon>0$, Archimedean gives $n>1/\varepsilon$, so $1/n<\varepsilon$. Zero is a lower bound (trivially). Hence $\inf=0$.
  • To show $\sup E=\alpha$: upper bound is usually easy. For tightness, if someone claims $\alpha-\varepsilon$ is an upper bound, produce an element of $E$ exceeding it.
  • $\mathbb{R}$ is uncountable: Cantor diagonal. $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable: list as $p/q$ with $|p|+q\leq n$.

Basic Topology

Metric spaces · Open/closed · Compact · Heine-Borel · Connected · Cantor

Metric Spaces & Open/Closed Sets
Metric Space $(X,d)$

$d:X\times X\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfies: (i) $d(x,y)\geq 0$, $=0\iff x=y$; (ii) $d(x,y)=d(y,x)$; (iii) $d(x,z)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,z)$.

Open ball:$B_r(x)=\{y\in X:d(x,y)\lt r\}$
Open set:every point is interior: $\forall p\in E,\;\exists r>0: B_r(p)\subseteq E$
Closed set:contains all its limit points ($E^c$ is open)
Limit point:every $B_r(p)$ contains a point of $E\setminus\{p\}$
Closure $\bar E$:$E\cup E'$ = smallest closed set $\supseteq E$
Interior $E^\circ$:largest open set $\subseteq E$
Union/Intersection Rules
  • Arbitrary union of open sets is open.
  • Finite intersection of open sets is open.
  • Arbitrary intersection of closed sets is closed.
  • Finite union of closed sets is closed.
Trap — Open ≠ Not Closed

Open and closed are not opposites. Sets can be: open only, closed only, both (clopen), or neither. $\mathbb{R}$ and $\emptyset$ are clopen. $[a,b)$ is neither. $\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty(-1/n,\,1/n)=\{0\}$ is closed — infinite intersections of open sets can be closed.

Compactness

$K$ is compact if every open cover of $K$ has a finite subcover.

Key Facts

Thm 2.34: Compact $\Rightarrow$ closed and bounded (in any metric space).

Heine-Borel (2.41, $\mathbb{R}^k$): $E$ compact $\iff$ $E$ closed and bounded. Only in $\mathbb{R}^k$!

Seq. compact (2.37): In metric spaces, compact $\iff$ every sequence has a convergent subsequence with limit in $K$.

B-W (2.42): Every bounded infinite subset of $\mathbb{R}^k$ has a limit point in $\mathbb{R}^k$.

Trap — Heine-Borel fails in ∞-dim

Closed unit ball in $\ell^2$ is closed and bounded but not compact — the sequence $e_n$ (standard basis) has no convergent subsequence.

Cantor Intersection & Nested Sets

Thm 2.36: If $\{K_n\}$ is a sequence of nonempty compact sets with $K_1\supseteq K_2\supseteq\cdots$, then $\bigcap K_n\neq\emptyset$.

If additionally $\text{diam}(K_n)\to 0$, the intersection consists of exactly one point.

The Move — Proving Compact

In $\mathbb{R}^k$: show closed (complement open, or sequences in $E$ converging in $\mathbb{R}^k$ land in $E$) and bounded (all points within radius $R$ of origin). To show not compact: exhibit a sequence with no convergent subsequence, or an open cover with no finite subcover.

Connectedness & Special Sets
Connectedness

$E$ is connected if it cannot be written as $A\cup B$ where $A,B$ are nonempty and separated: $\bar A\cap B=\emptyset$ and $A\cap\bar B=\emptyset$.

$\mathbb{R}$ is connected. The connected subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ are exactly the intervals (including rays and $\mathbb{R}$ itself). $\mathbb{Q}$ is not connected.

Trap

Compact $\not\Rightarrow$ connected ($\{0,1\}$ is compact, not connected). Connected $\not\Rightarrow$ compact ($\mathbb{R}$ is connected, not compact).

Perfect Sets & Cantor Set

Perfect set: closed + every point is a limit point. No isolated points.

Every perfect set in $\mathbb{R}^k$ is uncountable (Thm 2.43).

Cantor set: Remove open middle thirds from $[0,1]$ iteratively. Result is: perfect, nowhere dense, uncountable, has measure zero, totally disconnected. The canonical example of a "large" set that is topologically negligible.

Countability Facts
  • $\mathbb{N},\mathbb{Z},\mathbb{Q}$: countable. $\mathbb{R}$: uncountable (Cantor diagonal).
  • Countable union of countable sets is countable.
  • Any perfect set is uncountable. Algebraic numbers: countable.

Sequences & Series

Cauchy · limsup/liminf · Convergence tests · Power series

Sequences
Convergence

$a_n\to L$ iff $\forall\varepsilon>0,\;\exists N\in\mathbb{N}: n\geq N\Rightarrow|a_n-L|\lt\varepsilon$. Limit is unique.

Bounded + monotone $\Rightarrow$ convergent (in $\mathbb{R}$; uses LUB property).

Subsequences: $a_n\to L\Rightarrow$ every subsequence $\to L$. Two subsequences with different limits $\Rightarrow$ $a_n$ diverges.

Cauchy Sequences

$\{a_n\}$ is Cauchy iff $\forall\varepsilon>0,\;\exists N: m,n\geq N\Rightarrow|a_m-a_n|\lt\varepsilon$.

In $\mathbb{R}^k$: Cauchy $\iff$ convergent (completeness). In $\mathbb{Q}$: Cauchy $\not\Rightarrow$ convergent.

Every convergent sequence is Cauchy. Every Cauchy sequence is bounded.

The Move — Proving Convergence Without Knowing Limit

Show the sequence is Cauchy: estimate $|a_m-a_n|$ by telescoping or geometric series, show it $\to 0$. In $\mathbb{R}$ this is enough.

limsup & liminf

Let $s_n^*=\sup\{a_n,a_{n+1},\ldots\}$ and $s_{n*}=\inf\{a_n,a_{n+1},\ldots\}$. Then:

$$\limsup_{n\to\infty}a_n=\lim_{n\to\infty}s_n^*=\inf_n s_n^*$$ $$\liminf_{n\to\infty}a_n=\lim_{n\to\infty}s_{n*}=\sup_n s_{n*}$$

Always defined in extended $\mathbb{R}$ (even for divergent sequences). $\liminf\leq\limsup$ always.

$a_n\to L$ $\iff$ $\limsup a_n=\liminf a_n=L$.

$\limsup a_n$ is the largest subsequential limit; $\liminf$ is the smallest.

Trap

$\limsup$ and $\liminf$ always exist (possibly $\pm\infty$). Use them for divergent sequences too. $(-1)^n$: $\limsup=1$, $\liminf=-1$.

Series & Convergence Tests
Series Basics

$\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n$ converges iff partial sums $S_N=\sum_{n=1}^N a_n$ converge. Necessary: $a_n\to 0$ (contrapositive: $a_n\not\to 0\Rightarrow$ diverges).

Absolute convergence: $\sum|a_n|\lt\infty\Rightarrow\sum a_n$ converges. Converse false: $\sum(-1)^n/n$ converges conditionally.

Power Series

$\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_nz^n$, $c_n,z\in\mathbb{C}$. Radius of convergence:

$$R=\frac{1}{\limsup_{n\to\infty}|c_n|^{1/n}}$$

Converges absolutely for $|z|\lt R$; diverges for $|z|\gt R$; boundary $|z|=R$ requires separate analysis.

On any $|z|\leq r\lt R$, convergence is uniform. The sum is a continuous function of $z$ on $|z|\lt R$.

Chapter 4

Continuity

Limits · Continuous functions · Compactness · Uniform continuity · IVT · Discontinuities

Definitions & Global Characterization
Limit of a Function

$\lim_{x\to p}f(x)=q$ iff $\forall\varepsilon>0,\;\exists\delta>0: 0\lt d_X(x,p)\lt\delta\Rightarrow d_Y(f(x),q)\lt\varepsilon$.

$x=p$ is excluded from the limit condition. $p$ must be a limit point of the domain.

Continuity at a Point

$f:X\to Y$ is continuous at $p$ iff $\lim_{x\to p}f(x)=f(p)$:

$\forall\varepsilon>0,\;\exists\delta>0: d_X(x,p)\lt\delta\Rightarrow d_Y(f(x),f(p))\lt\varepsilon$

Composition of continuous functions is continuous. Arithmetic of continuous real functions is continuous.

Global Characterization (Thm 4.8)

$f:X\to Y$ is continuous $\iff$ $f^{-1}(V)$ is open in $X$ for every open $V\subseteq Y$.

Equivalently: $f^{-1}(F)$ is closed in $X$ for every closed $F\subseteq Y$.

Key Consequences

Thm 4.14: $f$ continuous, $K\subseteq X$ compact $\Rightarrow f(K)$ compact.

Thm 4.16: $f$ continuous on compact $K$ $\Rightarrow$ $f$ attains its max and min on $K$.

Thm 4.22: $f$ continuous, $E\subseteq X$ connected $\Rightarrow f(E)$ connected.

Trap — Images of Closed Sets

$f(\text{compact})$ = compact ✓. But $f(\text{closed})$ need not be closed: $f(x)=1/x$ maps the closed set $[1,\infty)$ to $(0,1]$, which is not closed.

Uniform Continuity & IVT
Uniform Continuity

$f:X\to Y$ is uniformly continuous iff $\forall\varepsilon>0,\;\exists\delta>0:\forall x,y\in X,$

$d_X(x,y)\lt\delta\Rightarrow d_Y(f(x),f(y))\lt\varepsilon$

One $\delta$ works for all pairs simultaneously. Uniform $\Rightarrow$ continuous; converse fails on non-compact sets.

Thm 4.19

Continuous on compact $\Rightarrow$ uniformly continuous.

Proof: if not, find $p_n,q_n$ with $d(p_n,q_n)\lt 1/n$ but $d(f(p_n),f(q_n))\geq\varepsilon_0$. Compactness gives subsequence of $p_n$ converging to some $p^*$; then $q_n\to p^*$ too; but $f$ continuous at $p^*$ gives $f(p_n),f(q_n)\to f(p^*)$, contradicting the gap.

Trap — x² on ℝ

$f(x)=x^2$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$ but not uniformly continuous: $x_n=n+1/n$, $y_n=n$ give $|x_n-y_n|=1/n\to 0$ but $|x_n^2-y_n^2|=2+1/n^2\geq 2$.

Intermediate Value Theorem (4.23)

$f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ continuous, $f(a)\lt\gamma\lt f(b)$ $\Rightarrow\exists c\in(a,b): f(c)=\gamma$.

IVT Proof Move

Let $A=\{x\in[a,b]:f(x)\lt\gamma\}$. Set $c=\sup A$ (exists since $A$ nonempty and bounded).

If $f(c)\gt\gamma$: continuity gives $\delta$ with $f(x)\gt\gamma$ on $(c-\delta,c+\delta)$, contradicting $c=\sup A$.

If $f(c)\lt\gamma$: continuity gives points beyond $c$ still in $A$, contradicting $c$ as sup.

So $f(c)=\gamma$.

Differentiation

Derivative · Chain rule · Local extrema · MVT · L'Hôpital · Taylor · Darboux

The Derivative & Algebra
Definition (5.1)

$f'(x)=\lim_{t\to x}\dfrac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}$ (Rudin's form — $t$ lives in the domain).

Equivalent $h\to 0$ form: $f'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$.

Thm 5.2: Differentiable at $x\Rightarrow$ continuous at $x$. Converse false.

Proof: $f(t)-f(x)=\frac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}\cdot(t-x)\to f'(x)\cdot 0=0$ as $t\to x$.

Trap — Converse is False

$f(x)=|x|$ is continuous at 0 but not differentiable: $f'_+(0)=1\neq-1=f'_-(0)$. Weierstrass function: continuous everywhere, differentiable nowhere.

Algebra of Derivatives
Sum:$(f+g)'=f'+g'$
Product:$(fg)'=f'g+fg'$
Quotient:$(f/g)'=(f'g-fg')/g^2$ (where $g\neq 0$)
Chain:$(g\circ f)'(x)=g'(f(x))\cdot f'(x)$
Product Rule Proof Move

Write $f(t)g(t)-f(x)g(x)=[f(t)-f(x)]g(t)+f(x)[g(t)-g(x)]$. Divide by $t-x$; the second piece → $f(x)g'(x)$; first piece → $f'(x)g(x)$ since $g(t)\to g(x)$ (differentiable ⇒ continuous).

Chain Rule Proof Move (No Division by Zero)

Define auxiliary: $q(t)=\frac{g(f(t))-g(f(x))}{f(t)-f(x)}$ when $f(t)\neq f(x)$, and $q(t)=g'(f(x))$ otherwise. Then $q$ is continuous at $t=x$ and $g(f(t))-g(f(x))=q(t)[f(t)-f(x)]$ always. Divide by $t-x$ and take limit.

Local Extrema (Thm 5.8)

If $f$ has a local max or min at $x\in(a,b)$ and $f'(x)$ exists, then $f'(x)=0$.

Proof: from the right, $(f(t)-f(x))/(t-x)\leq 0$ (since $f(t)\leq f(x)$ near max), so $f'(x)\leq 0$. From the left, ratio $\geq 0$, so $f'(x)\geq 0$. Hence $f'(x)=0$.

Trap — f'(x)=0 ≠ Extremum

$f(x)=x^3$ at $x=0$: $f'(0)=0$ but $f$ is strictly increasing — no extremum. The theorem is one-directional: extremum $\Rightarrow f'=0$, not $f'=0\Rightarrow$ extremum.

Darboux's Theorem (5.12)

If $f'$ exists on $[a,b]$, then $f'$ has the intermediate value property — even if $f'$ is not continuous.

Consequence: $f'$ cannot have jump discontinuities. If $f'(a)\neq f'(b)$, then $f'$ takes every value between them.

Trap

A derivative need not be continuous (it can have essential discontinuities), but it can never have jump discontinuities. Darboux rules that out.

MVT, L'Hôpital & Taylor
Mean Value Theorem (5.11)

$f$ continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$ $\Rightarrow\exists c\in(a,b)$:

$$f(b)-f(a)=f'(c)(b-a)$$

Proof via Rolle's (5.10): apply to $h(t)=f(t)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(t-a)$; $h(a)=h(b)=0$; by Rolle $\exists c: h'(c)=0$.

Key Applications

$f'\equiv 0$ on $(a,b)$ $\Rightarrow f$ constant. $|f'|\leq M$ $\Rightarrow |f(b)-f(a)|\leq M|b-a|$ (Lipschitz). $f'\geq 0$ $\Rightarrow f$ nondecreasing.

Cauchy MVT (5.26): $\exists c$: $[f(b)-f(a)]g'(c)=[g(b)-g(a)]f'(c)$. Used to derive L'Hôpital.

L'Hôpital's Rule (5.13)

If $f,g\to 0$ as $x\to a$ (or $g\to\pm\infty$), $g'(x)\neq 0$ near $a$, and $\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}=L$, then $\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=L$.

Trap — Check f'/g' First

L'Hôpital requires $f'/g'\to L$ to hold. If $f'/g'$ oscillates or diverges, no conclusion about $f/g$ (even if $f/g$ converges). Never apply L'Hôpital without verifying the limit of $f'/g'$ first.

Taylor's Theorem (5.15)

$f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$, $f^{(n)}$ exists on $[a,b]$. For $\alpha,\beta\in[a,b]$:

$$f(\beta)=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{f^{(k)}(\alpha)}{k!}(\beta-\alpha)^k+\frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(\beta-\alpha)^{n+1}$$

where $c$ lies strictly between $\alpha$ and $\beta$. The remainder is exact (Lagrange form).

Taylor Proof Move

Define $M$ by $f(\beta)=P_n(\beta)+M(\beta-\alpha)^{n+1}$ (the polynomial is the sum). Apply Rolle $n+1$ times to $g(t)=f(\beta)-P_n^\alpha(t)-M(\beta-t)^{n+1}$ (which vanishes at $t=\alpha$ and $t=\beta$ with appropriate conditions), yielding $g^{(n+1)}(c)=0$. Solve: $M=f^{(n+1)}(c)/(n+1)!$.

Trap — n! vs (n+1)!

The remainder is $f^{(n+1)}(c)/(n+1)!$, not $f^{(n+1)}(c)/n!$. The polynomial terms use $k!$ but the remainder uses $(n+1)!$.

Chapter 6

The Riemann-Darboux Integral

Darboux sums · Riemann criterion · FTC · Lebesgue criterion

Partitions & Integrability
Setup

Partition $P=\{x_0\lt x_1\lt\cdots\lt x_n\}$ of $[a,b]$; $\Delta x_k=x_k-x_{k-1}$.

$M_k=\sup_{[x_{k-1},x_k]}f$, $m_k=\inf_{[x_{k-1},x_k]}f$.

$$U(P,f)=\sum_{k=1}^n M_k\Delta x_k,\qquad L(P,f)=\sum_{k=1}^n m_k\Delta x_k$$ $$\overline{\int_a^b}f=\inf_P U(P,f),\qquad\underline{\int_a^b}f=\sup_P L(P,f)$$

$f\in\mathscr{R}$ iff $\overline{\int}f=\underline{\int}f$. Both always exist (bounded $f$ + completeness of $\mathbb{R}$).

Refinement & Riemann Criterion

Refinement Lemma (6.4): $P\subseteq P^*\Rightarrow L(P)\leq L(P^*)\leq U(P^*)\leq U(P)$.

Adding points narrows the gap. Corollary (6.5): For any two partitions $P_1,P_2$: $L(P_1)\leq U(P_2)$.

Proof of corollary: form $P^*=P_1\cup P_2$; apply refinement lemma to each.

Riemann Criterion (6.6): $f\in\mathscr{R}$ $\iff$ $\forall\varepsilon>0,\;\exists P: U(P,f)-L(P,f)<\varepsilon$.

The Move — Proving Integrability

Construct an explicit partition (e.g. uniform $P_n$ with $n$ subintervals). Bound the gap: $U-L=\sum(M_k-m_k)\Delta x_k$. Use uniform continuity (for continuous $f$) or monotonicity to bound $M_k-m_k$ on each piece. Choose $n$ large enough that the total gap $<\varepsilon$.

Trap

The partition $P$ in the Riemann criterion can depend on $\varepsilon$ — you do NOT need one $P$ for all $\varepsilon$ simultaneously. A finer partition for smaller $\varepsilon$ is perfectly fine.

Integrability Theorems
Sufficient Conditions for f ∈ ℛ

Thm 6.8: $f$ continuous on $[a,b]$ $\Rightarrow$ $f\in\mathscr{R}$. (Continuity on compact $\Rightarrow$ uniform continuity; use Riemann criterion.)

Thm 6.9: $f$ monotone on $[a,b]$ $\Rightarrow$ $f\in\mathscr{R}$. Uniform partition with $n$ points gives $U-L=(f(b)-f(a))/n\to 0$.

Thm 6.11: $f\in\mathscr{R}$, $\phi$ continuous on range of $f$ $\Rightarrow\phi\circ f\in\mathscr{R}$. In particular: $f^2\in\mathscr{R}$, $|f|\in\mathscr{R}$.

Lebesgue criterion: $f\in\mathscr{R}$ $\iff$ $f$ bounded and discontinuous on a set of measure zero.

Trap — Dirichlet Function

$f=\mathbf{1}_\mathbb{Q}$ (1 on rationals, 0 on irrationals): bounded, discontinuous everywhere — NOT in $\mathscr{R}$ (both $U\equiv 1$ and $L\equiv 0$ for any partition). Modified Dirichlet ($f(p/q)=1/q$ in lowest terms, $f(\text{irrationals})=0$): IS in $\mathscr{R}$ — discontinuities only at rationals, a countable (measure zero) set.

Properties & Fundamental Theorem
Properties of ∫
  • Linearity: $\int(f+g)=\int f+\int g$; $\int cf=c\int f$.
  • Monotone: $f\leq g$ on $[a,b]$ $\Rightarrow\int_a^b f\leq\int_a^b g$.
  • Triangle: $\left|\int_a^b f\right|\leq\int_a^b|f|$.
  • Additivity: $\int_a^b f=\int_a^c f+\int_c^b f$ for $c\in[a,b]$.
  • Product: $f,g\in\mathscr{R}\Rightarrow fg\in\mathscr{R}$.
Integration by Parts & Substitution
$$\int_a^b f'g\,dx = \bigl[fg\bigr]_a^b - \int_a^b fg'\,dx$$

Requires $f',g'\in\mathscr{R}$ and $f,g$ continuous.

$$\int_a^b f(\phi(t))\phi'(t)\,dt=\int_{\phi(a)}^{\phi(b)}f(x)\,dx$$

Requires $\phi'$ continuous and $f$ continuous on range of $\phi$.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

FTC Part 1 (6.20): Let $f\in\mathscr{R}[a,b]$ and $F(x)=\int_a^x f$. Then $F$ is continuous on $[a,b]$. If $f$ is continuous at $x_0$, then $F'(x_0)=f(x_0)$.

Proof of differentiability: $\frac{F(x)-F(x_0)}{x-x_0}=\frac{1}{x-x_0}\int_{x_0}^x f(t)\,dt$. For $|t-x_0|\lt|x-x_0|$: $\left|\frac{1}{x-x_0}\int_{x_0}^x(f(t)-f(x_0))dt\right|\leq\sup_{|t-x_0|\leq|x-x_0|}|f(t)-f(x_0)|\to 0$ by continuity of $f$ at $x_0$.

FTC Part 2 (6.21): $f\in\mathscr{R}[a,b]$, $F$ continuous on $[a,b]$, $F'=f$ on $(a,b)$ $\Rightarrow$

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a)$$

Proof: given $\varepsilon$, pick $P$ with $U-L\lt\varepsilon$. MVT gives $F(x_k)-F(x_{k-1})=f(t_k)\Delta x_k$ for some $t_k$. Then $F(b)-F(a)=\sum f(t_k)\Delta x_k\in[L(P),U(P)]$, and so does $\int f$, so $|F(b)-F(a)-\int f|\lt\varepsilon$.

Trap — Volterra Function

A function $F$ can be differentiable everywhere with $F'=f$ bounded, yet $f\notin\mathscr{R}$. FTC Part 2 requires both $F'=f$ AND $f\in\mathscr{R}$. Also: $F'$ existing everywhere does NOT guarantee $F'\in\mathscr{R}$.

Proof Moves — At a Glance
Universal Moves
  • Show sup/inf: upper/lower bound + tightness ($\forall\varepsilon$, exhibit element in $(α-ε, α]$).
  • Cauchy = convergent in $\mathbb{R}$: show $|a_m-a_n|\to 0$ to prove convergence without knowing the limit.
  • Compactness in $\mathbb{R}^k$: show closed + bounded (or sequential compactness).
  • Continuity globally: preimage of every open set is open.
  • IVT: let $A=\{x:f(x)\lt\gamma\}$, take $c=\sup A$, show $f(c)=\gamma$ by ruling out $\gt$ and $\lt$.
  • MVT application: bound $|f(b)-f(a)|$ by $\max|f'|\cdot|b-a|$.
  • Riemann integrability: construct partition, bound $U-L=\sum(M_k-m_k)\Delta x_k$.
  • FTC2 proof: MVT on each subinterval, telescope, compare to Riemann sum.
