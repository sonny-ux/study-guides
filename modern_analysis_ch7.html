00 What This Problem Set Is About

Chapter 7 of Rudin is one of the most conceptually rich chapters in the book, and it sits at the heart of real analysis. The central question it asks is deceptively simple: when you have a sequence of functions $f_n \to f$, which properties of the $f_n$ carry over to the limit $f$? Is $f$ continuous if each $f_n$ is? Is $f$ integrable? Differentiable? Can you swap a limit with an integral or derivative?

The answer, it turns out, depends critically on what kind of convergence you have. Pointwise convergence alone is too weak to preserve most analytic properties. The right notion is uniform convergence — where the functions don't just converge at each individual point, but converge at the same rate everywhere simultaneously. This single upgrade from "pointwise" to "uniform" is what unlocks the theorems about continuity of limits, integrability of limits, and differentiability of limits.

PS9 tests your understanding of these ideas from several angles. Exercise 1 probes why products of uniformly convergent sequences need an extra boundedness assumption. Exercise 2 asks whether a natural space of functions is complete under a weaker norm. Exercise 3 uses equicontinuity to force a function to be constant — a surprising consequence of a very strong uniformity assumption. Exercise 4 is the integral test, which at its core is a comparison between a sum and an integral when both live under the same monotone decreasing function. Exercise 5 is the theorem that power series can be differentiated term-by-term — one of the central theorems of the chapter. Exercise 6 upgrades pointwise boundedness to uniform boundedness using compactness and equicontinuity.

The theory pages (tabs §1–§3) cover the essential background in depth. Read those first, then go to the Exercises tab, where each problem begins with a preparation section before the problem statement.

Theory § 1

Uniform Convergence

The engine of Chapter 7 — definitions, the key theorems, and why they matter

1.1 Pointwise vs. Uniform Convergence

Before we can understand uniform convergence, we need to be precise about what pointwise convergence says — and why it falls short. A sequence of functions $f_n : E \to \mathbb{R}$ converges pointwise to a function $f : E \to \mathbb{R}$ if, for every fixed $x \in E$, the real sequence $f_n(x)$ converges to $f(x)$. In other words, pick any $x$ and ask: does $f_n(x) \to f(x)$? If yes for every $x$, the convergence is pointwise.

The problem with this definition is that the rate of convergence is allowed to vary wildly from point to point. For a given $\varepsilon > 0$ and a given $x$, there is some threshold $N(x, \varepsilon)$ after which $|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$. But $N$ may depend on $x$ in an uncontrolled way — as $x$ ranges over $E$, the threshold $N(x, \varepsilon)$ might shoot off to infinity, meaning that no matter how large $n$ is, there are always some points where $f_n$ and $f$ are far apart.

Definition — Pointwise Convergence

$f_n \to f$ pointwise on $E$ means: for every $x \in E$ and every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N = N(x, \varepsilon)$ such that $n \geq N$ implies $|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$.

Definition 7.7 — Uniform Convergence

$f_n \to f$ uniformly on $E$ means: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N = N(\varepsilon)$ (independent of $x$) such that for all $n \geq N$ and all $x \in E$, $$|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon.$$

Equivalently, $\sup_{x \in E} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$.

The critical upgrade is that $N$ no longer depends on $x$. Once $n \geq N(\varepsilon)$, the function $f_n$ is within $\varepsilon$ of $f$ simultaneously at every point. Geometrically, $f_n$ lies inside a uniform $\varepsilon$-tube around $f$ for all large $n$.

Reading the Sup Formulation

The equivalent formulation $\sup_{x \in E} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \to 0$ is extremely useful. Define $\|g\|_\infty = \sup_{x \in E}|g(x)|$ — this is called the supremum norm or uniform norm. Then uniform convergence is exactly convergence in this norm: $\|f_n - f\|_\infty \to 0$. The space of bounded continuous functions on a compact set is complete under this norm — this is Rudin Theorem 7.15. By contrast, the $L^1$ norm $\|g\|_{L^1} = \int|g|$ gives a weaker notion where this completeness fails (see Exercise 2).

A canonical example to hold in mind

Consider $f_n(x) = x^n$ on $[0, 1]$. The pointwise limit is $f(x) = 0$ for $x \in [0,1)$ and $f(1) = 1$. Each $f_n$ is continuous, but the limit $f$ is not continuous at $x = 1$ — it jumps. This is the paradigm case showing that pointwise convergence does not preserve continuity. But the convergence is not uniform: $\sup_{x \in [0,1]} |x^n - f(x)| = 1$ for every $n$ (the sup is nearly achieved near $x = 1$). By contrast, on $[0, r]$ for any $r < 1$, convergence is uniform, because $\sup_{x \in [0,r]} x^n = r^n \to 0$.

1.2 The Cauchy Criterion for Uniform Convergence

Just as Cauchy sequences in $\mathbb{R}$ converge without needing to know the limit in advance, there is a Cauchy criterion for uniform convergence. This is often the easiest way to prove uniform convergence in practice, since you do not need an explicit formula for the limit.

Theorem 7.8 (Cauchy Criterion) — Rudin

$\{f_n\}$ converges uniformly on $E$ if and only if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $N$ such that for all $m, n \geq N$ and all $x \in E$, $$|f_n(x) - f_m(x)| < \varepsilon.$$

This is the uniform version of the real Cauchy criterion. The proof of the "if" direction proceeds by first using the pointwise Cauchy criterion at each $x$ to get a pointwise limit $f$, then upgrading to uniform convergence by a careful estimate. The "only if" direction is just the triangle inequality.

1.3 Uniform Convergence Preserves Continuity

Here is the central theorem that makes uniform convergence worth caring about. If each $f_n$ is continuous and convergence is uniform, then the limit is continuous. This fails for pointwise convergence, as the example $x^n$ on $[0,1]$ shows.

Theorem 7.12 — Continuity of the Uniform Limit

Suppose $f_n \to f$ uniformly on $E$ and each $f_n$ is continuous at a point $x_0 \in E$. Then $f$ is continuous at $x_0$.

Proof sketch (the "split the $\varepsilon$ in three" argument):

Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. By uniform convergence, choose $N$ so that $|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon/3$ for all $x$, $n \geq N$. By continuity of $f_N$ at $x_0$, choose $\delta$ so that $|x - x_0| < \delta$ implies $|f_N(x) - f_N(x_0)| < \varepsilon/3$. Then: $$|f(x) - f(x_0)| \leq |f(x) - f_N(x)| + |f_N(x) - f_N(x_0)| + |f_N(x_0) - f(x_0)| < \frac{\varepsilon}{3} + \frac{\varepsilon}{3} + \frac{\varepsilon}{3} = \varepsilon.$$

Why the three-term split is the standard move

This "$\varepsilon/3$ argument" (or $\varepsilon/3$ split) appears constantly in Chapter 7. You cannot go directly from $f$ to $f(x_0)$ because you do not know $f$ is continuous — that is what you are trying to prove. Instead, you route through a known-continuous approximation $f_N$. The uniform convergence controls the outer two terms globally (same $N$ works for every $x$), while continuity of $f_N$ controls the middle term locally near $x_0$. Recognize this pattern: it is used in essentially the same form in Exercise 1, Exercise 3, and Exercise 6.

1.4 Algebra of Uniform Limits — Products

Uniform convergence is compatible with the usual algebraic operations on functions — sums, scalar multiples, and (under a boundedness assumption) products. This is what Exercise 1 probes directly.

Uniform Convergence is Preserved Under Sums

If $f_n \to f$ and $g_n \to g$ uniformly on $E$, then $f_n + g_n \to f + g$ uniformly. This is immediate: $|(f_n + g_n) - (f + g)| \leq |f_n - f| + |g_n - g|$, and the right side goes to $0$ uniformly.

Uniform Convergence of Products — Requires Boundedness

If $f_n \to f$ and $g_n \to g$ uniformly, and both sequences are uniformly bounded (there exists $M$ with $|f_n(x)| \leq M$ and $|g_n(x)| \leq M$ for all $x$ and all $n$), then $f_n g_n \to fg$ uniformly.

Key identity: $$f_n g_n - fg = (f_n - f)g_n + f(g_n - g).$$ So $|f_n g_n - fg| \leq |f_n - f| \cdot |g_n| + |f| \cdot |g_n - g|.$

Boundedness controls $|g_n|$ and $|f|$ (bounded by $M$ and the limit also bounded by $M$), turning this into $M|f_n - f| + M|g_n - g| \to 0$ uniformly.

Without Boundedness, Products Can Fail

If $f_n$ and $g_n$ are unbounded (even though each uniformly converges), the product $f_n g_n$ can fail to converge uniformly. The term $|f_n - f| \cdot |g_n|$ cannot be bounded because $|g_n|$ has no uniform upper bound — the decay of $|f_n - f|$ can be cancelled by the growth of $|g_n|$. Exercise 1(b) asks for a concrete example of exactly this failure.

1.5 Completeness of $C(K)$ Under the Sup Norm

One of the most important structural facts about uniform convergence is that the space of continuous functions on a compact set is complete under the sup norm. This means that any Cauchy sequence of continuous functions (in the sup norm sense) converges uniformly to a continuous function. Nothing "escapes" the space.

Theorem 7.15 — Rudin (Completeness of $C(K)$)

Let $K$ be a compact metric space. The space $\mathscr{C}(K)$ of continuous functions $f: K \to \mathbb{R}$, equipped with the norm $\|f\|_\infty = \sup_{x \in K}|f(x)|$, is a complete metric space.

Proof idea: If $\{f_n\}$ is Cauchy in $\mathscr{C}(K)$, then for each $x$ the sequence $f_n(x)$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb{R}$ (since $|f_n(x) - f_m(x)| \leq \|f_n - f_m\|_\infty \to 0$). By completeness of $\mathbb{R}$, define $f(x) = \lim f_n(x)$ pointwise. The Cauchy condition in sup norm upgrades this to uniform convergence ($N$ works for all $x$ simultaneously). Uniform convergence of continuous functions gives a continuous limit (Thm 7.12). So $f \in \mathscr{C}(K)$ and $\|f_n - f\|_\infty \to 0$.

The key insight: the sup norm is precisely calibrated to make this work. The $L^1$ norm does not have this property — see Exercise 2, where you will show $C_b([0,1])$ is incomplete under the $L^1$ metric.

Theory § 2

Equicontinuity

Controlling an entire family of functions simultaneously

2.1 What Equicontinuity Means

Continuity is a property of a single function at a single point: for a given $\varepsilon$, you find a $\delta$ that works for that function at that location. Uniform continuity removes the dependence on the point: a single $\delta$ works everywhere on the domain for a given function. Equicontinuity goes one step further: a single $\delta$ works simultaneously for every function in a family and every point in the domain.

To see why this matters, imagine you have infinitely many functions $f_n$ and you want to say something about their collective behavior. If each $f_n$ is just individually continuous, you have no control over the family as a whole — the $\delta$ for $f_1$ at a point might be $1$, while the $\delta$ for $f_n$ at the same point might be $1/n$, shrinking to zero as $n$ grows. Equicontinuity forbids this deterioration.

Definition 7.22 — Equicontinuity (Rudin)

A family $\mathscr{F}$ of functions $f: X \to \mathbb{R}$ is equicontinuous at a point $x_0 \in X$ if: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $f \in \mathscr{F}$ and all $x \in X$ with $d(x, x_0) < \delta$, $$|f(x) - f(x_0)| < \varepsilon.$$

The family is uniformly equicontinuous (on $X$) if: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $f \in \mathscr{F}$ and all $x, y \in X$ with $d(x, y) < \delta$, $$|f(x) - f(y)| < \varepsilon.$$

Parsing the Quantifier Order

In uniform equicontinuity, the order of quantifiers is: $\forall \varepsilon > 0$, $\exists \delta > 0$, $\forall f \in \mathscr{F}$, $\forall x, y$ with $d(x,y) < \delta$: $|f(x) - f(y)| < \varepsilon$. The $\delta$ is chosen before $f$ is specified and before $x, y$ are chosen. A single $\delta$ controls oscillation for the entire family at once. This is the "uniform" in uniform equicontinuity — uniform over both the family and the domain.

Equicontinuity is closely connected to compactness of function families. The Arzelà–Ascoli theorem (Rudin Theorem 7.25) says that on a compact space, a family that is both pointwise bounded and equicontinuous is totally bounded in $C(K)$ — every sequence has a uniformly convergent subsequence. This is the function-space analogue of the Bolzano–Weierstrass theorem. Exercise 6 uses equicontinuity to prove uniform boundedness, which is one ingredient of Arzelà–Ascoli.

2.2 Worked Example: Detecting Equicontinuity

Consider the family $\{f_n\}$ where $f_n(x) = \sin(nx)$ on $\mathbb{R}$. Is this family equicontinuous? No. For any fixed $\delta > 0$, we can choose $x, y$ with $|x - y| < \delta$ such that $|f_n(x) - f_n(y)| = |\sin(nx) - \sin(ny)|$ is close to $2$ for large $n$, because the frequency of oscillation grows without bound. So no single $\delta$ can work for all $n$.

Now consider $\{f_n\}$ where $f_n(x) = x^n/n$ on $[0,1]$. The derivative is $f_n'(x) = x^{n-1}$, which is bounded by $1$ on $[0,1]$ for all $n$. By the mean value theorem, $|f_n(x) - f_n(y)| \leq |x - y|$ for all $n$. So given $\varepsilon > 0$, taking $\delta = \varepsilon$ works for every $n$. This family is uniformly equicontinuous.

Lipschitz Condition Implies Equicontinuity

If a family $\{f_n\}$ satisfies $|f_n(x) - f_n(y)| \leq L|x - y|$ for a single constant $L$ and all $n, x, y$, then the family is uniformly equicontinuous: take $\delta = \varepsilon/L$. This is the most common way equicontinuity arises in practice.

2.3 Equicontinuity and Compactness

The following theorem, related to Arzelà–Ascoli, is directly relevant to Exercise 6. When you have equicontinuity plus a pointwise bound on a compact space, you can upgrade to a uniform bound. The key trick is covering the compact space by finitely many $\delta$-balls (using compactness), choosing a center in each ball where you have a pointwise bound, and then using equicontinuity to transfer that bound to the whole ball.

Key Lemma (relevant to Ex 6)

Let $(X, d)$ be compact, and let $\{f_n\}$ be uniformly equicontinuous and pointwise bounded. Then $\{f_n\}$ is uniformly bounded: there exists $C$ with $|f_n(x)| \leq C$ for all $n$ and all $x \in X$.

Proof sketch: Take $\varepsilon = 1$. By uniform equicontinuity, find $\delta$ such that $d(x,y) < \delta \Rightarrow |f_n(x) - f_n(y)| < 1$ for all $n$. Since $X$ is compact, cover it by finitely many open balls $B(x_1, \delta), \ldots, B(x_k, \delta)$. At each center $x_i$, by pointwise boundedness, there exists $M_i$ with $|f_n(x_i)| \leq M_i$ for all $n$. Set $M = \max_i M_i$. For any $x \in X$, $x$ lies in some ball $B(x_i, \delta)$, so $|f_n(x)| \leq |f_n(x) - f_n(x_i)| + |f_n(x_i)| < 1 + M$. Thus $C = M + 1$ works for all $n$ and $x$.

Theory § 3

Series of Functions & Power Series

Term-by-term operations, radius of convergence, and differentiation

3.1 Convergence of Series of Functions

A series of functions $\sum_{n=0}^\infty f_n(x)$ converges pointwise to $S(x)$ if the partial sums $S_N(x) = \sum_{n=0}^N f_n(x)$ converge pointwise. It converges uniformly if the partial sums converge uniformly. The key test for uniform convergence of series is the Weierstrass $M$-test.

Theorem 7.10 — Weierstrass M-test

Suppose $|f_n(x)| \leq M_n$ for all $x \in E$ and all $n$, and $\sum M_n$ converges. Then $\sum f_n$ converges uniformly (and absolutely) on $E$.

Why: The tail $|\sum_{k=N}^\infty f_k(x)| \leq \sum_{k=N}^\infty M_k$, and the right side goes to $0$ uniformly in $x$ as $N \to \infty$ (since $\sum M_n < \infty$ means its tail tends to $0$).

The Weierstrass $M$-test is the workhorse for power series. Inside a compact subset of the disk of convergence, the terms $|c_n x^n| \leq |c_n| r^n$ for some $r < R$, and $\sum|c_n|r^n$ converges. So power series converge uniformly on compacta inside the radius of convergence.

3.2 Power Series and Radius of Convergence

A power series is a series of the form $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n$, where the $c_n$ are coefficients (possibly complex) and $x$ is a real variable. The fundamental fact is that there is a single number $R \in [0, \infty]$ — the radius of convergence — such that the series converges absolutely for $|x| < R$ and diverges for $|x| > R$. Behavior at $|x| = R$ is subtle and depends on the specific series.

Theorem 3.39 (Hadamard formula) — Rudin

The radius of convergence of $\sum c_n x^n$ is $$R = \frac{1}{\limsup_{n \to \infty} |c_n|^{1/n}}.$$ (With the conventions $1/0 = \infty$ and $1/\infty = 0$.)

This formula comes from the root test applied to $|c_n x^n| = (|c_n|^{1/n} |x|)^n$: the series converges when $\limsup |c_n|^{1/n} |x| < 1$, i.e., when $|x| < 1/\limsup |c_n|^{1/n} = R$.

Reading the Radius: What $R$ Tells You

Think of $R$ as a threshold. If $|x| < R$, the power series $\sum c_n x^n$ converges absolutely — you can rearrange terms, integrate term by term, differentiate term by term. If $|x| > R$, the terms $c_n x^n$ do not even tend to $0$, so the series diverges. On the circle $|x| = R$ itself, anything can happen: $\sum x^n/n$ converges at $x = -1$ but diverges at $x = 1$; $\sum x^n/n^2$ converges at both endpoints; $\sum x^n$ diverges at both.

3.3 Differentiation of Power Series

This is one of the most remarkable theorems about power series, and it is the content of Exercise 5. A power series $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n$ with radius of convergence $R$ can be differentiated term by term: the derivative is $f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n c_n x^{n-1}$, and this differentiated series has the same radius of convergence $R$.

Why is this remarkable? In general, you cannot differentiate a series term by term just because it converges uniformly — you additionally need the series of derivatives to converge uniformly. For power series, the algebra of the radius of convergence works out exactly so that both conditions are satisfied simultaneously inside the disk of convergence.

Theorem 7.17 — Rudin (Differentiation of Power Series)

Let $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n$ have radius of convergence $R > 0$. Then:

(1) The differentiated series $g(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n c_n x^{n-1}$ has radius of convergence $R$.

(2) $f$ is differentiable on $(-R, R)$ and $f'(x) = g(x)$ for all $x \in (-R, R)$.

Why the Radius Stays the Same

The radius of the differentiated series $\sum n c_n x^{n-1}$ is determined by $\limsup |n c_n|^{1/n}$. Now, $|n c_n|^{1/n} = n^{1/n} \cdot |c_n|^{1/n}$. The key fact is that $n^{1/n} \to 1$ as $n \to \infty$ (check: $n^{1/n} = e^{(\ln n)/n} \to e^0 = 1$). Therefore $\limsup|nc_n|^{1/n} = \limsup|c_n|^{1/n}$, and the radius is unchanged.

Proof Strategy for Exercise 5
1
Show the differentiated series has radius $R$.
Use Hadamard: $\limsup|nc_n|^{1/n} = \limsup n^{1/n}|c_n|^{1/n} = 1 \cdot \limsup|c_n|^{1/n}$ since $n^{1/n} \to 1$.
2
Fix $x_0 \in (-R, R)$ and choose $r$ with $|x_0| < r < R$.
The buffer $r$ gives room. Both $\sum c_n r^n$ and $\sum n c_n r^{n-1}$ converge absolutely, giving uniform convergence on $[-r, r]$.
3
Apply Rudin Theorem 7.17: the partial sums $S_N$ of $f$ converge uniformly on $[-r,r]$, and their derivatives $S_N'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^N nc_n x^{n-1}$ also converge uniformly there.
The key condition for term-by-term differentiation (Rudin Thm 7.17 antecedent): each $S_N$ is differentiable, $S_N \to f$ pointwise, and $S_N' \to g$ uniformly. Under these conditions, $f$ is differentiable and $f' = g$.
4
For the Weierstrass M-test on $[-r,r]$: $|nc_n x^{n-1}| \leq nc_n r^{n-1}$, and $\sum nc_n r^{n-1}$ converges since the differentiated series has radius $R > r$.
So the $M$-test applies to the series of derivatives, giving the required uniform convergence of $S_N'$.
Term-by-term differentiation: the general theorem it rests on

Rudin Theorem 7.17 (the one about sequences, not power series specifically) says: if $f_n$ are differentiable on $[a,b]$, $f_n(x_0) \to f(x_0)$ for some $x_0$, and $f_n' \to g$ uniformly on $[a,b]$, then $f_n \to f$ uniformly and $f' = g$. This is the general engine. For power series, we verify its hypotheses using the M-test and the radius-of-convergence computation.

T/FThe power series $\sum_{n=1}^\infty x^n/n$ and its derivative $\sum_{n=1}^\infty x^{n-1} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n$ have the same radius of convergence $R = 1$.
True
For $c_n = 1/n$: $\limsup|c_n|^{1/n} = \limsup(1/n)^{1/n} = 1$ (since $n^{1/n} \to 1$, so $(1/n)^{1/n} \to 1$ too). For $nc_n = 1$: $\limsup|nc_n|^{1/n} = 1$. Same radius. Note that behavior at the boundary differs: $\sum x^n/n$ converges at $x=-1$ (alternating harmonic series) but $\sum x^n$ diverges there.

3.4 The Integral Test — Background

The integral test (Exercise 4) connects the convergence of a series $\sum f(n)$ to the convergence of an improper integral $\int_1^\infty f(x)\,dx$ when $f$ is nonnegative and decreasing. The mechanism is a simple monotone comparison: since $f$ is decreasing, $f$ is bounded below by its value at the right endpoint of each unit interval and above by its value at the left endpoint. This traps each term $f(n)$ between two integrals over an interval of length $1$.

Setup for the Integral Test

Since $f$ is nonneg and decreasing on $[n, n+1]$: $f(n+1) \leq f(x) \leq f(n)$ for all $x \in [n, n+1]$. Integrating over $[n, n+1]$: $$f(n+1) \leq \int_n^{n+1} f(x)\,dx \leq f(n).$$ Summing from $n = 1$ to $N-1$ gives the two key inequalities that link partial sums of the series to partial integrals.

From these inequalities you get: $\sum_{n=2}^N f(n) \leq \int_1^N f(x)\,dx \leq \sum_{n=1}^{N-1} f(n)$. As $N \to \infty$, both the partial sums and the integral either both stay bounded (and both converge) or both blow up (and both diverge). This is the equivalence the problem asks you to prove.

Ex 1 Uniform Convergence of Products

Preparation

This problem has two parts that tell opposite stories. Part (a) says: under boundedness, products behave as nicely as sums — uniform convergence is preserved. Part (b) says: drop boundedness and the product can fail to converge uniformly, even if both factors converge uniformly.

The core algebraic identity for products is the add-and-subtract trick: $$f_n g_n - fg = (f_n - f) g_n + f(g_n - g).$$ You arrive at this by writing $f_n g_n - fg = f_n g_n - f g_n + f g_n - fg = (f_n - f)g_n + f(g_n - g)$. This decomposes the error into two pieces. To bound the whole thing you need each term to go to zero uniformly.

For part (a), the boundedness assumption ensures that $|g_n|$ and $|f|$ are bounded by some constant $M$, making both pieces small: $M \cdot |f_n - f|$ and $M \cdot |g_n - g|$, each going to zero uniformly. For part (b), you need an example where one of these "weights" is unbounded, so the product of small and large doesn't go to zero.

Key tools: Part (a) uses the algebraic identity above, plus the triangle inequality, plus the uniform bound hypothesis. You will need to establish that the limit $f$ is also bounded — uniform limits of bounded functions are bounded (pass $|f_n(x)| \leq M$ through the limit at each $x$). Part (b): choose $f_n(x) = g_n(x) = 1/x + 1/n$ on $(0,1)$. Each converges uniformly to $1/x$. But $|f_ng_n - 1/x^2| = |2/(nx) + 1/n^2|$ and $\sup_{x \in (0,1)} 2/(nx) = \infty$ for every $n$. So the product does not converge uniformly.

Common pitfall: For part (a), when you bound $|g_n|$ via the triangle inequality $|g_n| \leq |g_n - g| + |g|$, you need $|g|$ bounded — make sure you establish this before invoking it. The cleaner route is to use $|g_n| \leq M$ directly from the hypothesis (boundedness of $\{g_n\}$), and separately note that $|f| \leq M$ since $f$ is a pointwise limit of functions bounded by $M$.

Proof Outline — Part (a)
1
Let $M$ be a uniform bound: $|f_n(x)| \leq M$ and $|g_n(x)| \leq M$ for all $n, x$.
Establish that the limits $f, g$ are also bounded by $M$ (pass the inequality through the limit at each $x$).
2
Write $|f_n g_n - fg| \leq |f_n - f| \cdot |g_n| + |f| \cdot |g_n - g| \leq M|f_n - f| + M|g_n - g|$.
Both terms go to $0$ uniformly: for any $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N$ so that $n \geq N$ gives $|f_n - f| < \varepsilon/(2M)$ everywhere and $|g_n - g| < \varepsilon/(2M)$ everywhere.
3
Then for $n \geq N$ and all $x$: $|f_n(x)g_n(x) - f(x)g(x)| \leq M \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2M} + M \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2M} = \varepsilon$.
This $N$ depends only on $\varepsilon$ and $M$, not on $x$. So $f_n g_n \to fg$ uniformly. $\square$

Ex 2 Completeness of $C_b([0,1])$ Under the $L^1$ Metric

Preparation

This problem asks you to determine whether a specific metric space is complete. Recall what completeness means: a metric space $(X, d)$ is complete if every Cauchy sequence in $X$ converges to a point that is also in $X$. The space here is $C_b([0,1])$, the bounded continuous functions on $[0,1]$, but equipped with the $L^1$ metric $d_{L^1}(f, g) = \int_0^1 |f(x) - g(x)|\,dx$ rather than the sup metric.

The answer is no — this space is not complete. To prove incompleteness, you need to exhibit a sequence of functions $f_n \in C_b([0,1])$ that is Cauchy in $d_{L^1}$ but whose $L^1$-limit is not in $C_b([0,1])$, meaning the limit is either discontinuous or does not exist as a bounded continuous function.

The key insight is that the $L^1$ norm is much weaker than the sup norm. A sequence can be Cauchy in $L^1$ — meaning the functions get close in average absolute value — while the pointwise behavior near a single point is terrible. A spike of height $1$ but width $1/n$ carries $L^1$ weight $1/n \to 0$ but causes the sup-norm to stay at $1$.

The standard counterexample: Define $f_n : [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}$ as the piecewise-linear function that ramps linearly from $0$ to $1$ on $[1/2 - 1/n, 1/2]$ and holds at $1$ on $[1/2, 1]$, and is $0$ on $[0, 1/2 - 1/n]$. Each $f_n$ is continuous and bounded. The pointwise limit is the step function $f = \mathbf{1}_{[1/2,1]}$, which is discontinuous at $1/2$. The $L^1$ distance $\|f_n - f\|_{L^1} \leq 1/n \to 0$, confirming the sequence is Cauchy (in fact $L^1$-convergent) in $C_b([0,1])$ but its limit lies outside the space.

Direction of argument: To show incompleteness you must exhibit a Cauchy sequence and show it has no limit inside $C_b([0,1])$. It is not enough to find a sequence that fails to converge uniformly — that is a different statement. Use uniqueness of $L^1$ limits: if $d_{L^1}(f_n, g) \to 0$ for some $g \in C_b([0,1])$, then $g = f$ a.e. But $g$ is continuous and $f$ is not, contradiction.

Contrast with Theorem 7.15: $C(K)$ under the sup norm is complete. The sup norm forces every term to be within $\varepsilon$ everywhere simultaneously, which passes continuity to the limit. The $L^1$ norm is too forgiving near a single bad point — it lets that point slide by unpenalized as long as the "bad region" shrinks.

Proof Outline: $(C_b([0,1]), d_{L^1})$ is not complete
1
Define $f_n$ as above (the ramp sequence). Show each $f_n$ is continuous and bounded, hence $f_n \in C_b([0,1])$.
Continuous: each piece is linear and they join continuously. Bounded: $0 \leq f_n \leq 1$.
2
Show $\{f_n\}$ is Cauchy in $d_{L^1}$. For $m \geq n$, $f_m$ and $f_n$ differ only on $[\tfrac{1}{2}-\tfrac{1}{n}, \tfrac{1}{2}]$, an interval of length $1/n$, where both functions are bounded by $1$. So $d_{L^1}(f_m, f_n) \leq 2/n \to 0$.
Alternatively: both $f_n$ and $f_m$ are $L^1$-close to the step function $f$, so they are $L^1$-close to each other by the triangle inequality.
3
Show that any $L^1$-limit must be the step function $f = \mathbf{1}_{[1/2,1]}$ (a.e.), which is not continuous.
Suppose $g \in C_b([0,1])$ with $d_{L^1}(f_n, g) \to 0$. Then $f_n \to g$ in $L^1$, but also $f_n \to f$ in $L^1$. By uniqueness of $L^1$ limits, $g = f$ a.e. But $g$ is continuous and $f$ is not, contradiction.
4
Conclude: the Cauchy sequence $\{f_n\}$ has no limit in $C_b([0,1])$, so the space is not complete.
$\square$

Ex 3 Equicontinuity Forces $f$ to Be Constant

Preparation

This is one of the most elegant problems on the set. The setup is: $f : [0, \infty) \to \mathbb{R}$, and we define $f_n(x) = f(nx)$ — a sequence obtained by rescaling the argument by $n$. So $f_1$ is $f$ itself, $f_2$ is $f$ run "twice as fast" (compressed horizontally by a factor of $2$), and so on. The assumption is that the family $\{f_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ is uniformly equicontinuous. The conclusion is that $f$ must be constant.

To see why this is surprising: if $f$ is not constant, there exist points $a, b \in [0, \infty)$ with $f(a) \neq f(b)$. When we apply the rescaling $x \mapsto nx$, the distance between any two inputs shrinks by a factor of $n$. Specifically, if we take $x = a/n$ and $y = b/n$, then $f_n(x) = f(a)$ and $f_n(y) = f(b)$, but $|x - y| = |a - b|/n \to 0$. So for large $n$, the points $x$ and $y$ are very close together, but $f_n(x)$ and $f_n(y)$ are still a fixed distance apart. This directly contradicts equicontinuity.

Key tool: The argument is a direct $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ contradiction. Take $\varepsilon = |f(a) - f(b)|/2 > 0$. By uniform equicontinuity, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $n$ and all $x, y$ with $|x - y| < \delta$: $|f_n(x) - f_n(y)| < \varepsilon$. Now choose $n$ large enough that $|a - b|/n < \delta$, and set $x = a/n, y = b/n$. Then $|x - y| = |a-b|/n < \delta$, but $f_n(x) = f(a)$ and $f_n(y) = f(b)$, so $|f_n(x) - f_n(y)| = |f(a) - f(b)| = 2\varepsilon > \varepsilon$ — contradiction.

Common pitfall: The hypothesis is on the family $\{f_n\}$, not on $f$ alone. Do not assume $f$ itself is uniformly continuous — that would be circular. The equicontinuity of the rescaled family is what constrains $f$. Also note: you do not need $f$ to be continuous — the hypothesis gives what you need about the $f_n$'s, which evaluate $f$ at rescaled points.

Proof Outline
1
Suppose for contradiction that $f$ is not constant: there exist $a, b \in [0, \infty)$ with $f(a) \neq f(b)$. Set $\varepsilon_0 = |f(a) - f(b)|/2 > 0$.
2
By uniform equicontinuity of $\{f_n\}$: for this $\varepsilon_0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and all $x, y \in [0,\infty)$ with $|x - y| < \delta$, we have $|f_n(x) - f_n(y)| < \varepsilon_0$.
3
Choose $n$ large enough that $|a - b|/n < \delta$ (possible since $|a-b|$ is fixed). Set $x = a/n$ and $y = b/n$. Then $|x - y| = |a - b|/n < \delta$.
4
Compute: $f_n(x) = f(n \cdot a/n) = f(a)$ and $f_n(y) = f(n \cdot b/n) = f(b)$. So $|f_n(x) - f_n(y)| = |f(a) - f(b)| = 2\varepsilon_0 > \varepsilon_0$.
This contradicts the equicontinuity condition applied to $x, y$ with $|x - y| < \delta$. The contradiction shows $f$ must be constant. $\square$

Ex 4 The Integral Test

Preparation

The integral test says that if $f : [1, \infty) \to \mathbb{R}$ is nonnegative and monotonically decreasing, then $\int_1^\infty f(x)\,dx$ converges if and only if $\sum_{n=1}^\infty f(n)$ converges. The key mechanism is the monotone comparison: on each unit interval $[n, n+1]$, since $f$ is decreasing, $f(n+1) \leq f(x) \leq f(n)$ for all $x$ in that interval. Integrating over the interval (of length $1$) gives $f(n+1) \leq \int_n^{n+1} f(x)\,dx \leq f(n)$.

The proof pattern: Sum this inequality from $n = 1$ to $N-1$. The middle sum telescopes to $\int_1^N f(x)\,dx$. The left sum is $\sum_{n=2}^N f(n)$ and the right sum is $\sum_{n=1}^{N-1} f(n)$. These two inequalities — the partial integral sandwiched between two shifted partial sums — are the entire content of the proof. Once you have them, each direction of the "if and only if" is just a monotone convergence argument using nonnegativity of $f$.

Key tools: (1) The definition of series convergence via partial sums $S_N = \sum_{n=1}^N c_n$ (recall Chapter 3). (2) The improper integral definition: $\int_1^\infty f := \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_1^b f$. (3) The monotone comparison inequality derived above. (4) The fact that for nonneg terms, partial sums are nondecreasing, so convergence is equivalent to boundedness above.

Convergence vs. equality: The integral and the series both converge or both diverge, but they need not be equal. For example, $\sum_{n=1}^\infty 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$ (the Basel problem) while $\int_1^\infty 1/x^2\,dx = 1$. The test is about convergence/divergence, not about computing values.

Convergence of a Series — The Definition

The series $\sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n$ converges if the sequence of partial sums $S_N = \sum_{n=1}^N c_n$ converges (i.e., $\lim_{N \to \infty} S_N$ exists and is finite). If $S_N \to \infty$ or the limit does not exist, the series diverges. For nonneg terms, the partial sums are nondecreasing, so convergence is equivalent to $S_N$ being bounded above.

Proof of the Equivalence
1
From the monotone inequality on $[n, n+1]$: $f(n+1) \leq \int_n^{n+1} f \leq f(n)$. Sum $n = 1$ to $N-1$: $$\sum_{n=2}^{N} f(n) \leq \int_1^N f(x)\,dx \leq \sum_{n=1}^{N-1}f(n).$$
2
($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $\int_1^\infty f$ converges to some $L < \infty$. Then for all $N$: $\sum_{n=2}^N f(n) \leq \int_1^N f \leq L$. Since $f \geq 0$, adding the nonneg term $f(1)$: $\sum_{n=1}^N f(n) \leq f(1) + L < \infty$. The partial sums are bounded above, so $\sum f(n)$ converges.
3
($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $\sum_{n=1}^\infty f(n)$ converges to some $S < \infty$. Then for all $N$: $\int_1^N f \leq \sum_{n=1}^{N-1}f(n) \leq S$. The function $F(b) = \int_1^b f$ is nondecreasing and bounded above by $S$, so $\lim_{b \to \infty} F(b)$ exists and is finite. Hence $\int_1^\infty f$ converges.
4
Both directions established. The integral converges if and only if the series converges. $\square$
Note: the two limits need not be equal. In general, $\sum f(n)$ and $\int_1^\infty f$ differ by $O(f(1))$. The theorem is about convergence/divergence, not about the values.
T/FIf $f : [1,\infty) \to \mathbb{R}$ is nonneg and decreasing, and $\sum f(n)$ converges, then $\int_1^\infty f\,dx$ converges to the same value as $\sum f(n)$.
False
The integral and the series both converge, but they need not be equal. For example, $f(x) = 1/x^2$: $\sum_{n=1}^\infty 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$ (Basel problem), while $\int_1^\infty 1/x^2\,dx = 1$. Same convergence, different values. The integral test is a convergence test, not a computation of the sum.

Ex 5 Differentiation of Power Series

Preparation

This problem asks you to prove Rudin Theorem 7.17 for power series: if $\sum c_n x^n$ has radius of convergence $R > 0$, then $f$ is differentiable on $(-R, R)$ and $f'(x) = \sum n c_n x^{n-1}$. There are two parts to this: first showing the differentiated series has radius $R$, then showing $f' = \sum nc_n x^{n-1}$ inside that radius.

Radius of the differentiated series: By the Hadamard formula, the radius is $1/\limsup|nc_n|^{1/n}$. Now $|nc_n|^{1/n} = n^{1/n} \cdot |c_n|^{1/n}$. The crucial fact is $n^{1/n} \to 1$ (since $n^{1/n} = e^{(\ln n)/n} \to e^0 = 1$). Therefore $\limsup|nc_n|^{1/n} = \limsup|c_n|^{1/n}$, and the radius is unchanged.

Showing $f' = g$: Fix $x_0 \in (-R, R)$. Choose $r$ with $|x_0| < r < R$ — this buffer is essential. Apply the Weierstrass $M$-test to the series $g(x) = \sum nc_n x^{n-1}$ on $[-r, r]$: bound $|nc_n x^{n-1}| \leq n|c_n|r^{n-1}$, and $\sum n|c_n|r^{n-1}$ converges since the differentiated series has radius $R > r$. So $g$ converges uniformly on $[-r, r]$. Now apply Rudin Thm 7.17 for sequences: the partial sums $S_N$ are polynomials (differentiable), $S_N \to f$ pointwise, and $S_N' \to g$ uniformly on $[-r,r]$. Conclusion: $f$ is differentiable at $x_0$ and $f'(x_0) = g(x_0)$. Since $r < R$ was arbitrary, this holds on all of $(-R, R)$.

Recognizing the proof pattern: This is the general engine from Rudin §7.17: if $f_n \to f$ (pointwise at one point) and $f_n' \to g$ uniformly on $[a,b]$, then $f_n \to f$ uniformly and $f' = g$. For power series, the $f_n$ are the partial sums $S_N(x) = \sum_{n=0}^N c_n x^n$, which are polynomials. You verify the hypotheses using the $M$-test.

Common mistake: Do not try to apply the $M$-test on all of $(-R, R)$ at once. Power series need not converge uniformly on the full open interval — only on compact subsets. The strategy of working on $[-r, r]$ for $r < R$ is essential.

Full Proof Outline
A1
Radius of differentiated series: The differentiated series is $\sum_{n=1}^\infty nc_n x^{n-1}$. By the Hadamard formula and $n^{1/n} \to 1$: $\limsup|nc_n|^{1/n} = \limsup|c_n|^{1/n}$. Radius equals $R$.
Check: $n^{1/n} = e^{(\ln n)/n} \to e^0 = 1$.
B1
Setup for differentiation: Fix $x_0 \in (-R, R)$. Let $r$ satisfy $|x_0| < r < R$. Let $S_N(x) = \sum_{n=0}^N c_n x^n$ be the partial sums.
B2
M-test for the derivative series on $[-r,r]$: $|nc_n x^{n-1}| \leq n|c_n|r^{n-1}$ for $x \in [-r,r]$. Since $\sum n|c_n|r^{n-1}$ converges (differentiated series has radius $R > r$), the M-test gives uniform convergence of $\sum nc_n x^{n-1}$ on $[-r, r]$.
B3
Apply Rudin 7.17: $S_N$ are differentiable on $[-r,r]$, $S_N(0) = c_0$ converges, and $S_N' \to g(x) = \sum nc_n x^{n-1}$ uniformly on $[-r,r]$. Conclusion: $f$ is differentiable on $[-r,r]$ and $f' = g$ there.
Since this holds for every $r < R$ and $x_0$ was arbitrary in $(-R,R)$, we get $f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty nc_n x^{n-1}$ for all $x \in (-R,R)$. $\square$

Ex 6 Pointwise Bounded + Equicontinuous $\Rightarrow$ Uniformly Bounded

Preparation

This problem is a key ingredient in the proof of the Arzelà–Ascoli theorem. The setup: $(X, d)$ is a compact metric space, and $\{f_n\}$ is a sequence of functions that is pointwise bounded (at each $x$, the values $\{f_n(x)\}$ are bounded, though the bound may depend on $x$) and uniformly equicontinuous (a single $\delta$ controls oscillation for all $n$ and all pairs of points). The conclusion is that $\{f_n\}$ is uniformly bounded: a single constant $C$ bounds all $|f_n(x)|$ for all $n$ and all $x$.

The compactness of $X$ is absolutely essential. Without it, the argument breaks down: you would need infinitely many $\delta$-balls to cover $X$, giving infinitely many center points, and the sup of infinitely many pointwise bounds might be infinite. Compactness forces the cover to be finite.

The idea: Equicontinuity says that within any $\delta$-ball around a point, the functions don't change much. So if you know the functions are bounded at the center of the ball, you can control them everywhere in the ball. Compactness gives you finitely many balls that cover all of $X$. The max of the finitely many bounds at the centers plus $1$ is the global bound.

Key tools: (1) Apply uniform equicontinuity with $\varepsilon = 1$ to get a $\delta$ that works for all $n$. (2) Cover $X$ by finitely many open balls $B(x_1, \delta), \ldots, B(x_k, \delta)$ — this uses compactness. (3) At each center $x_i$, pointwise boundedness gives $M_i$ with $|f_n(x_i)| \leq M_i$ for all $n$. Set $M = \max_i M_i$ (finite, since there are finitely many centers). (4) For any $x \in X$, find the ball $B(x_i, \delta)$ containing $x$; equicontinuity gives $|f_n(x) - f_n(x_i)| \leq 1$; triangle inequality gives $|f_n(x)| \leq M + 1$.

Common pitfall: The problem states equicontinuity with $d_X(x,y) \leq \delta \Rightarrow |f_n(x) - f_n(y)| \leq \varepsilon$ (closed balls). Be consistent: if you cover by open balls $B(x_i, \delta)$ (where $d < \delta$), then $d < \delta \leq \delta$, so the equicontinuity condition still applies. Either formulation works — just be careful about the inequality direction when you invoke equicontinuity.

Quantifier comparison: Pointwise bounded says $\forall x, \exists M_x, \forall n: |f_n(x)| \leq M_x$. Uniformly bounded says $\exists C, \forall x, \forall n: |f_n(x)| \leq C$. The proof moves the existential quantifier on $C$ out by using compactness to reduce to finitely many $M_{x_i}$ and taking the max.

Proof Outline
1
Apply uniform equicontinuity with $\varepsilon = 1$: There exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and all $x, y \in X$ with $d(x, y) \leq \delta$, we have $|f_n(x) - f_n(y)| \leq 1$.
2
Use compactness to find a finite $\delta$-cover: The open balls $\{B(x, \delta)\}_{x \in X}$ cover $X$. By compactness, finitely many suffice: $X = B(x_1, \delta) \cup \cdots \cup B(x_k, \delta)$ for some $x_1, \ldots, x_k \in X$.
3
Use pointwise boundedness at the centers: For each center $x_i$, by hypothesis there exists $M_i$ such that $|f_n(x_i)| \leq M_i$ for all $n$. Set $M = \max_{i=1}^k M_i$ (finite maximum of finitely many numbers).
4
Transfer the bound to all of $X$: Let $x \in X$ be arbitrary. Then $x \in B(x_i, \delta)$ for some $i$, so $d(x, x_i) \leq \delta$. By equicontinuity: $|f_n(x) - f_n(x_i)| \leq 1$ for all $n$. Therefore: $$|f_n(x)| \leq |f_n(x) - f_n(x_i)| + |f_n(x_i)| \leq 1 + M.$$
Set $C = M + 1$. Then $|f_n(x)| \leq C$ for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and all $x \in X$. This is uniform boundedness. $\square$
MCWhy does the proof fail if $X$ is not compact?
B
The crucial step is covering $X$ by finitely many $\delta$-balls. On a non-compact space, you might need infinitely many balls, giving infinitely many centers $x_1, x_2, \ldots$ and corresponding bounds $M_1, M_2, \ldots$. There is no reason for $\sup_i M_i$ to be finite — the pointwise bounds might grow without limit as you move further out in the space. Compactness is exactly what forces the cover to be finite, making the max of the bounds a finite number.
T/FIn the proof, we need the equicontinuity to hold for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$ simultaneously — not just for each fixed $n$ separately.
True
In step 1, we use $\delta$ that works for all $n$ simultaneously. If we only had: for each fixed $n$, there exists $\delta_n$ such that... (ordinary uniform continuity of each $f_n$ individually), then each $\delta_n$ might depend on $n$ and we could not apply a single $\delta$ to cover all $n$. The key word "uniformly equicontinuous" means one $\delta$ handles all $n$ at once. This is what allows the bound $|f_n(x) - f_n(x_i)| \leq 1$ to hold for all $n$ and all $x \in B(x_i, \delta)$ simultaneously.
Exercise 6 Uniform Boundedness from Equicontinuity
Problem Statement (Hirsch, PS9)

Let $(X, d)$ be a compact metric space, and let $f_n : X \to \mathbb{R}$, $n \in \mathbb{N}$, be a pointwise bounded and uniformly equicontinuous sequence of functions, i.e.: $$\forall x \in X : \exists M \in \mathbb{R} : \forall n \in \mathbb{N} : |f_n(x)| \leq M,$$ and $$\forall \varepsilon > 0 : \exists \delta > 0 : \forall x, y \in X : \forall n \in \mathbb{N} : \bigl(d_X(x,y) \leq \delta \Rightarrow |f_n(x) - f_n(y)| \leq \varepsilon\bigr).$$ Show that $(f_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ is also uniformly bounded, i.e., $$\exists C \in \mathbb{R} : \forall x \in X : \forall n \in \mathbb{N} : |f_n(x)| \leq C.$$
