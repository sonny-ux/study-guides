The Electric Field

A charge $q$ creates an electric field at every point in surrounding space. Place a positive test charge $q_0$ at point $P$ and measure the force $\vec{F}$ it feels — the field there is defined as that force per unit test charge.

Definition of Electric Field
$$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0}$$
Units: N/C. Direction: same as force on a positive test charge.

For a single point charge $q$ at distance $r$:

Coulomb / Point-Charge Field
$$E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q}{r^2}, \qquad \vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q}{r^2}\hat{r}$$
$\hat{r}$ points away from $q$. If $q < 0$, $\vec{E}$ points toward $q$. $k = 1/4\pi\varepsilon_0 \approx 8.99\times10^9\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$
The test charge $q_0$ must be small enough not to disturb the source charge distribution. $\vec{E}$ is a property of the source — it exists at $P$ regardless of whether anything is there to feel it.

Superposition: fields from multiple charges add as vectors, not magnitudes. Always decompose into components before summing.

Continuous charge distributions use charge densities: linear $\lambda$ (C/m), surface $\sigma$ (C/m²), volume $\rho$ (C/m³). Slice into $dq$ elements, write $d\vec{E}$, integrate — components often cancel by symmetry.

Key Results to Know
$$E_\text{ring, axis} = \frac{qz}{4\pi\varepsilon_0(z^2+R^2)^{3/2}}$$ $$E_\text{disc, axis} = \frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}\!\left(1 - \frac{z}{\sqrt{z^2+R^2}}\right)$$ $$E_\text{infinite sheet} = \frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}$$
Disc result → infinite sheet as $R\to\infty$ with $z$ finite.

Electric dipole: two equal and opposite charges $\pm q$ separated by distance $d$. The dipole moment is $\vec{p} = q\vec{d}$ (points from $-$ to $+$, magnitude $p = qd$). On the dipole axis at large distance:

Dipole Field (far, on axis)
$$E \approx \frac{1}{2\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{p}{z^3}$$
Falls off as $1/r^3$, faster than a monopole. Valid for $z \gg d$.

Dipole in a uniform field: net force is zero, but there is a torque $\vec{\tau} = \vec{p}\times\vec{E}$ that tends to align $\vec{p}$ with $\vec{E}$. Potential energy of orientation: $U = -\vec{p}\cdot\vec{E}$.

Torque on a dipole is $pE\sin\theta$ — maximum when $\vec{p} \perp \vec{E}$, zero at both aligned ($\theta=0$, stable equilibrium) and anti-aligned ($\theta=\pi$, unstable equilibrium).

Field lines: tangent to $\vec{E}$ at every point; density proportional to magnitude; originate on $+$, terminate on $-$.

Gauss's Law & Gaussian Surfaces

Electric flux through a patch $dA$ is the component of $\vec{E}$ perpendicular to (i.e., piercing through) that patch times its area:

Electric Flux
$$\Phi = \oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{A}$$
The loop integral means over the entire closed surface. $d\vec{A}$ points outward by convention. Units: N·m²/C.

Only the component of $\vec{E}$ parallel to $d\vec{A}$ (perpendicular to the surface) contributes. A field line running parallel to the surface contributes zero flux through it.

Gauss's Law
$$\oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$
$q_\text{enc}$: algebraic sum of all charges inside the closed Gaussian surface. Outside charges contribute zero net flux.
Gauss's law is always true, but it is only useful for finding $\vec{E}$ when the charge distribution has enough symmetry to pull $E$ outside the integral. The three geometries: spherical, cylindrical, planar.

Applying Gauss's law — the recipe:

  1. Identify the symmetry of the charge distribution.
  2. Choose a Gaussian surface that matches that symmetry so $\vec{E}$ is constant in magnitude and either parallel or perpendicular to $d\vec{A}$ on every part of the surface.
  3. Compute $\oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{A} = E \cdot A_\text{surface}$ (where $E$ is constant on the surface).
  4. Set equal to $q_\text{enc}/\varepsilon_0$ and solve for $E$.
GeometrySurfaceResult
Point charge / sphereSphere of radius $r$$E = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$
Infinite line / cylinderCoaxial cylinder, length $L$$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}$
Infinite planePillbox straddling surface$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}$ (each side)
Conducting surfacePillbox, inner face inside conductor$E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}$ (outside only)
The infinite line result falls off as $1/r$ (not $1/r^2$); the infinite plane result is independent of distance. Both of these are non-obvious and frequently confused. The planar case gives $\sigma/2\varepsilon_0$ per sheet; two opposing sheets (like a capacitor) give $\sigma/\varepsilon_0$ in the gap.
Conductors in electrostatic equilibrium: $\vec{E} = 0$ inside, any net charge resides entirely on the surface, and $\vec{E}$ just outside is perpendicular to the surface with magnitude $\sigma/\varepsilon_0$.

Electric Potential

The electric force is conservative, so we can associate a potential energy $U$ with any configuration of charges. Electric potential $V$ is potential energy per unit charge — a scalar field:

Definition
$$V = \frac{U}{q_0}, \qquad \Delta V = -\int_i^f \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{s}$$
Units: volt (V = J/C). Potential difference $\Delta V$ is what's physically meaningful; absolute $V$ requires a reference point (usually $V=0$ at $r\to\infty$).
Point Charge & Superposition
$$V = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q}{r}, \qquad V = \sum_i \frac{q_i}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r_i}$$
Crucially, $V$ is a scalar — no vector components to worry about when summing contributions. This is often easier than finding $\vec{E}$ directly.

Recovering $\vec{E}$ from $V$: the field points in the direction of steepest decrease of $V$:

Gradient Relation
$$\vec{E} = -\nabla V = -\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}\hat{x} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial y}\hat{y} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial z}\hat{z}\right)$$
In 1D: $E_x = -dV/dx$. Equipotential surfaces are always perpendicular to field lines.
$V$ is often easier to compute from charge distributions than $\vec{E}$ because it's a scalar sum. Then differentiate to get $\vec{E}$ if needed.

Work and energy: work done by the electric field moving charge $q$ from $i$ to $f$ is $W = q(V_i - V_f) = -q\,\Delta V$. The work done by an external agent is the negative of this: $W_\text{ext} = q\,\Delta V$.

Potential due to a dipole at point $(r, \theta)$ from the dipole center (for $r \gg d$):

Dipole Potential (far field)
$$V = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{p\cos\theta}{r^2}$$
$\theta$ is angle from the dipole axis ($\vec{p}$ direction). Falls off as $1/r^2$, faster than a monopole ($1/r$).
Do not confuse $W = q\,\Delta V$ with $W = \Delta V$ alone. The sign is also crucial: moving a positive charge from high to low $V$ means the field does positive work (releases energy); moving against the field requires positive work input.

Capacitance

A capacitor is any arrangement of two conductors carrying equal and opposite charges $\pm Q$. The potential difference $V$ between them is proportional to $Q$; the proportionality constant defines capacitance:

Definition of Capacitance
$$C = \frac{Q}{V}$$
Units: farad (F = C/V). $C$ depends only on geometry, not on $Q$ or $V$ individually. Larger $C$ means more charge stored per volt.

Common geometries (derived using Gauss's law to find $E$, then integrating to find $V$):

TypeCapacitanceVariables
Parallel plate$C = \varepsilon_0 A/d$plate area $A$, separation $d$
Cylindrical$C = 2\pi\varepsilon_0 L / \ln(b/a)$length $L$, radii $a < b$
Spherical$C = 4\pi\varepsilon_0 ab/(b-a)$radii $a < b$

Capacitors in combination:

Parallel vs. Series
$$C_\text{parallel} = \sum_i C_i$$ $$\frac{1}{C_\text{series}} = \sum_i \frac{1}{C_i}$$
Parallel: same $V$, charges add. Series: same $Q$, voltages add. Series reduces total $C$; parallel increases it.

Energy stored in a capacitor:

Stored Energy
$$U = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}QV$$
All three forms are equivalent via $Q = CV$. The energy resides in the electric field between the plates.

Energy density of the electric field (energy per unit volume in the gap):

Energy Density
$$u = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$$
This is a general result — not just for capacitors. Wherever there is an electric field, there is stored energy with this density.

Dielectrics: inserting a dielectric (insulator) with dielectric constant $\kappa > 1$ between the plates replaces $\varepsilon_0$ with $\kappa\varepsilon_0$, multiplying $C$ by $\kappa$. The dielectric weakens $E$ inside by the same factor.

When a capacitor is disconnected from its battery, $Q$ is fixed — inserting a dielectric decreases $V$ and $U$. When connected to a battery, $V$ is fixed — inserting a dielectric increases $Q$ and $U$. The two scenarios behave oppositely.

Current & Resistance

When a potential difference is maintained across a conductor, charges flow. Current $i$ is the rate of charge flow:

Current
$$i = \frac{dq}{dt}$$
Units: ampere (A = C/s). By convention, current direction is the direction positive charge would flow (opposite to electron flow).

Current density $\vec{J}$ is current per unit cross-sectional area, and it is related to the drift velocity of charge carriers:

Current Density & Drift
$$J = \frac{i}{A}, \qquad \vec{J} = (ne)\vec{v}_d$$
$n$: number density of carriers, $e$: electron charge, $v_d$: drift speed (typically $\sim$mm/s, much slower than the signal speed).

Resistance is the ratio of voltage across a device to current through it:

Ohm's Law
$$V = iR, \qquad R = \frac{\rho L}{A}$$
Units: ohm ($\Omega$ = V/A). $\rho$: resistivity (material property, units $\Omega$·m), $L$: length, $A$: cross-sectional area.

Resistivity and temperature: for metals, $\rho$ increases with temperature: $\rho = \rho_0[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)]$ where $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistivity.

Power dissipated in a resistor:

Power
$$P = iV = i^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$
All three forms are equivalent via $V = iR$. Power is dissipated as heat (Joule heating).
Ohm's law ($V = iR$ with constant $R$) is a material-specific approximation, not a universal law. A device is "ohmic" only if $R$ is independent of $V$. Diodes, transistors, and many biological materials are not ohmic.
The distinction between EMF $\mathcal{E}$ and terminal voltage: a real battery has internal resistance $r$, so terminal voltage $V = \mathcal{E} - ir$ (less than EMF when current is flowing).
