A solid non-conducting sphere of radius $R = 0.10\text{ m}$ carries uniform volume charge density $\rho = 8.0\;\mu\text{C/m}^3$. Use Gauss's Law to find $E(r)$ everywhere and locate the maximum field.

Solid charged sphere — two Gaussian surfaces (inside and outside)
(a) Find $E(r)$ for $r < R$. Evaluate at $r = 0.05\text{ m}$.
Gauss's Law — spherical surface radius r < R
$Q_{enc} = \rho\cdot\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3$. By symmetry: $\oint\vec{E}\cdot d\vec{A} = E(4\pi r^2) = \dfrac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$
Solve: $E(4\pi r^2) = \dfrac{\rho\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3}{\varepsilon_0}$
$E(r) = \dfrac{\rho r}{3\varepsilon_0}$  (directed radially outward)
Numerical evaluation at r = 0.05 m
$E = \dfrac{(8.0\times10^{-6})(0.05)}{3(8.85\times10^{-12})} = \dfrac{4.0\times10^{-7}}{2.655\times10^{-11}}$
$E = 1.51\times10^4\text{ N/C} = 15.1\text{ kN/C}$
(b) Find $E(r)$ for $r > R$. Evaluate at $r = 0.20\text{ m}$.
Total charge enclosed for r > R
$Q_{total} = \rho\cdot\tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3 = (8.0\times10^{-6})\tfrac{4}{3}\pi(0.10)^3 = 3.35\text{ nC}$
Gauss's Law (point-charge behavior outside)
$E(r) = \dfrac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} = \dfrac{\rho R^3}{3\varepsilon_0 r^2}$
At $r = 0.20\text{ m}$: $E = \dfrac{(8.0\times10^{-6})(0.001)}{3(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.04)} = \mathbf{752\text{ N/C}}$
(c) At what radius $r$ is $E$ maximum? Compute $E_{max}$.
Compare slopes: inside E grows as r; outside E falls as 1/r²
Maximum at the surface $r = R$. Both formulas agree there:
$E_{max} = \dfrac{\rho R}{3\varepsilon_0} = \dfrac{(8.0\times10^{-6})(0.10)}{3(8.85\times10^{-12})}$
$E_{max} = 3.01\times10^4\text{ N/C} \approx 30.1\text{ kN/C}$ at $r = R$

A parallel-plate capacitor (area $A = 0.020\text{ m}^2$, separation $d = 4.0\text{ mm}$) has a dielectric slab ($\kappa = 4.0$, thickness $t = 1.5\text{ mm}$) inserted parallel to the plates, with the remaining $2.5\text{ mm}$ being air. Connected to $V = 200\text{ V}$.

Partial dielectric fill — air (top) and dielectric (bottom) in series
(a) Find the capacitance $C$ of this configuration.
Two capacitors in series (same Q, same area)
$C_{air} = \dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d_1} = \dfrac{(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.020)}{2.5\times10^{-3}} = 70.8\text{ pF}$
$C_{diel} = \dfrac{\kappa\varepsilon_0 A}{d_2} = \dfrac{4(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.020)}{1.5\times10^{-3}} = 472\text{ pF}$
Series formula
$\dfrac{1}{C} = \dfrac{1}{70.8} + \dfrac{1}{472} \Rightarrow C = \dfrac{70.8\times472}{543}$
$C \approx 61.6\text{ pF}$
Alternatively: $C = \dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d_1 + d_2/\kappa} = \dfrac{(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.020)}{0.0025 + 0.00375} = 61.6\text{ pF}$ ✓
(b) Find $E_1$ (air) and $E_2$ (dielectric). Use $D = \sigma_f$ is continuous across the interface.
Find surface charge density
$Q = CV = (61.6\times10^{-12})(200) = 12.32\text{ nC}$
$\sigma_f = Q/A = 12.32\text{ nC}/0.020\text{ m}^2 = 616\text{ nC/m}^2$
The displacement field: $D = \sigma_f = 616\text{ nC/m}^2$ (same in both layers)
Fields from D = κε₀E
$E_1 = D/\varepsilon_0 = 69.6\text{ kV/m}$ (air)
$E_2 = D/(\kappa\varepsilon_0) = 17.4\text{ kV/m}$ (dielectric)
Check: $E_1 d_1 + E_2 d_2 = (69600)(0.0025)+(17400)(0.0015) = 174+26 = 200\text{ V}$ ✓
C-3CALC

A spherical capacitor: inner conductor radius $a = 5.0\text{ cm}$ with charge $+Q$, outer conductor inner radius $b = 10.0\text{ cm}$ with charge $-Q$. Vacuum gap. $Q = 20\text{ nC}$.

Spherical capacitor — inner shell radius a, outer shell radius b
(a) Find $E(r)$ for $a < r < b$, and argue why $E = 0$ for $r > b$.
Between shells: Q_enc = +Q
$E(4\pi r^2) = Q/\varepsilon_0$
$E(r) = \dfrac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} = \dfrac{(20\times10^{-9})}{4\pi(8.85\times10^{-12})r^2} = \dfrac{180}{r^2}\text{ N/C}$
Outside r > b
Gaussian sphere at $r > b$ encloses $+Q + (-Q) = 0$, so $E = 0$. (Capacitor has no net charge.)
(b) Find the potential difference $V_{ab} = V(a) - V(b)$ by integrating $\vec{E}\cdot d\vec{l}$.
Integrate E from a to b (path along radial direction)
$V_a - V_b = -\int_b^a E\,dr = \int_a^b \dfrac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}dr = \dfrac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\left(\dfrac{1}{a}-\dfrac{1}{b}\right)$
$= (180)\left(\dfrac{1}{0.05}-\dfrac{1}{0.10}\right) = 180\times10$
$V_{ab} = 1800\text{ V}$
(c) Find $C$ and the energy $U$ stored.
Capacitance
$C = \dfrac{Q}{V_{ab}} = \dfrac{20\times10^{-9}}{1800} = 11.1\text{ pF}$
Formula check: $C = 4\pi\varepsilon_0\dfrac{ab}{b-a} = \dfrac{(0.05)(0.10)}{(0.05)(9\times10^9)} = 11.1\text{ pF}$ ✓
Energy
$U = \tfrac{1}{2}QV_{ab} = \tfrac{1}{2}(20\text{ nC})(1800\text{ V}) = 18\,\mu\text{J}$

In the circuit below: $\varepsilon_1 = 24\text{ V}$ (left battery), $\varepsilon_2 = 12\text{ V}$ (right battery), $R_1 = 6\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 12\,\Omega$ (middle branch), $R_3 = 4\,\Omega$ (right branch). Use mesh analysis.

Two-loop circuit — mesh currents I₁ (left) and I₂ (right), both clockwise
(a) Define mesh currents $I_1$ (left loop, CW) and $I_2$ (right loop, CW). Write the KVL equation for each loop.
Left loop (CW): traverse in direction of I₁
$+\varepsilon_1 - R_1 I_1 - R_2(I_1 - I_2) = 0$
$24 - 6I_1 - 12(I_1-I_2) = 0$
$24 = 18I_1 - 12I_2$    (1)
Right loop (CW): traverse in direction of I₂
$+\varepsilon_2 - R_3 I_2 - R_2(I_2 - I_1) = 0$
$12 - 4I_2 - 12(I_2 - I_1) = 0$
$12 = -12I_1 + 16I_2$    (2)
(b) Solve the system for $I_1$, $I_2$, and the current through $R_2$.
Solve simultaneously
From (1): $24 = 18I_1 - 12I_2$ → multiply by (2/3): $16 = 12I_1 - 8I_2$
Add to (2): $16+12 = 12I_1-8I_2-12I_1+16I_2$ → $28 = 8I_2$ → $I_2 = 3.5\text{ A}$
Back-sub: $24 = 18I_1 - 42$ → $I_1 = 66/18 = \mathbf{3.67\text{ A}}$
Current through R₂
$I_{R_2} = I_1 - I_2 = 3.67 - 3.5 = 0.167\text{ A}$ (downward if I₁ > I₂)
$I_1 = 11/3\approx3.67\text{ A}$, $I_2 = 3.5\text{ A}$, $I_{R_2} = 1/6\approx0.167\text{ A}$
(c) Find the power dissipated in each resistor. Verify it equals total power delivered by both batteries.
Power in each R
$P_1 = I_1^2 R_1 = (11/3)^2(6) = (121/9)(6) = 80.7\text{ W}$
$P_2 = I_{R_2}^2 R_2 = (1/6)^2(12) = (1/36)(12) = 0.333\text{ W}$
$P_3 = I_2^2 R_3 = (3.5)^2(4) = 49\text{ W}$
Power from batteries
$P_{\varepsilon_1} = \varepsilon_1 I_1 = 24(11/3) = 88\text{ W}$
$P_{\varepsilon_2} = \varepsilon_2 I_2 = 12(3.5) = 42\text{ W}$
Total supplied: $88+42=130\text{ W}$; Total dissipated: $80.7+0.33+49=130\text{ W}$ ✓
$P_1 \approx 80.7\text{ W}$, $P_2 \approx 0.33\text{ W}$, $P_3 = 49\text{ W}$

C-8CALC

A proton ($m = 1.67\times10^{-27}\text{ kg}$, $q = 1.6\times10^{-19}\text{ C}$) enters a region with $\vec{E} = E_0\hat{y}$ ($E_0 = 5000\text{ V/m}$) and $\vec{B} = B_0\hat{z}$ ($B_0 = 0.20\text{ T}$). The proton moves in the $+\hat{x}$ direction.

Velocity selector: E upward, B out of page, proton entering in +x
(a) Find the speed $v$ for which the proton travels in a straight horizontal line (velocity selector condition).
Balance electric and magnetic forces
Electric force: $\vec{F}_E = q\vec{E} = qE_0\hat{y}$ (upward)
Magnetic force: $\vec{F}_B = q\vec{v}\times\vec{B} = q(v\hat{x})\times(B_0\hat{z}) = qvB_0(\hat{x}\times\hat{z}) = -qvB_0\hat{y}$ (downward)
For straight-line: $F_E = F_B$: $qE_0 = qvB_0$
Selector speed
$v = \dfrac{E_0}{B_0} = \dfrac{5000}{0.20} = 2.5\times10^4\text{ m/s}$
(b) Now $\vec{E}$ is turned off. The proton enters with $v = 2.5\times10^4\text{ m/s}$ in the $+\hat{x}$ direction and $\vec{B} = 0.20\hat{z}\text{ T}$. Find the radius $r$ of circular motion.
Centripetal = magnetic force
$\dfrac{mv^2}{r} = qvB$ → $r = \dfrac{mv}{qB}$
Evaluate
$r = \dfrac{(1.67\times10^{-27})(2.5\times10^4)}{(1.6\times10^{-19})(0.20)} = \dfrac{4.175\times10^{-23}}{3.2\times10^{-20}}$
$r = 1.30\times10^{-3}\text{ m} = 1.30\text{ mm}$
(c) Find the period $T$ of the circular orbit. Is $T$ independent of the proton's speed?
Period from circumference / speed
$T = \dfrac{2\pi r}{v} = \dfrac{2\pi (mv/qB)}{v} = \dfrac{2\pi m}{qB}$
The $v$ cancels! Period is independent of speed — key to cyclotron operation.
Numerical
$T = \dfrac{2\pi(1.67\times10^{-27})}{(1.6\times10^{-19})(0.20)} = \dfrac{1.049\times10^{-26}}{3.2\times10^{-20}}$
$T = 3.28\times10^{-7}\text{ s} \approx 328\text{ ns}$ (independent of speed)

A finite straight wire of total length $2L = 0.40\text{ m}$ carries current $I = 8.0\text{ A}$. Find the magnetic field at point $P$ located a perpendicular distance $d = 6.0\text{ cm}$ from the midpoint of the wire.

Finite wire, length 2L = 0.40 m, point P at perpendicular distance d = 6 cm from midpoint
(a) Set up the Biot-Savart integral $dB$ from a segment $d\ell$ at position $z$ along the wire to point $P$.
Biot-Savart: $d\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi}\frac{d\vec{\ell}\times\hat{r}}{r^2}$
Current element: $d\vec{\ell} = dz\,\hat{z}$. Position of $P$ from segment at $z$: $\vec{r} = d\hat{y} - z\hat{z}$, $r = \sqrt{d^2+z^2}$.
Cross product: $\hat{z}\times\hat{r} = \hat{z}\times\dfrac{(d\hat{y}-z\hat{z})}{\sqrt{d^2+z^2}} = \dfrac{d(\hat{z}\times\hat{y})}{\sqrt{d^2+z^2}} = \dfrac{-d\hat{x}}{\sqrt{d^2+z^2}}$
Magnitude of dB (into page by RHR if current is in +z and P is above)
$dB = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi}\cdot\dfrac{d\,dz}{(d^2+z^2)^{3/2}}$    directed out of page (by RHR)
(b) Integrate from $z = -L$ to $z = +L$ to find $B$. Use result: $\int_{-L}^{L}\dfrac{dz}{(d^2+z^2)^{3/2}} = \dfrac{2L}{d^2\sqrt{d^2+L^2}}$
Apply the integral result
$B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I d}{4\pi}\cdot\dfrac{2L}{d^2\sqrt{d^2+L^2}} = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi}\cdot\dfrac{2L}{d\sqrt{d^2+L^2}}$
With $L = 0.20\text{ m}$, $d = 0.06\text{ m}$: $\sqrt{(0.06)^2+(0.20)^2} = \sqrt{0.0436} = 0.2088\text{ m}$
$B = \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(8.0)}{4\pi}\cdot\dfrac{2(0.20)}{(0.06)(0.2088)}$
$= (10^{-7})(8.0)\cdot\dfrac{0.40}{0.01253} = (8\times10^{-7})(31.93)$
Result
$B = 2.55\times10^{-5}\text{ T} = 25.5\,\mu\text{T}$ (out of page)
(c) Show the result reduces to the infinite-wire formula as $L\to\infty$. Compare to the actual infinite-wire value.
Limiting case L >> d
$B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi}\cdot\dfrac{2L}{d\sqrt{d^2+L^2}} \xrightarrow{L\gg d} \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi}\cdot\dfrac{2L}{d\cdot L} = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi}\cdot\dfrac{2}{d} = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi d}$ ✓
Infinite wire at d = 6 cm
$B_\infty = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi d} = \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(8.0)}{2\pi(0.06)} = \dfrac{3.2\times10^{-6}}{0.3770}$
$B_\infty = 26.7\,\mu\text{T}$  vs.  $B_{finite} = 25.5\,\mu\text{T}$ (95.5% of infinite value)
C-12CALC

An infinite solid cylindrical conductor of radius $R = 3.0\text{ cm}$ carries total current $I = 15\text{ A}$ uniformly distributed across its cross-section. Use Ampère's Law.

Cylindrical conductor: amber dashed (r < R, inside), purple dashed (r > R, outside)
(a) Find $B(r)$ for $r < R$ (inside the conductor).
Ampère's Law: $\oint\vec{B}\cdot d\vec{\ell} = \mu_0 I_{enc}$
Circular Amperian loop, radius $r < R$. $I_{enc} = I\cdot\dfrac{\pi r^2}{\pi R^2} = I\dfrac{r^2}{R^2}$
By symmetry: $B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I\dfrac{r^2}{R^2}$
Result
$B(r) = \dfrac{\mu_0 I r}{2\pi R^2}$   (linear in r)
At $r=R$: $B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi R} = \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(15)}{2\pi(0.03)} = 100\,\mu\text{T}$
(b) Find $B(r)$ for $r > R$ (outside). Evaluate at $r = 6.0\text{ cm}$.
Outside: I_enc = I (full current)
$B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I$
$B(r) = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$ (same as infinite thin wire)
At $r = 6.0\text{ cm}$: $B = \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(15)}{2\pi(0.06)} = \dfrac{18.85\times10^{-7}}{0.3770}$
$B = 50.0\,\mu\text{T}$ at $r=2R$
(c) At what radius is $B$ maximum? What is $B_{max}$? Sketch $B$ vs $r$.
Match at surface: inside B increases, outside B decreases
Maximum at $r = R$ (surface). Both formulas agree: $B_{max} = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi R}$
$= \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(15)}{2\pi(0.03)}$
$B_{max} = 100\,\mu\text{T}$ at $r = R = 3.0\text{ cm}$
Inside: $B\propto r$ (linear). Outside: $B\propto 1/r$ (hyperbolic). Same peak shape as $E$ for charged sphere.
C-13CALC

A toroidal solenoid has $N = 400$ turns, inner radius $a = 8.0\text{ cm}$, outer radius $b = 12.0\text{ cm}$, carrying current $I = 3.0\text{ A}$.

Toroidal solenoid: N=400 turns, inner radius a=8 cm, outer radius b=12 cm
(a) Apply Ampère's Law with a circular path of radius $r$ inside the toroid ($a < r < b$) to find $B(r)$.
Amperian loop (circle of radius r, a < r < b)
Loop encloses all $N$ turns each carrying current $I$: $I_{enc} = NI$
$B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 NI$
$B(r) = \dfrac{\mu_0 NI}{2\pi r}$   (varies with $r$ — not uniform inside!)
At $r = 10\text{ cm}$ (center): $B = \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(400)(3.0)}{2\pi(0.10)} = \dfrac{1.508\times10^{-3}}{0.6283}$
$B(r=10\text{ cm}) = 2.40\text{ mT}$
(b) Show $B = 0$ outside the toroid ($r > b$ or $r < a$) using Ampère's Law.
Outside (r > b): Amperian loop encloses all turns going "up" and all going "down"
Each turn contributes $+I$ going one way through the loop but the return wire contributes $-I$. Total: $I_{enc} = NI - NI = 0$.
$B(2\pi r) = 0$ →
$B = 0$ for $r > b$ (and also for $r < a$)
This is a key property of the toroid: field is confined entirely within the windings.
(c) Find the total magnetic flux $\Phi$ through the rectangular cross-section of the toroid (height $h$, width $b - a$).
Integrate B over cross-section (height h = b-a = 4 cm in this example, or keep general)
$\Phi = \int_a^b B(r)\cdot h\,dr = \int_a^b \dfrac{\mu_0 NI}{2\pi r}\cdot h\,dr = \dfrac{\mu_0 NIh}{2\pi}\ln\dfrac{b}{a}$
With $h = b - a = 0.04\text{ m}$, $b/a = 12/8 = 1.5$, $\ln(1.5) = 0.405$:
$\Phi = \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(400)(3.0)(0.04)}{2\pi}\cdot0.405$
$= \dfrac{6.032\times10^{-5}}{2\pi}\cdot0.405 = (9.6\times10^{-6})(0.405)$
$\Phi = 3.89\times10^{-6}\text{ Wb} = 3.89\,\mu\text{Wb}$

A rectangular loop of sides $a = 0.04\text{ m}$ by $b = 0.06\text{ m}$, carrying $I = 2.0\text{ A}$, acts as a magnetic dipole. Treat the loop dimensions as small compared to distances of interest.

(a) Find the magnetic dipole moment $\vec{\mu}$.
$\mu = IA$
$\mu = IA = (2.0)(0.04\times0.06) = (2.0)(2.4\times10^{-3})$
$\mu = 4.8\times10^{-3}\text{ A·m}^2 = 4.8\text{ mA·m}^2$, directed perpendicular to the loop plane (by RHR)
(b) Find $B$ on the axis of the loop at $z = 1.0\text{ m}$ (far-field approximation: $B_{axis} \approx \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{2\mu}{z^3}$).
Far-field dipole formula
$B = \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\cdot\dfrac{2\mu}{z^3} = (10^{-7})\cdot\dfrac{2(4.8\times10^{-3})}{(1.0)^3}$
$= 10^{-7}\times9.6\times10^{-3}$
$B = 9.6\times10^{-10}\text{ T} \approx 0.96\text{ nT}$
Compare to Earth's field $\sim 50\,\mu\text{T}$: the dipole is much weaker at 1 m.
(c) The loop is placed in $\vec{B}_{ext} = 0.50\text{ T}$ with $\vec{\mu} \perp \vec{B}_{ext}$. Find the torque and the potential energy in this orientation, and the work needed to rotate to $\vec{\mu} \parallel \vec{B}_{ext}$.
Torque at θ = 90°
$\tau = \mu B\sin90° = (4.8\times10^{-3})(0.50)$
$\tau = 2.4\times10^{-3}\text{ N·m} = 2.4\text{ mN·m}$
Potential energies
$U(\theta=90°) = -\mu B\cos90° = 0$
$U(\theta=0°) = -\mu B = -(4.8\times10^{-3})(0.50) = -2.4\text{ mJ}$
$W = U_f - U_i = (-2.4\text{ mJ}) - 0$
$W = -2.4\text{ mJ}$ (field does $2.4\text{ mJ}$ of work — energy released)
