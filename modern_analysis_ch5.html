Chapter 5

Differentiation

Rudin · PS7 Ex.4,7,8 · Spring 2026. The local linear structure of functions — and the Mean Value Theorem as the central engine of differential calculus.

01 What Chapter 5 Is About

Main Idea
Chapter 4 gave you continuity — the idea that a function doesn't jump. Chapter 5 asks a harder question: is the function smooth? Differentiability is continuity plus a linear approximation. A differentiable function near any point looks like a straight line at the right scale. That local linear structure is extraordinarily powerful: it connects function values at different points (MVT), controls how functions grow (monotonicity), and allows approximation by polynomials (Taylor's theorem).

The chapter culminates in the MVT, which is the central engine behind almost every quantitative result in analysis — bounding errors, proving constancy, establishing monotonicity. You will use MVT constantly from here on.

02 Definition of the Derivative

Main Idea
The derivative is the limit of the difference quotient. $f(t)-f(x)$ is the change in output; $t-x$ is the change in input. Their ratio is the average rate of change. Taking the limit as $t\to x$ asks: what does this ratio approach as the interval shrinks to a point? If the limit exists and is finite, $f$ is differentiable at $x$.
Definition — The Derivative
Let $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ and fix $x\in[a,b]$. The derivative of $f$ at $x$ is: $$f'(x)=\lim_{t\to x}\frac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$$ Both forms appear in proofs. Know both.
The Linear Approximation View
Differentiability at $x$ means: there exists $f'(x)$ such that $$f(x+h)=f(x)+f'(x)\cdot h+o(h)\quad\text{as }h\to 0$$ where $o(h)/h\to 0$. Near $x$, $f$ looks like the linear function $f(x)+f'(x)(t-x)$. The derivative $f'(x)$ is the slope of that line.
Differentiable implies continuous — but NOT vice versa
If $f'(x)$ exists, then $f$ is continuous at $x$. Proof: $f(t)-f(x)=\frac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}\cdot(t-x)\to f'(x)\cdot 0=0$.

The converse fails. Classic counterexample: $f(x)=|x|$ at $x=0$. The difference quotient is $|h|/h$, which equals $+1$ for $h>0$ and $-1$ for $h<0$ — different one-sided limits, so no derivative at $0$. Yet $|x|$ is continuous everywhere.
A Continuous Function That Is Nowhere Differentiable
Weierstrass (1872): $f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a^n\cos(b^n\pi x)$ for appropriate $a,b$. Continuous on all of $\mathbb{R}$ but differentiable at no point. This showed that continuity and differentiability are genuinely different — not just "almost the same."

03 Differentiation Rules

Main Idea
Rudin derives the standard rules as theorems, each following directly from the limit definition. Understanding the proof of each rule matters more than in a calculus course — your problem sets ask you to prove them from scratch.
Sum, Product, Quotient Rules (Rudin §5.3)
If $f$ and $g$ are differentiable at $x$:

$(f+g)'(x)=f'(x)+g'(x)$ — linearity of the limit.

$(fg)'(x)=f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x)$ — product rule. Proved by adding and subtracting $f(x+h)g(x)$.

$(f/g)'(x)=\dfrac{f'(x)g(x)-f(x)g'(x)}{g(x)^2}$ — quotient rule, when $g(x)\neq 0$.
Chain Rule (Rudin §5.5) — the key proof technique
If $g$ is differentiable at $x$ and $f$ is differentiable at $g(x)$, then $(f\circ g)'(x)=f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$.

The auxiliary $\varphi$ trick: Define $$\varphi(k)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{f(g(x)+k)-f(g(x))}{k}-f'(g(x)) & k\neq 0\\0 & k=0\end{cases}$$ By differentiability of $f$, $\varphi(k)\to 0$ as $k\to 0$, so $\varphi$ is continuous at $0$. The key identity: $f(g(x)+k)-f(g(x))=(f'(g(x))+\varphi(k))\cdot k$ holds for all $k$.

Set $k=g(x+h)-g(x)$, divide by $h$, take $h\to 0$: $k\to 0$ (since $g$ is continuous), $\varphi(k)\to 0$, and $k/h\to g'(x)$.

Why the auxiliary $\varphi$? The naive approach divides by $k=g(x+h)-g(x)$, which might be zero for small $h\neq 0$. The $\varphi$ trick avoids division by $k$ entirely.

04 Power Rule for Rationals

Main Idea
Rudin proves $(x^q)'=qx^{q-1}$ for $q\in\mathbb{Q}$ in three steps, each using the previous. This is PS7 Exercise 8 — you need to reproduce the argument.
Step 1: $(x^n)'=nx^{n-1}$ for $n\in\mathbb{N}$
By the product rule and induction. Base case $n=1$: $(x)'=1$. Inductive step: $(x^{n+1})'=(x\cdot x^n)'=x^n+x\cdot nx^{n-1}=(n+1)x^n$.
Step 2: $(x^{1/n})'=\frac{1}{n}x^{1/n-1}$ for $n\in\mathbb{N}$, $x>0$
Let $r(x)=x^{1/n}$, the inverse of $s(y)=y^n$. Since $s'(y)=ny^{n-1}\neq 0$ for $y>0$, the inverse function theorem gives: $$r'(x)=\frac{1}{s'(r(x))}=\frac{1}{n\cdot(x^{1/n})^{n-1}}=\frac{1}{n}x^{\frac{1}{n}-1}$$
Step 3: $(x^{m/n})'=\frac{m}{n}x^{m/n-1}$ for $m\in\mathbb{Z}$, $n\in\mathbb{N}$
Write $h(x)=x^{m/n}=(x^{1/n})^m$. By the chain rule with $f(y)=y^m$ and $g(x)=x^{1/n}$: $$h'(x)=m(x^{1/n})^{m-1}\cdot\frac{1}{n}x^{1/n-1}=\frac{m}{n}x^{\frac{m-1}{n}+\frac{1}{n}-1}=\frac{m}{n}x^{m/n-1}$$

05 Local Extrema

Theorem (Rudin §5.8)
If $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ has a local maximum at $x\in(a,b)$ and $f'(x)$ exists, then $f'(x)=0$.

Proof: For $t>x$ close to $x$: $\frac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}\leq 0$ (since $f(t)\leq f(x)$). Taking $t\to x^+$ gives $f'(x)\leq 0$. For $t<x$: $\frac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}\geq 0$. Taking $t\to x^-$ gives $f'(x)\geq 0$. So $f'(x)=0$.
$f'(x)=0$ does NOT imply a local extremum
$f(x)=x^3$ has $f'(0)=0$ but $x=0$ is an inflection point, not an extremum. The condition $f'(x)=0$ is necessary but not sufficient. You need the second derivative or a sign change in $f'$ to conclude an extremum exists.
Chapter 5

The Mean Value Theorem

The engine of differential calculus — connecting derivative information to function values at different points.

01 Why MVT Is Central

Main Idea
Almost every "quantitative" result in Chapter 5 and beyond reduces to MVT. Want to show $f$ is constant? Show $f'=0$ and apply MVT. Want to bound $|f(x)-f(y)|$? Bound $|f'|$ and apply MVT. Want monotonicity? Check the sign of $f'$ and apply MVT. The theorem converts derivative information into value information.

02 Rolle and the MVT

The Geometric Picture — Why We Subtract the Secant Line
Draw $f$ on $[a,b]$. The secant line connecting $(a,f(a))$ to $(b,f(b))$ has slope $\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$. The MVT says: at some interior point $c$, the tangent to the graph is parallel to that secant — the instantaneous rate equals the average rate over the whole interval.

The proof strategy: Rolle's theorem only applies when $f(a)=f(b)$ — both endpoints at the same height. The insight is to tilt the graph until the endpoints are level. If you subtract the secant line $\ell(x)=f(a)+\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)$ from $f$, you get a new function $h=f-\ell$ where $h(a)=h(b)=0$. The secant line is gone; the endpoints are equal; Rolle applies.

How to discover $h$: Ask: what is the simplest function that agrees with $f$ at $a$ and $b$? It's the affine interpolant — the secant line. Subtracting it from $f$ is the canonical way to zero out both endpoints. This is not magic; it is the one natural choice, and once you see it, it becomes automatic.
Rolle's Theorem (Rudin §5.10)
Let $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ be continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$, and $f(a)=f(b)$. Then $\exists\,c\in(a,b)$ with $f'(c)=0$.

Proof: By the EVT (compactness!), $f$ attains its max and min on $[a,b]$. If both extrema are at the endpoints, $f$ is constant (since $f(a)=f(b)$), so $f'\equiv 0$. Otherwise, $f$ attains an extremum at some interior $c\in(a,b)$, and the local extremum theorem gives $f'(c)=0$.
Mean Value Theorem (Rudin §5.10)
Let $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ be continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$. Then $\exists\,c\in(a,b)$ such that: $$f(b)-f(a)=f'(c)(b-a)$$ Reading: $f(b)-f(a)$ is the total change in output; $b-a$ is the total input change; $f'(c)$ is the instantaneous rate at some intermediate $c$. The instantaneous rate somewhere equals the average rate over the whole interval.

Proof: Define $g(x)=f(x)-f(a)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)$. Then $g(a)=g(b)=0$, so by Rolle's theorem $\exists\,c$ with $g'(c)=0$, i.e., $f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$.
The Three Standard Consequences
1. $f'=0\Rightarrow f$ is constant: For any $x<y$, MVT gives $f(y)-f(x)=f'(c)(y-x)=0$.

2. $f'\geq 0\Rightarrow f$ is increasing: For $x<y$, $f(y)-f(x)=f'(c)(y-x)\geq 0$.

3. $|f'|\leq M\Rightarrow|f(x)-f(y)|\leq M|x-y|$: Lipschitz bound directly from MVT. The fundamental estimate for controlling function oscillation.
Proof Walk — MVT via Rolle
1
Define $h(x)=f(x)-f(a)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)$.
How to find $h$: We need $h(a)=h(b)=0$ so Rolle applies. The unique affine function through $(a,f(a))$ and $(b,f(b))$ is the secant line $\ell(x)=f(a)+\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)$. Setting $h=f-\ell$ gives $h(a)=h(b)=0$ by construction. Any critical point of $h$ is a point where $f'=\ell'=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$.
2
Verify: $h(a)=0$ and $h(b)=f(b)-f(a)-(f(b)-f(a))=0$.
Check Rolle's hypothesis $h(a)=h(b)=0$.
3
Compute $h'(x)=f'(x)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$.
Know what $h'(c)=0$ means in terms of $f'$.
4
Apply Rolle: $\exists\,c\in(a,b)$ with $h'(c)=0$, i.e., $f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$. $\blacksquare$
Translate $h'(c)=0$ back into the MVT statement.
The Subdivision Trick — an Alternative to MVT
Sometimes MVT is unavailable. Substitute: subdivide $[x,y]$ into $n$ equal parts and sum: $$|f(y)-f(x)|\leq\sum_{k=1}^n\left|f\!\left(x+\frac{k(y-x)}{n}\right)-f\!\left(x+\frac{(k-1)(y-x)}{n}\right)\right|$$ If each term satisfies a bound of the form $C/n^2$, the sum goes to $0$ as $n\to\infty$, proving $f(y)=f(x)$. This is the technique in PS7 Exercise 4.

03 Monotonicity and Derivatives

Main Idea
PS7 Exercise 7 asks you to prove the equivalence between $f$ increasing and $f'\geq 0$. Both directions are important and use different tools. The forward direction reads off the sign of the difference quotient directly; the backward direction invokes the MVT.
$f$ increasing $\Rightarrow$ $f'\geq 0$: read the difference quotient
If $f$ is increasing and $h>0$: $\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\geq 0$ (numerator and denominator both $\geq 0$). Taking $h\to 0^+$ gives $f'(x)\geq 0$.
$f'\geq 0$ $\Rightarrow$ $f$ increasing: apply MVT
For any $x<y$ in $[0,1]$: MVT gives $c\in(x,y)$ with $f(y)-f(x)=f'(c)(y-x)\geq 0$ (since $f'(c)\geq 0$ and $y-x>0$). So $f(y)\geq f(x)$.
Strict vs. Non-Strict
$f'\geq 0$ gives $f$ non-decreasing. For strictly increasing you need $f'>0$ — but even that's not necessary: $f(x)=x^3$ is strictly increasing with $f'(0)=0$. So $f'>0$ is sufficient but not necessary for strict increase.

04 Cauchy's Mean Value Theorem

The Parametric Curve Picture
Think of $t\mapsto(g(t),f(t))$ as a parametric curve in the plane, with $t$ going from $a$ to $b$. The curve starts at the point $(g(a),f(a))$ and ends at $(g(b),f(b))$.

The secant line connecting these two endpoints has slope $\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}$. The tangent to the curve at parameter $t$ has slope $\dfrac{f'(t)}{g'(t)}$ (rise over run, using the chain rule).

Cauchy's MVT says: at some parameter value $c\in(a,b)$, the tangent slope equals the secant slope. When $g(t)=t$, the parametric curve is just the graph of $f$ itself, and you recover the ordinary MVT. The Cauchy version is the MVT for curves, not just graphs.
Cauchy's MVT (Rudin §5.9)
Let $f,g:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ be continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$. Then $\exists\,c\in(a,b)$ such that: $$[f(b)-f(a)]\cdot g'(c)=[g(b)-g(a)]\cdot f'(c)$$ Or equivalently, if $g(b)\neq g(a)$ and $g'(c)\neq 0$: $\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}=\dfrac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}$

When $g(x)=x$: The right side becomes $\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}=f'(c)$ — the ordinary MVT. The parametric curve $t\mapsto(t,f(t))$ is just the graph of $f$.
How the Proof Auxiliary Function Is Discovered
Again the strategy is Rolle: find $h$ with $h(a)=h(b)$. Guided by the parametric picture: the secant line connecting $(g(a),f(a))$ to $(g(b),f(b))$ can be written as $[f(b)-f(a)](x-g(a))=[g(b)-g(a)](y-f(a))$. The signed deviation of the parametric point $(g(t),f(t))$ from this secant is proportional to: $$h(t)=[f(b)-f(a)]\cdot g(t)-[g(b)-g(a)]\cdot f(t)$$ Check: $h(a)=[f(b)-f(a)]g(a)-[g(b)-g(a)]f(a)$ and $h(b)=[f(b)-f(a)]g(b)-[g(b)-g(a)]f(b)$ — both equal $f(a)g(b)-f(b)g(a)$, the same value. So $h(a)=h(b)$ and Rolle gives a $c$ with $h'(c)=0$, which is exactly the Cauchy MVT.
Why Cauchy MVT Is Worth Knowing — L'Hôpital Follows From It
The main application of Cauchy's MVT in Chapter 5 is L'Hôpital's rule. Given $f(a)=g(a)=0$, apply Cauchy's MVT on $[a,x]$ for $x$ near $a$: there exists $c\in(a,x)$ with $\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{g(x)-g(a)}=\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}$, i.e., $\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}$. As $x\to a$, the point $c$ (squeezed between $a$ and $x$) also $\to a$, giving $\lim\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\lim\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}=L$.

Taylor's Theorem & L'Hôpital

Higher-order approximations, Lagrange remainder, and computing limits of indeterminate forms.

01 Taylor's Theorem

Main Idea
The MVT gives a linear approximation: $f(x)\approx f(\alpha)+f'(\alpha)(x-\alpha)$, with an error term controlled by $f''$. But what if you want a better approximation — one that uses second, third, or higher derivative information? You extend the polynomial to degree $n$, matching $f$ and all of its first $n$ derivatives at $\alpha$. That is the Taylor polynomial, and Taylor's theorem gives a sharp bound on how far the polynomial is from $f$.

Connecting back to MVT: Take $n=0$ in Taylor's theorem and you get $f(x)=f(\alpha)+f'(c)(x-\alpha)$ — exactly the MVT. The hierarchy is: MVT ($n=0$) → linear approximation → quadratic → cubic, each buying one more order of accuracy as $(x-\alpha)\to 0$.
Taylor's Theorem (Rudin §5.15)
Let $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ have $n$ continuous derivatives on $[a,b]$ and suppose $f^{(n+1)}$ exists on $(a,b)$. Then for any $x,\alpha\in[a,b]$: $$f(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{f^{(k)}(\alpha)}{k!}(x-\alpha)^k+\frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-\alpha)^{n+1}$$ for some $c$ between $\alpha$ and $x$.

The sum is the Taylor polynomial of degree $n$ — it matches $f$ and all derivatives up to order $n$ at $\alpha$. The remainder term $\frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-\alpha)^{n+1}$ is the Lagrange remainder — it bounds how far the polynomial is from $f$.
The MVT as Taylor with $n=0$
Take $n=0$: $f(x)=f(\alpha)+f'(c)(x-\alpha)$, i.e., $f(x)-f(\alpha)=f'(c)(x-\alpha)$. This is exactly the MVT. Taylor's theorem is the MVT iterated $n$ times.
Using Taylor to Estimate Errors
If $|f^{(n+1)}(t)|\leq M$ for all $t$ in the interval: $$\left|f(x)-\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{f^{(k)}(\alpha)}{k!}(x-\alpha)^k\right|\leq\frac{M}{(n+1)!}|x-\alpha|^{n+1}$$ Example: Approximate $e^{0.1}$ using the Taylor polynomial of $e^x$ at $0$ of degree 3: $1+0.1+0.01/2+0.001/6=1.10517$. Error $\leq e^{0.1}\cdot(0.1)^4/4!\approx 4.6\times 10^{-6}$.

02 L'Hôpital's Rule

Main Idea
L'Hôpital addresses $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)/g(x)$ when both $f(x)\to 0$ and $g(x)\to 0$ (or both $\to\pm\infty$). The trick: replace $f/g$ by $f'/g'$. The proof uses Cauchy's MVT, which is precisely why Cauchy's MVT appears just before this in Rudin — it's not an afterthought, it's the engine.
Why Does $f'/g'$ Replace $f/g$? — The Heuristic
When $f(a)=g(a)=0$, both functions vanish at $a$. Near $a$, a differentiable function behaves like its linear part: $f(x)\approx f'(a)(x-a)$ and $g(x)\approx g'(a)(x-a)$. So: $$\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\approx\frac{f'(a)(x-a)}{g'(a)(x-a)}=\frac{f'(a)}{g'(a)}$$ The factor $(x-a)$ cancels from both numerator and denominator — that is the entire intuition. L'Hôpital replaces the ratio of "sizes" with the ratio of "rates of growth." Cauchy's MVT makes this rigorous by producing a $c$ between $a$ and $x$ with $f(x)/g(x)=f'(c)/g'(c)$ exactly (not approximately), and then letting $x\to a$ forces $c\to a$.

The $\infty/\infty$ case is different — both functions are large, not small, so the linear-approximation heuristic breaks down. But the conclusion is the same, requiring a separate argument that Rudin handles by applying the $0/0$ version to $1/f$ and $1/g$.
L'Hôpital's Rule (Rudin §5.13)
Suppose $f,g:(a,b)\to\mathbb{R}$ are differentiable with $g'(x)\neq 0$ on $(a,b)$, and $f(x)\to 0$ and $g(x)\to 0$ as $x\to a^+$ (or both $\to\pm\infty$). If: $$\lim_{x\to a^+}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}=L$$ then $\lim_{x\to a^+}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=L$ as well.

Proof outline: Apply Cauchy's MVT on intervals $[x,t]$ near $a$: there exists $c\in(x,t)$ with $\frac{f(x)-f(t)}{g(x)-g(t)}=\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}$. As $t\to a$ and $f(t),g(t)\to 0$, the left side approaches $f(x)/g(x)$. As $x\to a$, $c\to a$ too, and $f'(c)/g'(c)\to L$.
Worked Example: $\lim_{x\to 0}\sin x/x$
$0/0$ form at $x=0$. Apply L'Hôpital: $\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{(\sin x)'}{(x)'}=\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\cos x}{1}=1$.
L'Hôpital Requires the Hypothesis
L'Hôpital only applies when you have an indeterminate form $0/0$ or $\infty/\infty$. Applying it to $\lim_{x\to 0}(1+x)/\sin x$ is wrong — the numerator goes to $1\neq 0$. Always check the form. Also: if $\lim f'/g'$ does not exist, you cannot conclude anything about $\lim f/g$.

03 Darboux's Theorem

Main Idea
If $f$ is differentiable everywhere, must $f'$ be continuous? The answer is no — but $f'$ cannot be too discontinuous either. Derivatives satisfy the intermediate value property even when they are not continuous.
Darboux's Theorem (Intermediate Value for Derivatives)
If $f$ is differentiable on $[a,b]$ and $f'(a)<\lambda<f'(b)$, then there exists $c\in(a,b)$ with $f'(c)=\lambda$.

Consequence: $f'$ cannot have jump discontinuities — a derivative can only have discontinuities of the oscillation type (second kind). This is a strong constraint on what functions can be derivatives.

01 Quick Drills — True / False

T/FIf $f'(p)>0$, then $f$ is increasing on some open interval around $p$.
False — the classic trap
$f(x)=x+2x^2\sin(1/x)$ for $x\neq 0$, $f(0)=0$ has $f'(0)=1>0$ yet oscillates near $0$ and is not monotone on any interval. $f'(p)>0$ means $f$ is locally increasing at $p$ in the infinitesimal sense, not on a neighborhood. You need $f'$ continuous and positive at $p$ for monotonicity on an interval.
T/FIf $f$ is differentiable at $p$, then $f$ is continuous at $p$.
True — Rudin Thm 5.2
$f(x)-f(p)=\frac{f(x)-f(p)}{x-p}\cdot(x-p)\to f'(p)\cdot 0=0$. So $f(x)\to f(p)$. Converse false: $|x|$ is continuous everywhere but not differentiable at $0$.
T/FThe MVT requires $f$ to be differentiable on the closed interval $[a,b]$, including at the endpoints.
False — only continuity at the endpoints
MVT (Rudin §5.10): $f$ continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$. Differentiability at $a$ and $b$ is NOT required. The proof applies EVT (needs only continuity) to the auxiliary function $h$, and Thm 5.8 (needs differentiability in the open interior). Example: $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$ on $[0,1]$ satisfies MVT even though $f'(0)=\infty$.
T/FIf $f'(x)=0$ for all $x\in(a,b)$, then $f$ is constant on $(a,b)$.
True — by MVT
For any $x,y\in(a,b)$ with $x<y$: MVT gives $f(y)-f(x)=f'(c)(y-x)=0$. So $f(y)=f(x)$ for all pairs. Corollary: if $f'=g'$ on $(a,b)$, then $f-g$ is constant.
T/FIf $f'(x)=g'(x)$ for all $x\in(a,b)$, then $f(x)=g(x)$ for all $x\in(a,b)$.
False — they can differ by a constant
Apply the corollary above to $h=f-g$: $h'\equiv 0$, so $h\equiv C$. If $C\neq 0$, $f\neq g$ even though $f'=g'$. Example: $f(x)=x^2+1$ and $g(x)=x^2$ have the same derivative everywhere.
T/FL'Hôpital's rule: whenever $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=0$, we have $\lim f/g=\lim f'/g'$.
False — two additional conditions required
(1) The limit $\lim f'/g'$ must exist. L'Hôpital gives no information if $f'/g'$ oscillates or diverges. (2) $g'(x)\neq 0$ near $a$. Without these the conclusion fails. The rule says: if you have $0/0$ or $\pm\infty/\infty$ AND $\lim f'/g'$ exists, THEN $\lim f/g$ equals it.

02 Proof Ordering

ORDERDrag to arrange: prove the MVT via Rolle's theorem.
⠿AApply Rolle's theorem to $h$: since $h(a)=h(b)=0$, $\exists\,c\in(a,b)$ with $h'(c)=0$.
⠿BDefine $h(x)=f(x)-f(a)-\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)$.
⠿CCompute $h'(x)=f'(x)-\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$.
⠿DCheck: $h(a)=0$ and $h(b)=f(b)-f(a)-(f(b)-f(a))=0$.
⠿ETherefore $f'(c)=\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$. $\blacksquare$
Correct order: B → D → C → A → E
B: Define the auxiliary function $h$ — subtract the secant line so endpoint values match.
D: Verify Rolle's equal-endpoint hypothesis by direct calculation.
C: Compute $h'$ so you know what "$h'(c)=0$" means in terms of $f'$.
A: Apply Rolle.
E: Translate: $h'(c)=0$ gives the MVT statement.
ORDERDrag to arrange: prove that differentiability at $p$ implies continuity at $p$.
⠿ATherefore $\lim_{x\to p}(f(x)-f(p))=f'(p)\cdot 0=0$, so $f(x)\to f(p)$.
⠿BWrite $f(x)-f(p)=\dfrac{f(x)-f(p)}{x-p}\cdot(x-p)$ for $x\neq p$.
⠿CAs $x\to p$: the first factor $\to f'(p)$ (definition of derivative) and $(x-p)\to 0$.
⠿DBy the product rule for limits, the product $\to f'(p)\cdot 0$.
Correct order: B → C → D → A
B: The key algebraic trick: multiply and divide by $(x-p)$ to extract the difference quotient.
C: Identify what each factor converges to independently.
D: Combine via the product rule for limits — valid since both limits exist.
A: Conclude: $f(x)-f(p)\to 0$ means $f(x)\to f(p)$.

03 Multiple Choice

MULTIYou want to show $|f(b)-f(a)|\leq M|b-a|$ given $|f'|\leq M$ on $(a,b)$. The first step is to write $f(b)-f(a)$ as:
B — MVT is the direct route
MVT gives $f(b)-f(a)=f'(c)(b-a)$ exactly, so $|f(b)-f(a)|=|f'(c)||b-a|\leq M|b-a|$. (D also gives the exact value via FTC, but bounding the integral is an extra step. MVT is cleaner for Lipschitz-type bounds.)
MULTIL'Hôpital's rule directly applies when the limit has which indeterminate form?
B — ratio forms only
For $0\cdot\infty$, first rewrite as a ratio: $f\cdot g=f/(1/g)$ or $g/(1/f)$. For $1^\infty$ or $\infty-\infty$, take logs or algebraically manipulate before applying L'Hôpital.
MULTIRolle's theorem requires which hypotheses on $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$?
B — exactly the three Rolle hypotheses
(1) Continuous on closed $[a,b]$ — needed for EVT to find the interior extremum. (2) Differentiable on open $(a,b)$ — needed for Thm 5.8 (interior extremum $\Rightarrow$ zero derivative). (3) $f(a)=f(b)$. A is too strong; D drops continuity at endpoints, which EVT requires.

ProblemSuppose $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfies $|f(x)-f(y)|\leq(x-y)^2$ for all $x,y\in\mathbb{R}$. Show $f$ is constant. PS7 Ex.4
Solution (two methods)
Method 1 (derivative): Fix $x\in\mathbb{R}$. For $h\neq 0$: $$\left|\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\right|\leq\frac{h^2}{|h|}=|h|\to 0\text{ as }h\to 0$$ So $f'(x)=0$ for all $x$. By the MVT, for any $x<y$ there exists $c\in(x,y)$ with $f(y)-f(x)=f'(c)(y-x)=0$. Hence $f$ is constant. $\blacksquare$

Method 2 (subdivision): Fix $x<y$. For any $n\in\mathbb{N}$, let $x_k=x+k(y-x)/n$. Then: $$|f(y)-f(x)|\leq\sum_{k=1}^n|f(x_k)-f(x_{k-1})|\leq\sum_{k=1}^n\left(\frac{y-x}{n}\right)^2=\frac{(y-x)^2}{n}\to 0$$ So $f(y)=f(x)$ for all $x,y$. $\blacksquare$
ProblemLet $f:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}$ be differentiable. Show $f$ is monotonically increasing if and only if $f'(x)\geq 0$ for all $x\in[0,1]$. PS7 Ex.7
Proof
$(\Rightarrow)$: Suppose $f$ is increasing. For any $x\in[0,1]$ and $h>0$ small enough: $$\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\geq 0$$ since $f(x+h)\geq f(x)$ (increasing) and $h>0$. Taking $h\to 0^+$ gives $f'(x)\geq 0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$: Suppose $f'(x)\geq 0$ for all $x$. Let $x<y$ in $[0,1]$. Apply the MVT to $f$ on $[x,y]$: there exists $c\in(x,y)$ such that $f(y)-f(x)=f'(c)(y-x)$. Since $f'(c)\geq 0$ and $y-x>0$, we get $f(y)\geq f(x)$. $\blacksquare$
ProblemProve the chain rule. Then derive $(x^q)'=qx^{q-1}$ for $q\in\mathbb{Q}$, $x>0$. PS7 Ex.8
Chain Rule Proof
Let $y_0=g(x)$. Define: $$\varphi(k)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{f(y_0+k)-f(y_0)}{k}-f'(y_0) & k\neq 0\\0 & k=0\end{cases}$$ By differentiability of $f$ at $y_0$, $\varphi(k)\to 0$ as $k\to 0$, so $\varphi$ is continuous at $0$. The identity $f(y_0+k)-f(y_0)=(f'(y_0)+\varphi(k))k$ holds for all $k$.

Set $k=g(x+h)-g(x)$. Then: $$\frac{f(g(x+h))-f(g(x))}{h}=(f'(g(x))+\varphi(k))\cdot\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}$$ As $h\to 0$: $k\to 0$ (since $g$ is continuous), so $\varphi(k)\to 0$; and $(g(x+h)-g(x))/h\to g'(x)$. Therefore $(f\circ g)'(x)=f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$. $\blacksquare$
$(x^q)'=qx^{q-1}$ for $q=m/n\in\mathbb{Q}$
Step 1: $(x^n)'=nx^{n-1}$ by induction and the product rule.

Step 2: $(x^{1/n})'=\frac{1}{n}x^{1/n-1}$. The function $r(x)=x^{1/n}$ is the inverse of $s(y)=y^n$. The inverse function theorem gives $r'(x)=1/s'(r(x))=1/(n(x^{1/n})^{n-1})=\frac{1}{n}x^{1/n-1}$.

Step 3: $h(x)=x^{m/n}=(x^{1/n})^m$. Chain rule: $$h'(x)=m(x^{1/n})^{m-1}\cdot\frac{1}{n}x^{1/n-1}=\frac{m}{n}x^{\frac{m-1}{n}+\frac{1-n}{n}}=\frac{m}{n}x^{m/n-1}\quad\blacksquare$$
R1Suppose $f'(x)>0$ on $(a,b)$. Prove $f$ is strictly increasing on $(a,b)$. Is the converse true? RUDIN
Proof and Counterexample
Strictly increasing: For $x<y$ in $(a,b)$, MVT gives $c\in(x,y)$ with $f(y)-f(x)=f'(c)(y-x)$. Since $f'(c)>0$ and $y-x>0$, we get $f(y)>f(x)$.

Converse is false: $f(x)=x^3$ on $\mathbb{R}$. Strictly increasing (if $x<y$ then $x^3<y^3$), but $f'(0)=0$. So $f'>0$ is sufficient but not necessary for strict increase. $\blacksquare$
R2Suppose $f$ is differentiable on $\mathbb{R}$, $f(0)=0$, and $|f'(x)|\leq|f(x)|$ for all $x$. Show $f\equiv 0$. RUDIN
Proof
Fix any $a>0$ and let $M=\sup_{x\in[0,a]}|f(x)|$. Since $f$ is continuous and $[0,a]$ is compact, $M$ is attained at some $x_0\in[0,a]$, so $M=|f(x_0)|$.

By MVT on $[0,x_0]$: $f(x_0)-f(0)=f'(c)\cdot x_0$ for some $c\in(0,x_0)$. Since $f(0)=0$: $$M=|f(x_0)|=|f'(c)|\cdot x_0\leq|f(c)|\cdot x_0\leq M\cdot a$$ So $M\leq Ma$. If $a<1$, then $M(1-a)\leq 0$, forcing $M=0$. Thus $f\equiv 0$ on $[0,a]$ for any $a<1$. By induction (shift the interval), $f\equiv 0$ on $[0,\infty)$. Similarly on $(-\infty,0]$. $\blacksquare$
R3Let $f$ be twice differentiable on $(a,b)$ with $f''(x)\geq 0$ (convex). Show that for any $x,y\in(a,b)$ and $t\in[0,1]$: $f(tx+(1-t)y)\leq tf(x)+(1-t)f(y)$. RUDIN
Proof
Let $z=tx+(1-t)y$. By Taylor at $z$ with $n=1$ (Lagrange remainder): $$f(x)=f(z)+f'(z)(x-z)+\frac{f''(c_1)}{2}(x-z)^2\geq f(z)+f'(z)(x-z)$$ $$f(y)=f(z)+f'(z)(y-z)+\frac{f''(c_2)}{2}(y-z)^2\geq f(z)+f'(z)(y-z)$$ since $f''\geq 0$ makes the remainder terms non-negative. Multiply the first by $t$ and the second by $(1-t)$ and add: $$tf(x)+(1-t)f(y)\geq f(z)+f'(z)[t(x-z)+(1-t)(y-z)]$$ But $t(x-z)+(1-t)(y-z)=tx+(1-t)y-z=z-z=0$. So $tf(x)+(1-t)f(y)\geq f(z)=f(tx+(1-t)y)$. $\blacksquare$
